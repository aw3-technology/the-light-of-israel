# Chapter 31: Ordinary Holiness

The stroller wheel catches on a cobblestone and Ethan jerks it free without thinking. Ari walks beside him, fist locked around Ethan's index finger, legs churning in that determined toddler stride that covers almost no ground. Lev sits in the stroller with his hands folded in his lap like a passenger on a long train journey, watching the city pass.

Wednesday morning. Nothing scheduled except the hours.

Machane Yehuda opens around them in stages. The produce vendors are already shouting, their voices carrying over the corrugated awnings in overlapping Hebrew that Ethan catches in fragments. A truck backs down the narrow lane, beeping, and Ari freezes to watch it, mouth open. The air smells of cardamom and diesel and stone fruit going soft in crates.

"Yalla, Ari." Ethan tugs gently. Ari doesn't budge. The truck is still interesting.

Lev reaches over the stroller's edge and touches a stack of dried apricots on a low shelf, then pulls his hand back and studies his fingers as if they've changed. The world is a series of textures to Lev, each one requiring careful examination before the next.

The spice vendor, Shimon, sees them from across the aisle and raises both hands. "Ha'Amerikaiim!" He's been calling them this for a year. Ethan has given up correcting him.

"Kurkum?" Shimon asks, already scooping turmeric into a bag. He doesn't wait for answers. He knows what Sera uses. "V'gam kamon, nachon?" Cumin too, right?

"Ken. V'paprika." Ethan's accent makes Shimon wince every time, a theatrical grimace followed by a grin.

"Paprika," Shimon repeats, correcting. "Pa-pri-ka. You live here how long?"

"Shnatayim." Two years.

Shimon shakes his head, scoops the paprika. Ari has found the dried chili peppers and is reaching for a fistful. Ethan catches his wrist. "Lo, motek." No, sweetheart. Ari's face compresses into the expression that precedes a scream, but Ethan redirects him toward the halvah samples and the crisis passes.

Lev watches from the stroller with an expression Ethan can only describe as philosophical.

Ethan picks through tomatoes, pressing each one with his thumb. Sera has taught him what to look for. Firm but not hard. No soft spots near the stem. He drops six into a bag. Ari reaches into the crate, grabs one with both hands, squeezes. The tomato bursts, seeds and juice running between his fingers. Ari stares at his hands with stunned delight.

"Ari. No."

But the vendor laughs, waves it off. "Stam agvaniya." Just a tomato. She hands Ethan a napkin. He wipes Ari's hands while Ari twists and protests, the napkin apparently worse than the tomato guts. Lev is watching a display of whole fish on ice at the neighboring stall. His eyes are wide and still. The fish stare back with the same expression.

"Dag," Ethan says, pointing. Fish.

"Da," Lev repeats. Close enough.

They buy cucumbers, dill, feta wrapped in paper. Ethan counts out shekels, still converting to dollars in his head. A habit he cannot break. The stroller's basket is filling up. Ari's shirt is streaked with tomato and halvah and clementine juice. They have been here forty minutes.

At the end of the aisle, an old man sits on a plastic chair playing an oud. The strings bright and winding in the market noise. Nobody stops. Nobody needs to. The music is part of the infrastructure, like the awnings and the stone and the arguments about price. Ari listens for three full seconds, which is an eternity in toddler time, then moves on. Lev turns his head and keeps looking even after the stroller has rounded the corner.

---

The barbershop is on a residential street in East Talpiot. The barber's name is Moshe. He is Moroccan, in his seventies, and has never once been silent in Ethan's presence. The shop smells of sandalwood aftershave and the metallic bite of old scissors. A radio plays Mizrachi music from a shelf above the mirror.

Ethan lifts Ari onto the booster seat. Ari grabs the armrests and looks around with the expression of a man surveying his kingdom. Lev sits on Ethan's lap in the waiting chair, thumb in his mouth, watching.

"Ah, ha'gevurot!" Moshe says. The heroes. He tucks a cape around Ari's neck and Ari immediately tries to pull it off. "Lo, lo, lo. Chamesh dakot." Five minutes. "Tishev yafe." Sit nicely.

Moshe talks while he cuts. This is not optional. The scissors move in rhythm with his voice. His granddaughter studies engineering at the Technion. His grandson just started in the navy. His wife is visiting her sister in Ashdod and left him with nothing to eat, though Ethan can see a full container of couscous behind the register.

"You know what my father told me?" Moshe says, angling Ari's head gently to the left. "'Moshe, a man who cuts hair touches every life in the neighborhood.' Fifty-two years I've been cutting. Three generations from the same families. Bar mitzvah boys who bring me their sons now." He blows a stray hair from Ari's ear. "Your boys. They'll sit in this chair when they're thirteen. I'll still be here. God willing."

Lev watches the scissors with unblinking focus. The sound of the blades opening and closing. The small dark curls falling to the tile floor. He reaches out from Ethan's lap and catches a falling curl in his fist and holds it.

Moshe finishes Ari, brushes the loose hair from his neck, lifts him down. "Yofi. Melech." A king.

"V'ha'sheni?" And the second?

"Just a trim. He doesn't like strangers touching his head."

But Lev surprises them. He climbs into the chair himself, chin up. Something about watching Ari survive the ordeal has given him permission. Moshe works slower, reading the child's tolerance. Lev sits perfectly still, eyes on his own reflection in the mirror.

"This one," Moshe says quietly, almost to himself. "This one pays attention."

He charges Ethan forty shekels for both. Ethan leaves sixty.

---

On the roof, the wind pulls at the wet shirts and sends them billowing like small white sails. Ethan clips each piece to the line with plastic pegs, the mechanical repetition emptying his mind. Sera's blouses. His work shirts. The twins' tiny onesies that seem impossibly small.

The twins wander among the hanging sheets, appearing and disappearing. Ari pulls one down and Ethan rehangs it without frustration. This is the third time. It will not be the last.

The roof faces west toward the Old City. The Dome of the Rock catches the early afternoon light, gold against pale stone. Beyond it, the Mount of Olives. The topography of three thousand years compressed into a single view Ethan sees every time he hangs laundry. It has not yet become ordinary.

"Shalom," says a voice behind him. Dina, the neighbor from the fourth floor, also carrying a basket.

"Shalom, Dina."

"Ha'yeladim gadlu." The kids have grown. She says this every week. It is always true.

They hang laundry in parallel, not speaking, the wind filling the silence. The sun is warm on the back of Ethan's neck. His hands are wet. A clothespin slips and he catches it against his thigh. Just this. Lift, stretch, pin. The fabric smells of the lavender detergent Sera buys. He knows the smell the way he knows the apartment, the way his hands know the stroller's latch.

Ari sits on the warm concrete and examines a pebble. Lev stands at the low wall, fingers curled over the edge, looking out at the city. Ethan watches him watching. Just his son standing in the light.

He pins the last onesie to the line and carries the empty basket downstairs.

---

The apartment is quiet. Both boys asleep, the door cracked open, the monitor on the kitchen counter cycling through green. Ethan sits at the table with hummus and bread and sliced cucumber. He eats slowly. The afternoon light comes through the kitchen window at a low angle, catching the dust, warming the counter where Sera's camera bag sits from yesterday's shoot.

On the shelf above the sink, beside the spice jars, the siddur. Ethan reaches for it. Not out of obligation or spiritual hunger. Just because the apartment is quiet and his hands want something to hold.

He opens it to no particular page. Lands somewhere in the afternoon psalms. Reads a few lines silently, his Hebrew slow but no longer effortful. The words enter him the way the market noise enters him, the way the wind on the roof enters him. Just present.

The cat jumps onto the table and puts its nose against the hummus plate. Ethan pushes it away gently. The cat resettles on the chair opposite, orange and thin and tolerant of this family that took it in.

His phone is on the counter but he doesn't reach for it. The sertraline is in the cabinet above the sink, this morning's dose already taken, doing its quiet chemical work beneath everything else. Dr. Yael would ask him how the day was. He would say fine, ordinary, nothing happened. She would nod because she understood nothing happening was sometimes the whole point.

The front door opens. Sera, earlier than expected, hair pulled back, face drawn from the shoot. She sets the camera bag down carefully and leans against the counter.

"How were they?"

"Good. Ari destroyed a tomato at the shuk. Lev made a friend at the fish counter."

She almost smiles. "I got a shot today. This woman in Sderot, showing me where her daughter's bedroom used to be. The wall is gone but the bookshelf is still standing. All the books still on it. She started organizing them while I was shooting. Putting them in order. In a room with no wall."

She presses her hands against the small of her back. The pregnancy is showing now, just barely, a new curve beneath her shirt she touches sometimes without knowing.

"Tired. The drive back was long." She takes a cucumber from his plate, bites it. "It's quiet."

"They're asleep."

She sits across from him. The cat moves to her lap immediately. For a moment they just sit in the kitchen, the afternoon light pooling on the table between them, the monitor cycling through green.

Sera reaches across and takes his hand. Her fingers are cold from the car. He holds them until they warm.

From the monitor, a small sound. Ari stirring, the crib creaking. Then quiet again. Then Lev's voice -- not crying but talking, the private babble he practices when he thinks no one is listening. Hebrew and nonsense braided together into something that sounds almost like prayer.

"Five more minutes," Sera says, eyes closed.

"Five more minutes," Ethan agrees.

The light moves across the table. The cat purrs. The city hums. In a few months their daughter will arrive and the noise will triple and the walks to the shuk will require a bigger stroller or a different strategy entirely. But that is later. Right now the kitchen smells of hummus and lavender detergent and the particular warmth of Jerusalem stone releasing the day's heat back into the air.

Sera opens her eyes. "I think he needs you."

Ari is standing in his crib, hair freshly cut, face still creased from sleep. He reaches both arms up. Ethan lifts him. Lev is sitting in his crib, awake, watching, hands folded, waiting for his turn.

Ethan carries one son on each hip and walks back to the kitchen where Sera is already pulling vegetables from the bag he filled that morning. She hands Ari a cucumber and he gnaws on it. Lev gets a piece of pita, which he holds but does not eat, studying it.

The noise returns. Ari banging the cucumber on the table. Lev making his quiet sounds. Sera running water in the sink. The knife on the cutting board. The radio she turns on for the evening news. The cat weaving between ankles hoping for scraps.

Their noise. Their kitchen. Their Wednesday.

Ethan sets the boys in their high chairs and starts slicing tomatoes for dinner, the knife steady, the fruit splitting clean and red under the blade.
