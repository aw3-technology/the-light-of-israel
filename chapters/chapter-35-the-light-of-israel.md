# Chapter 35: The Light of Israel

The muezzin's call threaded through the dark. The birds answered. The first gray light touched the limestone sill where Ethan Israel Stone's hand rested, palm down, feeling the cold seep out of the stone as Jerusalem warmed. He had been watching for this — the moment the city traded black for blue. Three years, and it still caught him.

The sertraline sat on the nightstand next to his phone. Seventy-five milligrams now, adjusted twice since the move. His psychiatrist, Dr. Yael, had warned him: "Aliyah is not an antidepressant. You moved your geography, not your neurochemistry." She was right. The gray mornings still came. Some weeks he woke feeling the old nothing, the ceiling-staring blankness no amount of Jerusalem stone could fill. Those weeks he called Dr. Yael, took his pills, went to the Wall anyway. The medication didn't make him spiritual. The spirituality didn't replace the medication. Both held him up.

Sera stirred beside him, her hand finding his chest. The twins slept in the next room, three years old now. Born during Passover 2024, when the sirens had been quiet for two weeks. He'd told the story that way -- freedom and birth, exodus and arrival -- until Sera pointed out the sirens had actually resumed thirty-six hours after the boys came home. The quiet had been a coincidence of targeting, not a blessing. He kept telling it anyway.

The Old City at dawn: empty except for soldiers and insomniacs. Same route for three years. Muscle memory now, like his seventeen-minute showers had been in Brooklyn.

The Wall. Some mornings it felt like comfort. Today it felt like a gravestone. The security checkpoint was routine now. Three years of muscle memory.

The stone was warm. Always warm. He didn't question it anymore.

He pulled out the paper. He'd written the note at the kitchen table at 4 AM, his Hebrew still crooked, the sentences simple because his grammar couldn't hold anything complex.

*Hineni. Ani kan. Ani lo yodea im zeh nakhon.*

Here I am. I am here. I don't know if this is right.

He folded the paper, found a crack between stones, pushed it deep where it would join the millions of other prayers — pleas for healing, for children, for peace, for parking spaces, his grandmother had told him. The Wall didn't judge. The sunrise turned Jerusalem gold, and the note was already invisible.

He wrapped tefillin. The leather straps left marks on his arm that lasted until noon. Sera said they looked like bondage marks. "They are," he'd told her. She hadn't laughed.

The prayers began. Shacharit, morning service. He found himself lingering on the Modim, the gratitude blessing, his lips moving slower over the words. He skipped the petition for prosperity but stayed longer with the Shehecheyanu, the blessing for reaching this moment alive.

"Shema Yisrael..."

Hear O Israel. His name called in the essential prayer.

His Hebrew was functional now. Accented. Mistakes in every sentence. But functional. The prayers came automatically, the way any repeated act becomes reflex.

---

Their apartment overlooked the Old City. Purchased with his Brooklyn money when the shekel crashed. The view had seemed worth everything until the missile alert system was installed on their balcony. Now they saw the city through metal slats.

Ari had removed his diaper and was trying to put it on the cat. The cat -- a street tabby they'd adopted against Sera's better judgment -- tolerated this with the resigned patience of all Jerusalem creatures. Lev sat in the corner peeling the label off a jar of tahini, his face and arms smeared with the stuff.

"Ari, no. The cat doesn't wear — Ari. *Ari.*"

Ari looked at his father with the serene defiance of a three-year-old who has made his decision. "Chatula needs," he said in Hebrew, pressing the diaper against the cat's haunches.

"Lev, stop eating the — that's not food, that's the *label* — "

Lev had moved on from peeling to tasting. When Ethan pulled the jar away, Lev's face crumpled into the operatic outrage of a toddler whose important work has been interrupted. He threw himself flat on the tile floor, kicked twice, then lay still, staring at the ceiling with his father's own thousand-yard stare.

*God, he looks like me when I'm depressed,* Ethan thought, and laughed despite himself.

"What's funny?" Sera called from the kitchen, where Ladino melodies her grandmother had taught her competed with the hiss of the stovetop espresso maker.

"Our son is lying on the floor having an existential crisis about tahini."

"Which son?"

"The one who isn't dressing the cat."

"Ah." She appeared in the doorway, seven months along now, a wooden spoon in one hand. She assessed the scene — naked Ari, tahini-covered Lev, the patient cat — and turned back to the kitchen without comment. Some battles weren't worth fighting.

Lev recovered as quickly as he'd collapsed, sat up, and began building a tower with blocks. Seven high, leaning left. Ari abandoned the cat project, came over, and raised his hand to topple it.

"Let it stand," Ethan said.

"It's going to fall anyway."

"Let it stand while it can."

The tower fell. Lev rebuilt without complaint, already reaching for blocks. Ari lost interest and went to find the cat again.

A knock at the door. Umm Khalil from two floors down, holding a plate covered with a cloth. She did this most Fridays -- knafeh or basbousa, something sweet, left without ceremony. Her husband drove a taxi. Her eldest son was studying pharmacy in Amman. She and Sera had an arrangement that predated Ethan: Sera watched her youngest when she had a doctor's appointment. Umm Khalil brought sweets and, once, during a power outage, a kerosene lamp. They spoke in a pidgin of Hebrew and Arabic and hand gestures. Ethan had tried, once, to talk to her husband about politics -- the checkpoints, the permits. The man had looked at him with patient exhaustion. "You are a good neighbor," he'd said in accented Hebrew. "Be a good neighbor."

"Knafeh," Sera said, lifting the cloth. "Still warm."

Ethan sat at the kitchen table with his coffee — Ethiopian beans, rich and dark. The table was solid wood, the first piece of furniture they'd chosen together for this life.

"Abba, teach me letters," Ari said, climbing onto his lap.

"Which letter?"

"The first one."

"Aleph," Ethan said, and began to draw it on the paper. His son's hand over his, learning the shape. "It makes no sound by itself."

"Why?"

"Because it's the... the breath before the word. The silence that — " He paused, searching. "That holds all sounds. I think."

Ari traced the letter, tongue out in concentration.

Through the window, Jerusalem. Stone and light and shadows.

His grandmother's candlesticks sat on the shelf, polished now, used every Friday. Beside them, new ones. Smaller. For the twins when they were older. If there was an older.

There would be. Had to be.

Ari traced another aleph, then another, filling the page with silent letters.

---

The siren cut through their lesson like a blade through prayer.

Ari's crayon stopped mid-stroke. Lev dropped his block. Three years old and they knew that sound meant running.

"Mamek!" Sera called from the kitchen, her voice sharp but controlled.

Ethan lifted both boys. "Safe room. Now."

They ran. Again. As they had dozens of times. As they might dozens more.

The safe room: reinforced concrete, water bottles, children's books, a flashlight with dying batteries. Their life reduced to essentials. They had taped glow-in-the-dark stars to the ceiling for the boys, a kindness that looked absurd in the fluorescent light but at night, in the dark, gave Ari something to count besides explosions.

Through the wall, Umm Khalil's children were crying. Her apartment didn't have a safe room — not all units in the building did, and not all tenants had the standing to demand one. During the last siren, Sera had banged on her door, pulled her and the three children into theirs. They'd sat together on the floor — four adults, five children, two languages, one concrete box. Umm Khalil had held Lev on her lap while Sera held her youngest, and nobody had thought about who belonged where. The siren didn't ask for papers. This time, though, Ethan heard the footsteps above — Umm Khalil running her children to the stairwell, the building's only shelter for those without a room. He thought about going, bringing them in. Then the explosion hit, and thinking became a luxury.

The explosion came from the north. Close enough to rattle windows. Far enough to mean someone else's tragedy.

Lev buried his face in Ethan's shoulder. Ari counted on his fingers — a game he'd invented. How many sirens this month. Seven. He held up the fingers to show Sera, who nodded with the grave attention she gave all their discoveries.

"Is this living?" Sera asked quietly, her hand on her belly.

The question hit different now. Not philosophical. Immediate. Real. Behind it, all the offers that came and went — the easier lives, the safer cities, the friends who said *come back* and meant it kindly. The question every immigrant to this place carried in their bones: *Is this worth what it costs?* He thought of Aaron in New York, of the life still available if he wanted it. Backyards and quiet mornings and the safety he had traded for this concrete room.

But the boys were already calming. Ari had found a picture book and was turning pages. Lev's breathing steadied against Ethan's chest. Sera hummed something — one of her grandmother's melodies, low and sure. The room that was supposed to feel like a cage felt, in this moment, like the center of their lives. Not its interruption.

When the all-clear sounded, they emerged blinking. In the stairwell, Umm Khalil was already leading her children back upstairs, the youngest on her hip, the older two walking ahead with the determined calm of children who had done this before. She caught Sera's eye and nodded — a look between mothers that contained everything and required no translation. Ethan wanted to say something — sorry, come in next time, the room is big enough — but Umm Khalil was already gone, her door closing quietly, and the moment passed into the accumulation of moments that made up life in this building, this city, this arrangement that no one had chosen and everyone continued to inhabit.

The lesson forgotten. The morning shattered. Or not shattered — cracked, the way everything here was cracked, and somehow still holding.

Ethan pulled out his notebook. Three years of trying to make sense of this choice, these changes, this life that felt both inevitable and impossible.

He wrote:

"October 7, 2026. Three years. The twins are three. I think I'm learning what it means to stay. Not certainty — I still wake up some mornings wondering if I'm fooling myself. Whether the prayers are faith or performance. Whether the sertraline is doing the real work and the Judaism is just the story I tell myself about why I take it. Dr. Yael says these questions are healthy. She also says my cortisol levels don't care about Kabbalistic frameworks — the body keeps its own accounts, and three years of sirens and checkpoints and the low hum of living in a place that wants to kill you have left chemical signatures no amount of Torah study will erase. Some mornings the depression returns not as emptiness but as a specific, physical weight behind my sternum that I recognize from Brooklyn, from the years before any of this. The same weight. Different city. The spiritual life didn't cure it. I don't think anything cures it. I manage it. I light candles and take pills and see my therapist and some weeks I do all three and still lie in bed staring at the ceiling while Sera sleeps, wondering if the whole thing — the aliyah, the teaching, the faith — is just an elaborate structure I built to house a depression that would have followed me anywhere.

"What I've lost: Saturday mornings reading alone. Friendships that couldn't survive the distance. The career I was good at. The freedom of not believing in anything, which I didn't appreciate until I started believing in something and discovered that belief is a kind of weight. I traded all of that for this: particular, demanding, dangerous, mine.

"The light that flickers but doesn't go out. I am Ethan Israel Stone, still wrestling, still learning what that means. Still here. Still not sure if 'here' is home or habit."

Through the window, Jerusalem caught the morning light. The flat, honest light of a Middle Eastern morning that illuminated everything equally: the ancient walls and the modern scars, the beauty and the barbed wire.

Sera read what he'd written, standing behind him. Her hand on his shoulder.

"You left something out," she said.

"What?"

"The part where you stay."

He looked at her. At the safe room door still ajar. At his sons, already recovered, Ari reaching for his crayons, Lev rebuilding his tower.

He picked up the notebook and added one line. Then set it down, and turned to Ari, who was staring at his abandoned aleph, half-finished on the paper.

"Let's finish the letter," Ethan said.

Ari's hand found his father's. Together, they completed the stroke.

"What comes after aleph?"

"Bet," Ethan said. "It means house."

Through the window, light caught the limestone — the flat, honest light of a Jerusalem morning that illuminated everything equally: the beauty and the barbed wire, the prayer shawls and the Iron Dome batteries, the muezzin's minaret and the crane building apartments for people like him. From here he couldn't tell if the glow on the northern horizon was dawn still unfolding or the trail of something intercepted.
