# Chapter 6: Candles Under the Arch

The candles appeared first. One, then dozens, then hundreds, flickering against October darkness.

Sunday evening, October 8th. The subway ride from Brooklyn carried the weight of crossing into foreign territory that was somehow also a homecoming. At 14th Street, he could already see them through the train window. The mass of bodies gathering with their pinpricks of light.

He'd seen the announcement on Instagram while lying in bed at 4 PM, still in yesterday's clothes. The screen's blue light the only illumination, curtains drawn against a Sunday that felt too raw for sunshine. "Vigil for the Victims--Washington Square Park, 7 PM, Bring Light Against Darkness." The graphics simple, stark. A candle, a Star of David, the date October 7th as a set of digits he couldn't stop looking at.


But his thumb had screenshotted it anyway. His body had risen, showered, dressed. The black shirt was unintentional but appropriate. On the platform, the overhead fluorescents hummed at a frequency that made his teeth ache. Or maybe that was just the tension he'd been carrying since yesterday.

The train carried others toward the same destination. An elderly couple whispered in Hebrew, the woman's knuckles white where she gripped the pole. Three college girls in Columbia sweatshirts passed a phone between them, mascara streaking their cheeks. A father steadied two children as the train lurched. The boy, maybe eight, clutched a Yankees *kippah* that kept sliding off his head. The girl wore a Star of David necklace tangled in her hair.

"Are we going to pray, Abba?" the boy asked.

"We're going to remember," the father answered, and the crack in his voice made Ethan look away, out the window into tunnel darkness.

At Union Square, the demographic shifted. A woman wrapped an Israeli flag around her shoulders, the fabric worn soft at the edges. Two Hasidic men argued in rapid Yiddish. One kept saying "But the Rebbe said," while the other countered with Pittsburgh, with Poway, with how nowhere was safe anymore.

Ethan remained standing though seats emptied around him. The metal rail chilled his palm. His phone screen was dark. He'd turned it off somewhere between Brooklyn and here.

The walk from the station took forever and no time at all. The October wind carried the smell of burning leaves from somewhere, though there were no trees burning in Manhattan, just candles—hundreds of them already visible as he turned onto Washington Square Park South.

The crowd was already thick, bodies pressed close enough that Ethan could smell someone's lavender perfume mixing with candle smoke.
He checked his phone as he stepped under the arch: 7:17 PM.

A hand materialized holding a *yahrzeit* candle. Thick glass, the kind you buy at Key Food for $2.99. The woman had deep-set eyes behind wire-rimmed glasses, a scar bisecting her left eyebrow. She didn't speak. Just touched her flame to his wick. The fire jumped between them.

"Wait," Ethan heard himself say. "Thank you."

She paused, studied him. "First yahrzeit candle?"

"First everything."

"Then you'll need another." She pressed a second candle into his free hand. "For whoever you haven't mourned yet."

She vanished into the crowd before he could ask what she meant. He stood holding both candles, one in each hand, their heat warming his palms. The glass was thick and heavy, the kind his grandmother had kept in a kitchen cabinet and lit every year on the anniversary of her mother's death. He'd seen them burning as a child and asked what they were for. "For remembering," she'd said. "For when the mouth can't say it, the fire says it instead."

Someone near the fountain had started "*Hatikvah*"—the national anthem, *The Hope*—but a younger voice interrupted with "Od Yavo Shalom Aleinu," a peace song. For a moment the two melodies tangled, competed, before settling into an uneasy harmony.

"*Acheinu kol beit Yisrael*"—*our brothers, all the house of Israel*. The prayer for captives. Someone led it, voice cracking on the high notes.

Ethan found himself not just humming but mouthing the words—words he didn't know he knew. His hand moved to steady the second candle, and without thinking, he lit it from the first.

The speeches began late. Someone had brought a portable PA system that kept cutting out. The mic cord was patched with silver duct tape that caught the candlelight.

A man in a Knicks jersey took the microphone, the sports logo jarring against his grief: "My sister is still missing. She's twenty-four. She was at the music festival." His voice broke. "She texted me a video of the sunrise. Then nothing."

A woman beside Ethan whispered to her friend: "My cousin's roommate in Jerusalem—Noa—she was at Nova too. Twenty-four, yoga teacher. Her friend Sera's been calling hospitals for two days straight." Her voice cracked. "How many Noas are there tonight?"

The crowd's silence had texture. Sniffles. The rustle of tissues pulled from pockets. Someone's phone playing a CNN alert they couldn't silence fast enough. Near Ethan, a woman whispered "*Baruch Dayan HaEmet*" while the man beside her muttered God had nothing to do with this--didn't she remember the Holocaust?

More speakers. Each story a world destroyed. Parents, children, friends, dancers, soldiers, farmers, peaceniks. Just humans who'd been alive yesterday and weren't today. Ethan listened with his whole body. He'd come here as an observer—a secular Jew who felt something but didn't know what to do with it, who'd gotten off the couch and onto the train for reasons he couldn't articulate. But standing here with wax cooling on his fingers and someone else's grief in his ears, the distinction between observer and participant was dissolving. The candle in his hand was an argument. The act of standing here was a position. He'd already chosen something. He just didn't have a name for it yet.

The rabbi wore jeans under his *tallit*, Adidas sneakers scuffed to gray. His beard grew in patches, trimmed unevenly.

"We are here because—" The mic squealed. He adjusted it, tried again. "We are here because we don't know where else to be. Some of you believe in God. Some don't. Some did yesterday but don't today. That's okay. Tonight we're not here for God. We're here for each other."

At the edge of the crowd, a scuffle. Someone shouting about occupation, someone else shouting back. A djembe started up, rhythms that didn't match the moment. Through it all, the *Kaddish* continued. Voices growing hoarse, wavering, but not stopping.

The arguments at the edge bothered him less than he expected. Or rather—they bothered him in a new way. He wasn't angry at the protesters. He wasn't angry at the counter-protesters. He was angry at himself, for standing in the middle of something and not knowing what to say. All around him people were speaking—declaring, accusing, mourning, praying—and he had no words. Just two candles and a throat full of something that wouldn't resolve into language.

Did he want to speak? He imagined stepping forward, saying his name, saying *my grandmother survived Auschwitz and I never asked her about it until it was too late.* But that was his grief, not theirs. And grief wasn't argument. It wasn't a position. It was just a fact, standing in the cold.

Ethan found himself moving to the *Kaddish* circle. He raised his second candle higher, adding his light to theirs. "*Yitgadal v'yitkadash*"—*magnified and sanctified*—he whispered, the words surfacing from a depth he'd forgotten he carried.

The crowd dissolved into streams—some toward the subway, some toward bars, some staying, unwilling to leave the light. Ethan held his two candles, wax dripping onto his sleeves, hardening in pale streaks on his black shirt. One for the dead. One for his grandmother. He hadn't planned the second candle. The woman with the scar had pressed it into his hand and he'd lit it without thinking, and now he was standing in a park at night holding a flame for a woman who'd died fifteen years ago, and it felt like the first honest thing he'd done in months.


The park smelled like extinguished candles. Smoke and melted wax and the chemical sweetness of a hundred cheap *yahrzeit* glasses cooling on stone.

The park was emptying. Someone was sweeping up programs, broom scraping against concrete. A woman collected forgotten candles into a cardboard box, handling each one carefully, as though the grief inside them might spill. Near the fountain, a man sat alone on a bench, staring at his phone, his candle still burning on the pavement beside him. He wasn't going anywhere yet.

The walk home was long and strange. The city had returned to normal—or what passed for normal on an October Sunday night. Restaurants had customers. Bars had lines. A group of NYU students spilled out of a dorm lobby arguing about something unrelated, their laughter sharp and foreign. The world had resumed. It hadn't waited.

Wax on his sleeve, ash on his collar. The bridge wind carried salt and diesel and the mineral breath of the harbor. Below, the East River held the city's lights in its dark surface, distorted, moving. He walked the bridge slowly, his candles cupped in both hands against the wind, the flames guttering but refusing to die. Other pedestrians gave him a wide berth—a man carrying fire across a bridge. He must have looked biblical, or unhinged, or both.

At his building, he hesitated with the key in the lock. The stairwell behind the door was dark and ordinary. It smelled like cleaning solution and the neighbor's cat. Everything in there—his apartment, his laptop, his unmade bed—belonged to the person he'd been yesterday. He wasn't sure that person existed anymore. Or maybe this was dramatic. Maybe he'd walk in and feel nothing, and tomorrow the vigil would be a memory that didn't change anything.

He opened the door.

His apartment was exactly as he'd left it. Mail scattered on the counter, yesterday's coffee mug in the sink. The notification light on his laptop blinking patiently. Everything waiting for him to resume the life he'd paused six hours ago. But the apartment felt different with the candles in his hands. The ordinary objects—the mail, the mug, the laptop—looked temporary. Provisional. As though they'd been standing in for something more permanent that hadn't arrived yet.

He set his two *yahrzeit* candles on the kitchen counter. One still burning for October 7th's dead. One for his grandmother.

Two flames in the kitchen window, reflected back at him through black glass. He stood watching them for a long time. The reflections looked farther away than they should have, as though the flames were burning in some other apartment, some other kitchen, where a different version of him knew what to do next.
