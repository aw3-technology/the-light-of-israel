# Chapter 27: Build and Be Built

July in Jerusalem, the heat different from Brooklyn summer. Dry, bone-deep, pressing down with the weight of blessing or burden depending on the day. He slept differently now — deep and sudden, the construction work emptying him so completely he'd fall unconscious at 9 PM and wake at 4:30 without an alarm. In Brooklyn, he'd been an insomniac, lying awake parsing his own thoughts until 2 AM. Here, sleep came like a trapdoor opening.

Seven months since his return from exile. Seven months of Sera's apartment becoming theirs, of navigating the byzantine process of transforming tourist status into temporary residency, then into the long aliyah process that would make him Israeli.

---

The Ulpan classroom at eight in the morning already stifling, windows open to catch breeze that never came. Twenty-three new immigrants bent over Hebrew worksheets, conjugating verbs that refused to follow patterns.

"Ani boneh," Ethan repeated, the verb for building. "I build. Atah boneh. You build. Hu boneh. He builds."

Beside him, Natasha from Moscow corrected his pronunciation. She'd been a cybersecurity specialist back home. Now she stocked shelves at Rami Levy while her credentials underwent "evaluation." "Boneh. From the throat. Like you're coughing up dust from old buildings."

The teacher, Orna, wrote on the board: "אני בונה את עצמי." I build myself.

"This is reflective verb," she said in Hebrew slow enough for them to catch. "Not just constructing. Shaping the self. You understand?"

They understood. Everyone in the room had dismantled themselves to be here. Former doctors and lawyers and tech workers, all apprentices again.

"Ethan," Orna called. "Tell us in Hebrew what you're building."

He rose, the words forming slowly, each one heavy as lifted stone. "Ani boneh... bayit. Ani boneh... mishpacha. Ani boneh..." He stopped, searched for the word. "Life. How do you say life?"

"Chayim," the class responded in unison. They all knew that one. L'chayim. To life. The toast that survived even the most thorough assimilation.

"Ani boneh chayim chadashim." I build new life.

---

At break, Sera came to pick him up. She'd been different lately, carrying some secret knowledge.

"How was class?" she asked in English, merciful.

"My brain hurts. Everything backward. Verbs don't make sense."

"You're thinking in English still. Stop translating. Let Hebrew think for you."

Her hand drifted to her stomach, an unconscious gesture he'd noticed more often lately. Something shifted in Ethan's chest. Recognition before knowledge. Body understanding before mind.

"Sera?"

"I was waiting. To be sure. To find the right words." She switched to Hebrew, slow, careful, making sure he understood. "Ani beherayon." I am pregnant.

The courtyard sounds faded. All of it became distant against the sound of future arriving in Hebrew words his brain translated slow as archaeology.

"When?" Joy and terror in equal measure. He wasn't ready. They couldn't afford this. None of that mattered.

"Hanukkah. That first week you were back." Her smile was tired but radiant. "Terrible timing, obviously."

"I need a job," he heard himself say. "A real job. My A-1 temp permit needs renewal next month, and they want employment verification for the aliyah application."

"Then we need to make it official," she said. "The practical part. You need a work visa at least, better to start the aliyah process properly. My cousin works at Misrad HaPnim." Ministry of Interior. "She says bring everything. Birth certificate, parents' birth certificates, grandparents' death certificates, everything proving you're Jewish enough to return."

Jewish enough. The phrase caught in his throat. Jewish enough for the Nazis to kill, now needing to prove Jewish enough for Israel to accept.

He thought about the word he had been avoiding since Columbus Circle. *Zionist.* The activist's camera in his face: *ARE YOU A ZIONIST?* He had said he did not know. But he was filling out aliyah forms. He was choosing to raise children in the Jewish state. The word still tasted wrong, too politicized, too loaded with other people's definitions. But avoiding it was its own kind of dishonesty. He was a Zionist by action if not by slogan. He believed this state had a right to exist and he was staking his family's future on it. He also knew, had seen with his own eyes, the cost paid disproportionately by people who had not chosen to pay it. Both things were true. The aliyah forms did not have a box for that.

---

That afternoon, Ethan came home early to find Sera on the living room floor, hundreds of photographs spread around her like a paper timeline, her laptop open beside her showing grant application forms.

"Applying for the Herzl Institute fellowship," she said, not looking up. "For documentary projects addressing collective trauma." She held up a photo of teenage Michal laughing at some long-ago beach. "Michal always said photos steal souls. I told her that was superstitious bullshit. Now I'm trying to prove her wrong."

"I have seventeen years of her in here. The fellowship would fund a proper exhibition. 'Interrupted Narratives: Lives Stolen by October 7th.' I'd photograph the places where they should have been — empty wedding venues, vacant apartments, unsent job applications."

"She was at Nova," Sera added quietly. "The festival. We were supposed to go together but I had a wedding to shoot. She went alone. Danced alone. Died alone while I was photographing someone else's happiness."

Ethan sat carefully at the edge of the photos, not disturbing the pattern.

"Sometimes I think that's why I fell for you. Another interrupted story."

---

The construction site foreman, Yossi, looked at his soft hands, his American clothes, his immigrant desperation.

"You ever build anything?"

"No."

"You strong?"

"I can become strong."

Yossi laughed, not unkindly. "Americans. You all come to find yourselves. You know what you find? Stones are heavy. Sun is hot. Building hurts. Tomorrow, five AM. Bring water. Bring work clothes. Bring humility."

He lasted — barely. The first day, he carried a bag of cement up two flights and tore something in his shoulder that wouldn't heal for months. The second day, he mixed mortar wrong and Yossi made him dump it and start over while the crew watched. The third day, he dropped a box of tiles worth eight hundred shekels. Yossi docked his pay without discussion.

But every morning, predawn darkness to the site, hands blistering then callousing, back screaming then strengthening, Hebrew improving through construction vocabulary — concrete, rebar, level, plumb. The Palestinians on the crew taught him Arabic curses. The Russians taught him to drink Turkish coffee so strong it could dissolve mortar. By ten the heat pressed down like a hand on the back of his neck. By noon the stone they carried burned through their work gloves. They moved slower in the midday hours — Jerusalem July could put a man on the ground if he fought it.

Mahmoud arrived every morning before anyone, already sweating through his shirt. He left Bethlehem at 3 AM to reach the Checkpoint 300 crossing by 3:30. Some mornings the line moved in twenty minutes. Some mornings it took two hours, bodies pressed into the metal-caged corridor, permits checked and rechecked, and he'd arrive at the site late and lose an hour's wage without discussion. He never complained about it. Not from stoicism — Mahmoud complained freely about the concrete mix, the architect's idiotic drainage specs, the coffee Yossi bought. The checkpoint he treated like weather. Something that happened to you every day and couldn't be changed and wasn't worth the breath. Ethan once asked him about it, and Mahmoud had looked at him with the gentle bewilderment of someone being asked to describe gravity. "This is how I go to work," he said. "How do you go to work?"

One afternoon they were pouring the fourth-floor slab, the whole crew in rhythm — Mahmoud mixing, Yossi directing the pump, the Russians hauling forms, Ethan on the vibrator tamping out air bubbles. Without anyone deciding, someone started singing. Viktor began with something low and Ukrainian. Mahmoud countered with an Arabic work song his father had taught him. Then Yossi was belting an old Israeli army tune completely off-key, and Ethan tried with the only Hebrew song he knew all the words to — Hava Nagila, which made the entire crew groan and then laugh harder.

They were covered in concrete splatter. The sun was cooking them. Mahmoud's song and Yossi's song clashed in every possible musical dimension. The sound was terrible, genuinely awful, and nobody cared. Just six men building something together in July heat, singing badly, alive and ridiculous.

---

The following Tuesday, Mahmoud and two other Palestinian workers weren't at the site.

"New security restrictions," Yossi told the crew. "Permits revoked. All Palestinian workers, effective this morning." He kicked a bag of grout. "I've got a deadline in six weeks and they take my best tile man."

Ethan stood in the half-finished apartment, the floor where Mahmoud's tiles should have been going down still bare concrete. He thought about his own paperwork sitting in the Ministry of Interior — the aliyah application, the Law of Return, the documents proving his grandmother's blood entitled him to walk through doors that Mahmoud, who'd built these doors, could not. Different paperwork. Same system. One set of papers opened a country; the other set closed it.

He didn't say anything. There was nothing to say that wouldn't sound like exactly what it was. He texted Mahmoud that evening. No response. The next day, nothing. He asked Yossi for Mahmoud's number and Yossi said, "You have it. He's not answering anyone. When permits get pulled, they go quiet. They've learned not to make noise."

The quiet was worse than complaint. Ethan thought about his grandmother's stories of Shanghai — how the refugees had learned the same thing. Don't make noise. Don't draw attention. Wait for the bureaucracy to remember you're human. The parallel was obvious enough that he hated himself for noticing it, because noticing was not the same as doing anything, and he was not doing anything.

Three days later, Mahmoud's permit was reinstated. He showed up at five as if nothing had happened, set his tools down, inspected where a Russian had tried to continue his tilework. Quietly pulled up two rows laid at the wrong angle. He didn't complain about the lost wages — three days of pay gone, no recourse, no appeal, absorbed into the household budget like a tax on existing. Over mint tea at the midday break he mentioned his daughter had a math exam he'd been able to help her study for, so the days weren't wasted. He showed Ethan a photo on his phone: a girl of ten or eleven, dark-eyed, serious, holding up a worksheet covered in equations. "She wants to be an engineer," Mahmoud said. "She tells me, Baba, I will build bridges. I tell her, habibti, start with the math." He put the phone away and went back to cutting tile, measuring twice with the steel square his own father had used. The tool was older than the state that issued and revoked his permit to use it.

Neither of them mentioned the three empty days again. The crack in the foundation was filled with epoxy. You could still see the line if you knew where to look.

---

His parents arrived two weeks later. They found their son dark from sun, rough from labor, speaking broken Hebrew to his pregnant girlfriend — not wife, they noted with concern — in a two-room apartment in East Talpiot where the mezuzah was actually touched, actually kissed, actually treated like more than decoration.

"This is what you chose?" His mother's voice cracked. She stood at the window, watching the construction site.

"Mom, this is what — it chose me."

"We gave you everything. Good schools. Safety. And you choose this?"

"You gave me everything except a reason to be Jewish. Beyond avoiding antisemitism. Here, my child will know who they are."

"Your child will grow up in a war zone," his father said.

"Home. My child will grow up home."

His mother reached into her purse, pulled out something wrapped in tissue paper. His grandmother's Shabbat candlesticks, the ones from Poland. "Your father found them in the attic. Along with her ketubah. And her diary. From 1948."

"She wrote about dancing in the streets of Brooklyn," his father said quietly. "Crying and dancing. She wrote she wished her parents could see it. That the promise had been kept." He looked at his son, conflict raw in his eyes. "I never knew she dreamed of going there herself. Never knew she saved money for a trip that never happened."

---

The next evening, his mother surprised him. "I got tickets. Jerusalem Theater. Beethoven — the late quartets. Opus 132. Don't argue."

The Heiliger Dankgesang began. Beethoven's hymn of thanksgiving after illness, the music moving between ancient modal writing and something almost modern, almost free. The first violin climbed into a register so high and pure it seemed to exist outside of argument. Outside of covenant.

Ethan felt something open in his chest that prayer hadn't reached. The music needed nothing from him. No response. No belief. No identity. It moved through the hall like light through glass.

His mother was crying, but differently this time.

"This is what you gave up," she whispered during the pause between movements. "Not Brooklyn. Not the salary. This — the freedom to be moved by something without needing it to mean you're chosen."

He had no answer. The music asked nothing except listening. His life here asked everything.

They walked back through the warm night without speaking. She held his arm. He did not try to explain why he was staying. She did not ask again.

At the airport the next morning, his mother hugged him long, whispered, "When you come to your senses — and you will — we're here."

---

That evening, Shabbat arriving as the heat finally loosened its grip. Ethan's hands, rough from the week's labor, lit candles with Sera. The Hebrew blessings came easier now, from repetition if not understanding.

"Baruch atah Adonai..." Blessed are you, Lord...

Through the window, construction cranes paused against the skyline.

"Your hands," Sera said, taking them after the blessing. "They're different."

They were. Calloused, scarred, strong. Builder's hands. Israeli hands.

"Everything's different," he said in Hebrew, the words coming without translation now, without thought, the language thinking for him as Sera had promised.

She guided his rough hands to her belly where something built itself without blueprint, without struggle.

"Names," she said. "If it's a boy?"

"Something with building. Boneh? Binyamin?"

"From everything," she said. "You came from grandmothers. From October 7th. From Cairo and Berlin and — " She yawned. "All of it led here."

The muezzin's call echoed from East Jerusalem, church bells from the Christian Quarter, and in their apartment, Jewish life continued its insistence on continuing.

His phone, set to silent, showed a text from his mother he wouldn't read until tomorrow: "Your father thinks I should let you go. I can't. You're my son. Even this version I don't recognize."

"Shabbat shalom," Sera said.

"Shabbat shalom," he replied, his builder's hands holding her carefully, concrete dust still in the grain of his prayer book.

Outside, the cranes stood motionless against the sky, waiting for Sunday. The worry remained, quiet and persistent as Jerusalem stone.
