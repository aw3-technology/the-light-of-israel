# Chapter 25: Egypt — First Exile

Thursday morning, December 7th. Ethan stood on his Cairo hotel balcony watching the sun rise over the Nile. Below, car horns and call to prayer, the smell of diesel and breakfast rising with the heat.

Forty days since he'd left Israel. Poland's earth still in his pocket.

His phone showed seventeen missed calls from his mother. He listened to the latest voicemail. Her voice was cold: "Forty days, Ethan. Forty days and I'm getting your itinerary from Find My Friends. Egypt. You went to Egypt and didn't tell me. Your father says I should give you space, but your father also thinks yogurt counts as dinner, so."

A pause, and then quieter, angrier: "You know what, don't call me back. I'm done chasing you across a map."

He almost smiled. His mother, furious. More honest than her usual worry. He texted back: "I'm safe. I'm sorry. I love you." She didn't respond for three hours.

The taxi driver studied him in the rearview mirror. "American?"

"Yes."

"Business or tourist?"

"Visiting history."

The driver, Mohamed according to his license, laughed. "All tourists visit history. You want pyramids? Sphinx?"

"Actually," Ethan said, showing his phone, "I need to go here."

Mohamed looked at the address, his expression shifting. The Jewish Quarter. "Why you want go there? Nothing there for tourists."

"There's a synagogue."

Silence filled the car. Mohamed's eyes found his again. "You are Jewish?"

The question hung between them.

"Yes."

Mohamed drove in silence, navigating Cairo's impossible traffic. Finally: "You know what we think about Israel here."

"I know."

"But individual Jews... People of the Book. Protected. Guests."

The synagogue looked like nothing. Deliberately nothing. Hidden behind walls and guards.

Inside, nine old men sat in what had once been grand. The mosaic floor was cracked in a dozen places, the pattern still readable through the breaking — Stars of David fractured but holding shape. Nine. One short of a *minyan*.

When Ethan entered, they looked up with desperate hope. The eldest, Joseph Haroun, stood with difficulty. Another man, thin, wearing a *yarmulke* held down with bobby pins, muttered, "Finally. My grandson's wedding photos are getting cold in my pocket."

"Hush, Sami," Joseph said. "You are the tenth," he told Ethan. "We have been here since dawn hoping for a tenth."

"Since dawn?" Sami adjusted his bobby pins. "I've been here since Thursday. My arthritis doesn't care about your dramatic timing."

They needed no explanation. He was Jewish, he was the tenth, he was the answer to their morning prayer.

The service proceeded in Hebrew that sounded different here. Egyptian-accented, older than anything he'd heard.

After prayers, Joseph insisted on tea. "Real tea," he said, "not the tourist garbage. My late wife Miriam knew how to make proper tea." He measured leaves with the precision of a pharmacist. "She ran the kosher kitchen at Adly Mansour Hospital for thirty years. Fed half the Jewish community when they were sick, and half the Muslim doctors too."

Sami adjusted his bobby pins. "Joseph's always talking about his wife. Dead ten years, still arguing with her."

"Twelve years," Joseph corrected sharply. "And she never let me win an argument alive, why should death change that?"

David showed Ethan a creased photo. "My great-granddaughter in Montreal. Twelve years old, speaks perfect French, decent Hebrew, not a word of Arabic." His pride mixed with loss. "Her mother sends videos sometimes. The girl's winning math competitions."

"You see this cabinet?" Joseph fumbled with keys. "Not the famous *Geniza*. Cambridge University owns our history now, making British careers out of our letters." His bitterness was earned. "But we kept some fragments they missed."

He opened it carefully. "Business correspondence mostly. This one, 1967, right after the war. A coffee merchant writing to his partner in Milan: 'Nasser's speeches getting uglier. Time to liquidate inventory.'" His finger traced faded Arabic mixed with Hebrew. "My father's handwriting. He was that merchant."

"How many Jews are left in all of Egypt?"

The three men exchanged glances. Joseph sighed. "Official count? Maybe fifty. Real count?" He shrugged. "Who knows? Some hide it completely. Can you blame them?"

Sami snorted. "Joseph always says fifty. Last year he said fifty. Ten years ago, fifty. Meanwhile, every year someone dies or leaves. It's not fifty. It's thirty, maybe twenty-five. But fifty sounds better in interviews."

"It might be fifty," Joseph said stiffly.

"It might be fifty if you count the cat," Sami said. "The cat comes to services more regularly than David."

David, the third man, who had been dozing, opened one eye. "I heard that. I come every week."

"You come when it's cold outside and the synagogue has heating."

"The synagogue has better heating than my apartment. This is a valid reason for worship." David closed his eye again. "Also, God and I have an arrangement. I show up. He doesn't ask questions."

Joseph threw up his hands. "You see what I work with? This is the future of Egyptian Jewry. A man who comes to temple for the radiator and a man who argues with everyone including God."

"Especially God," Sami corrected. "God can take it."

In Khan el-Khalili bazaar, Ethan stopped at a bookstall. "Protocols" in Arabic beside cookbooks. "Mein Kampf" next to travel guides. The vendor, seeing his interest, said in English: "Very popular. Explains many things." He tapped the cover. "History books."

Ethan walked away. His hands were shaking, though whether from anger or fear he couldn't tell.

Back in the taxi, Mohamed had been quiet, navigating traffic with the focused aggression of a Cairo native. Finally: "So you are really Jewish? American Jewish or...?"

"American. But my family comes from Poland originally."

Mohamed nodded, turning a thought over. "My daughter, she studies at university. International relations. She says the situation is..." He searched for words. "Complicated."

"Your daughter sounds smart."

"Too smart. Questions everything I tell her about Israel, about Jews." A rueful laugh. "Kids today, they read too much internet."

They crawled through traffic for another block. Mohamed glanced at the clock on his dashboard, then back at Ethan in the mirror. Something shifted in his face, a decision being made.

"You eat yet today?"

"Not really."

"I know a place. My family's place, not tourist. You want to eat something real before you go to the water?" He said it casually, but Ethan caught the deliberateness underneath. An offering.

"I'd like that."

Mohamed turned down a side street Ethan would never have found, then another, then parked beside a wall tagged with Arabic graffiti. The restaurant had no sign. Just a metal door propped open with a cinder block and a fluorescent light buzzing inside. Four plastic tables. A television mounted on the wall showing a football match with the sound off. The owner, a heavy man in a stained apron, greeted Mohamed by name, clasped his hand, spoke rapidly in Arabic.

Mohamed ordered for both of them without asking. "You trust me?"

"At this point, yes."

The food arrived quickly. Ful medames in a battered metal bowl, still bubbling. Flatbread torn from a larger round. Pickled turnips stained pink. A plate of tahini with oil pooling in the center. Two glasses of tea dark enough to stand a spoon in.

Mohamed tore bread and handed half to Ethan. "Eat. You're too thin. Your mother would be angry with me."

"My mother is already angry with me."

"All mothers." Mohamed spooned ful onto his bread with practiced economy. "My mother, same. I call her every day, she says I don't call enough. I call twice, she says I'm calling too much, something must be wrong."

They ate in the comfortable silence of shared food. The ful was earthy and rich, heavy with cumin and garlic, the bread warm and faintly charred. The owner brought a second round of tea without being asked, and Mohamed thanked him with a nod that carried years of habit.

Halfway through the meal, Mohamed set down his bread and looked at Ethan directly. Not through the rearview mirror this time. Face to face.

"You asked me before. About Jews who lived here."

"Yes."

"My grandmother — her name was Fatma — she lived in Heliopolis her whole life. Same apartment, same street, sixty years. And next door to her, the whole time she was growing up, there was a Jewish family. The Cohens." He said the name carefully, as if handling something that could still break. "Daoud Cohen and his wife Simha. They had three children. Two boys, one girl. The girl was my grandmother's age. Yael."

He paused to drink his tea.

"My grandmother and Yael — not best friends exactly. My grandmother was Muslim, covered her hair, prayed five times. Yael kept Shabbat, went to the synagogue on Saturday. Different lives. But they shared a balcony wall. You know how the old buildings are? Balconies so close you can pass things across. So they passed things. Yael's mother sent over pastries on Jewish holidays — my grandmother said they were the best she ever tasted, some kind of almond cookie with rosewater. And my grandmother's mother sent dishes back during Ramadan. Back and forth, over that wall, for years."

The owner replaced their tea glasses. Mohamed stirred sugar into his with a small spoon, three rotations, precise.

"Yael taught my grandmother to count in Hebrew. My grandmother taught Yael to say *bismillah* before eating. Stupid things. Kid things. But my grandmother remembered every word sixty years later. She'd count in Hebrew sometimes when she was cooking — *echad, shtayim, shalosh* — and my mother would say, 'Mama, what language is that?' And she'd say, 'The language next door.'"

He looked down at the table, at his hands around the glass.

"After sixty-seven, the war, it happened fast. Nasser on the radio every night. 'The Zionist enemy. The Jewish traitors.' Police came to the Jewish houses. Not the Cohens the first night, but others on the street. Arrests. Property seized. People taken in the middle of the night. The neighbors — the Muslim neighbors — some helped. Most just watched. My grandmother watched too. She told me this. She didn't help. She was afraid. She had children by then."

His voice had gone quiet. The football match played silently on the wall above them.

"The Cohens left in the night. No goodbye. My grandmother woke up and the apartment was dark. Door open. Some furniture still inside. Clothes in the closets. Simha's kitchen things still on the counter — a pot on the stove with water still in it, like she'd been about to cook and then just stopped. Left everything. Yael's school books on the table."

Mohamed looked up. His eyes were wet, and he didn't wipe them or look away.

"My grandmother went into that apartment every day for a month, waiting for them to come back. She watered Simha's plants. She collected their mail. Then the government men came and sealed the apartment and gave it to another family, and my grandmother never said the Cohens' name again in public. But at home, when it was just family, she would say, 'I wonder where Yael is. I wonder if Yael has children now.' For years she said this. Until she died she was wondering."

The restaurant was quiet around them. The owner had retreated behind his counter, polishing a glass that was already clean.

"She found out, at the end," Mohamed said. "A cousin of the Cohens wrote a letter from Paris in 1985. The whole family went to France first, then Israel. Yael married, had four children. Became a teacher in Haifa. The letter said they were well. My grandmother kept it in her Quran."

He picked up his bread again, tore a piece, but didn't eat it.

"I tell my daughter this story. She says, 'Baba, it's sad but it's politics, it's history.' She's right. She's smart. But she's also wrong. It's not politics when you know the name. When you know Simha made almond cookies. When you know Yael counted in Arabic because your grandmother taught her. Then it's just — " He opened his hands, searching. "Then it's just people who aren't there anymore."

Ethan couldn't speak for a moment. The ful sat cooling between them.

"Thank you for telling me that," Ethan said.

Mohamed shrugged, embarrassed now. "My grandmother would have wanted me to. She liked Jews. She just never got to tell one."

He insisted on paying. Ethan didn't argue.


His phone showed sunset approaching. First night of *Hanukkah*. He'd almost forgotten.

"There's somewhere else I need to go," he told Mohamed. "By the water."

Mohamed looked skeptical. "Red Sea? You want expensive tourist beach?"

"Not tourist. Just water."

At the shore, wind whipped off the sea. Ethan pulled out his grandmother's small travel candlesticks, the ones she'd carried from Poland to Brooklyn.

He set them on a rock.

Mohamed approached, curious despite himself. "What are you doing?"

"Lighting candles. Holiday called *Hanukkah*."

"Christmas?"

"Older."

Ethan struck a match. The first candle caught. He said the blessing quietly.

Mohamed watched, hands in his pockets. "My grandmother used to light candles too. Islamic way, but..." He shrugged. "Light is light."

They stood together watching the flame struggle against the salt wind. Mohamed was quiet for a while, then spoke carefully, as if weighing whether to share what came next.

"In Islam, we have this idea. *Tawakkul*. Trust in God. Not blind trust. You tie your camel first, then trust God." He laughed. "It means you do the work. Light your candle, say your prayer. But you accept that the wind is not your business. The wind is God's business."

"Judaism is mostly about arguing with the wind," Ethan said.

"Yes! This is what my daughter says. She studies your philosophers. She says Jews invented arguing with God. We think this is very strange." He paused. "But also brave. In Islam, we submit. The word *Islam* means submission. Your tradition says, what, *wrestle*? Maybe both are needed. The submission and the wrestling." He made a gesture with both hands, balancing. "My grandmother, she would pray five times and never doubt. I pray five times and doubt every one. But I still pray. Maybe your grandmother was the same?"

"Your grandmother sounds wise," Ethan said.

"She couldn't read," Mohamed said. "But she knew things books don't teach."

They stood together as the candle burned down. When it was finished, Ethan walked to the water's edge and pulled out the vial of Polish earth.

He scattered the soil into the foam. Polish earth mixing with water that had witnessed the first leaving.

"I'm going back to Israel," he told Mohamed.

"Of course you are," Mohamed replied, without judgment.

Mohamed extended his hand. Ethan took it. The grip was firm and held a beat longer than necessary.

"If you come back to Cairo," Mohamed said, "you come to the restaurant again. I'll tell my grandmother's story to your children."

"I'd like that."

"*Inshallah*." He paused. "You say that too, yes? God willing?"

"*Im yirtzeh Hashem*."

"Same God, different language." Mohamed smiled. "Go. Before I charge you for the whole day."

Next morning, Joseph was waiting at the synagogue as if Ethan had promised to return, though he hadn't. The old man had laid out tea and was sorting through the cabinet with movements careful and arthritic and slightly ceremonial.

"I found something last night," Joseph said. "After you left, I couldn't sleep. The older fragments, the ones from before the British took everything — I went through them again." He unwrapped a fragment in protective plastic, handling it with cotton-gloved fingers that trembled. "This is from the real *Geniza*. Not the 1967 letters. Older. Much older."

The fragment was smaller than Ethan expected. Parchment the color of weak tea, edges crumbled to lace.

"A merchant," Joseph said. "Writing to his son in Jerusalem. We think 1099, maybe a few years earlier." He pointed to a line near the top. "Here -- 'If I forget thee, O Jerusalem, may my right hand forget its craft.' He's quoting Psalms, but he changes the last word. Not *cunning*. *Craft*. Because he's a merchant. He makes it his own."

Joseph traced the lines with a gloved fingertip, translating as he went. "He writes about his trade routes. About prices of indigo and silk. Practical things. And then here, in the middle of a paragraph about shipping costs, he says — " Joseph's voice shifted, reading: "'Tell your mother I am well. Tell her the rains came. Tell her I dream of the courtyard and the sound of her grinding spices in the morning.'"

"A thousand years," Ethan said.

"A thousand years, and he misses the sound of his wife in the kitchen." Joseph looked up. "Some things don't change."

He turned the fragment over, gently. "Now. This is what I wanted to show you." On the reverse, another hand — a notation, possibly a filing mark from the *Geniza* itself. A name. Joseph held a magnifying glass over it and angled the desk lamp.

"The merchant's name. Can you read it?"

Ethan leaned close. The script was cramped, some letters smudged where the ink had run. He could make out a *resh*, then what might be a *vav*, then a cluster of letters — *zayin*, *nun*, and something after that was mostly gone, eaten by nine centuries.

"Rozen..." Ethan said. "Rozen-something."

"Rozenstein, maybe. Or Rozenfeld. The last letters are damaged." Joseph set down the magnifying glass and looked at Ethan over the rims of his spectacles. "Your family name. Before it was Stone. What was it?"

The question landed strangely. "Rosen. My grandmother's maiden name was Rosen."

Joseph was quiet for a moment. He picked up the magnifying glass again, held it over the fragment, set it down. "Rosen," he repeated. "Could be. Rozenstein is not Rosen, but names shift. They get shortened, translated. A Rozenstein in Cairo becomes a Rosen in Krakow becomes a Stone in Brooklyn. It happens." He paused. "Could also be coincidence. We have many Rosens in the archives. Rosen, Rosenthal, Rozenberg — it was a common root. Like Smith in English. Everyone's a Rosen somewhere."

But he kept looking at the fragment.

"The merchant was writing to his son in Jerusalem," Joseph said again, almost to himself. "A thousand years ago. And now the son's descendant — maybe, maybe not — walks into my synagogue because we needed a tenth for *minyan*." He shook his head. "I'm an old man. I see patterns in everything."

"Sami would say that," Ethan agreed.

"Sami would be right." Joseph rewrapped the fragment with the tenderness of a man swaddling an infant. "But I'll tell you something. I've been the keeper of these fragments for forty years. My hands shake now. Soon I won't be able to handle them properly. And in forty years, this is the first time someone with a name close to one of these merchants has walked through that door." He placed the fragment back in the cabinet. "Make of that what you will. I make nothing of it. I just notice it."

Ethan stood in the dim synagogue, the fragment sealed away behind the cabinet door, and felt something he couldn't name. Something unsettled, like hearing a melody through a wall and not being sure whether you recognize it or only want to.

The plane touched down in Tel Aviv. Passengers applauded. Through the window, the Judean hills. Jerusalem somewhere beyond them.

The customs officer looked at his passport. Warsaw, Rome, Berlin, Cairo.

"Tourism?"

"Close enough."

She stamped it. "*Bruchim haba'im*." Welcome.

Outside, December light. Hebrew voices.

He walked toward the arrivals hall.
