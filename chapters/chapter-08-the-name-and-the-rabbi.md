# Chapter 8: The Name and the Rabbi

The building at 541 West 77th Street looked like any other Upper West Side brownstone. Tuesday evening, mid-October. Ethan had walked past three times before stopping.

First pass: just walking, thinking. Another day of optimization meetings where he'd spoken fluent corporate while Friday's Shabbat service replayed in his mind.

Second pass: checking the card—Rabbi Levi Ben-David, Tuesday Evening Learning, All Levels Welcome. The last line underlined in blue ink.

Third pass: standing at the base of a narrow flight of stairs. The *mezuzah* on the doorframe caught dying light—a small brass case, tilted slightly, the way they're supposed to be. The stairs were steep and narrow, the kind New York stopped building in the 1940s, each tread worn to a valley in the center by decades of feet. The banister was wooden, dark with hand-oil, and when Ethan gripped it he could feel the smoothness where thousands of palms had preceded his. Halfway up, a water stain on the wall in the shape of something he almost recognized. The landing smelled of cooking—onions, maybe, or the sweet edge of braised meat from a neighbor's kitchen. A mezuzah on the second door too, and the third.

The door opened before he knocked.

"I thought that was you." The secretary stood in the doorway, silver-haired with amused eyes and a reading light clipped to the book in her hand—a romance novel, he noticed, with a shirtless man on the cover, which she didn't bother to hide. "Third time's the charm. Though in Hebrew, three times makes a *chazakah*. Permanence."

"I wasn't—I was just—"

"Walking. Rabbi Levi mentioned you might walk by. He has a gift for knowing when someone's circling." She held the door wider with her hip. Her cardigan was buttoned wrong—one button off, the whole thing slightly crooked—and she either didn't know or didn't care. "He's finishing with another student. Tea?"

The waiting room was everything his startup wasn't. Worn floors that creaked, walls lined with photographs of rabbis whose eyes measured time differently. A radiator clanked somewhere.

"Sugar?" Russian-style tea in a metal holder.

A door opened. A young woman emerged, eyes red-rimmed but relieved, clutching a photocopied page to her chest like a prescription. She was maybe twenty-five, nose ring, combat boots, a tattoo of Hebrew letters visible on her wrist. She nodded at the secretary, who handed her a tissue without looking up from her novel. The young woman glanced at Ethan with the solidarity of someone leaving a doctor's office—*your turn, good luck, it helps more than you think.*

"You can go in."

The study door stood ajar, warm light spilling out. He could hear the rabbi inside, the creak of a chair, the sound of a book being closed.

The room hit him before the rabbi did. Floor-to-ceiling bookshelves on every wall, books stacked sideways on top of books shelved properly, Post-it notes bristling from spines like yellow feathers. One shelf bowed visibly under the weight of forty years of accumulated commentary. The desk was older than anything in Ethan's life, its surface a geography of ring stains and ink marks. A brass lamp with a green glass shade cast the room in the color of old libraries. Behind the desk, a framed photograph: a younger Rabbi Levi standing beside an ancient man in Jerusalem, the Western Wall blurred gold behind them. Next to it, a smaller photo of a girl in a soccer uniform, gap-toothed, holding a trophy. A gray cat slept on a stack of Talmud volumes in the corner, its tail draped over the binding of Tractate Berachot. The room smelled of pipe tobacco and old paper and something else--cedar, maybe, or sandalwood.

"Shalom, Ethan Israel Stone."

The words hit with the force of recognition. Rabbi Levi Ben-David rose from behind the desk, showing no surprise at Ethan's shock.

"How did you—nobody knows my middle name."

"Your grandmother Rivka knew it. Spoke it often." The rabbi was younger than expected. Maybe fifty, beard more pepper than salt, eyes holding the patience of someone who reads slowly and remembers everything. "She was my teacher's student in Brooklyn. Before the Parkinson's."

A fissure opened in Ethan's chest. Not pain exactly. Something structural shifting, a load-bearing wall developing its first crack. "She talked about me?"

"To everyone who would listen. 'My Ethan,' she'd say. Said you had a Jewish soul in exile from itself." Rabbi Levi settled back in his chair, hands folded over his stomach. "She came to my teacher's study every Thursday for years. Took the F train from Flatbush, then the shuttle, then walked six blocks. Rain, snow, August heat. Every Thursday until the Parkinson's made the subway too dangerous—the shaking, the turnstiles. The last time she came, she brought a tin of rugelach. Apricot, I remember, because my teacher was allergic to walnuts and she always remembered. And a photograph of you at your bar mitzvah. Standing at the *bimah* in a suit two sizes too large." He paused. "'He'll come back,' she told my teacher. 'Not to me. But he'll come back.'"

The gray cat lifted its head, blinked at Ethan, resettled.

Ethan's hands had gone still in his lap. He was aware of his body in the way you become aware of it during turbulence—the weight of his legs, the pulse in his wrists, the breath that had gone shallow without his permission. His grandmother on the F train. Every Thursday. The image arrived fully formed and wouldn't leave—her in her good coat, the one with the fur collar she'd bought at a consignment shop on Flatbush Avenue, her hands trembling around a tin of rugelach, his photograph in her purse. Carrying it like evidence. Like a prayer she refused to abandon.

All those Thursdays. All those years when he was in college pretending Judaism was a sociological curiosity, when he was building product roadmaps and optimizing conversion funnels and dating women who thought his Hebrew name was charming the way a vintage jacket is charming. She was riding the subway with his bar mitzvah photo and a tin of cookies, telling a rabbi's teacher that her grandson would come back. The certainty of it—her certainty, unearned by anything he'd done—closed his throat. He pressed his palm flat against his thigh and tried to breathe.

"She was right, apparently." Rabbi Levi gestured to the worn chair across from him.

"But someone must've told you..."

"You think I'm performing magic tricks?" Rabbi Levi poured tea.

"I'm not religious."

"Neither was Moses when the bush caught fire." He cleaned his glasses, missed a spot, gave up. His phone buzzed on the desk; he glanced at it, silenced it with a sigh. "My daughter. She thinks I don't eat unless she texts me every ninety minutes." He gestured at the photo of the gap-toothed soccer player. "She's thirty-one now. Still thinks I'm incapable of operating a microwave. She's not entirely wrong." He set the phone face-down. "What brought you here?"

"October 7th."

"No. October 7th woke something. You can't wake what isn't there. What was already there?"

The radiator clanked.

"I don't know who I am."

Silence. Rabbi Levi let it stretch, let the words find their weight.

"Good." The rabbi cleaned his glasses again, considering.

The rabbi pulled a taped-together volume from the shelf. "Your depression. I can see it. Keep taking medication if you're on it."

"Sertraline," Ethan said, surprising himself with the admission. "Fifty milligrams. My therapist doubled it after October 7th."

"Good. Keep taking it. Keep seeing your therapist."

Ethan blinked. He'd been braced for the pitch—the spiritual bypass, the well-meaning rabbi who'd tell him to trade SSRIs for psalms. Every religious person he'd ever met had eventually gotten around to suggesting that God was better than chemistry. He'd expected this one to be no different. He'd expected a prescription for more prayer, a suggestion faith could replace chemistry. He'd been wrong.

"You're surprised."

"I guess I thought--"

"That I'd tell you to throw away the pills and daven three times a day?" The rabbi's voice carried an edge of weariness. "Half my congregants are medicated. Including me--twenty years of Lexapro. Torah doesn't replace serotonin. Anyone who tells you prayer alone can cure clinical depression is selling you something dangerous."

He paused. "But it's also chemistry. Usually both. The Talmud says we don't rely on miracles. That includes brain chemistry fixing itself through willpower."

A book slipped from a shelf, pages fluttering open. The Zohar, landing spine-up between them.

Ethan glanced at the shelf, then back. "Did the radiator--?"

Rabbi Levi shrugged, bending to retrieve it. "Old building. Things shift." But his eyes held a question too.

"You're making me nervous standing there like you might bolt."

Ethan sank into the chair. The leather yielded around him, warm from the radiator.

"Thursday." The rabbi handed him a photocopied page. Psalm 137. By the rivers of Babylon.

Ethan glanced from the shelves to his phone. "It feels like a system."

"It is." His phone buzzed again. He glanced, silenced it. "She's relentless. Gets it from her mother." A half-smile. "I once told a student that persistence is a Jewish virtue. My daughter took it as a personal mandate."

The cat stood, stretched elaborately, and relocated to Ethan's lap without invitation. Its weight was warm and startling. The rabbi raised an eyebrow. "She doesn't do that for everyone. Consider yourself vetted."

Through the door, footsteps—another student waiting.

"Come back Thursday. We'll study properly then." He stood, and the cat opened one eye. "Don't worry, I won't make you sing."

At the door, Ethan stopped. "My grandmother—she really talked about me?"

Rabbi Levi's expression softened. He reached to the shelf behind him and pulled down a photograph Ethan hadn't noticed, propped between two books. A small print, slightly out of focus. His grandmother, younger than he remembered, standing in this very room—or one very like it—with her hand on the shoulder of a boy in a too-large suit. Ethan at thirteen, hair uncombed, eyes already somewhere else.

"She left this here," the rabbi said. "After her last visit. Told my teacher to give it to whoever needed to see it."

Ethan held the photograph. His grandmother's face was turned slightly toward the camera, and she was smiling, but her grip on his shoulder—he could see it now, the white of her knuckles, the tension in her wrist—her grip said something her smile did not.

"Your Hebrew name, especially," the rabbi said. "Made it sound like a prayer."

Outside, October had turned properly cold. The stairs down felt different than climbing them had—lighter, as though he'd left something heavy in the room. His hand trailed along the wooden banister, and he noticed things he'd missed on the way up: a child's drawing taped to a first-floor door, a small ceramic *hamsa* nailed beside an apartment number, the building's ancient intercom with its row of handwritten names.

The N train came immediately. Nearly empty except for an elderly Black woman reading Hebrew, Koren edition with commentary.

She noticed his psalm page. "Good choice—that one stays with you." She smiled, returned to her reading.

At 14th Street, she stood. "*Shabbat shalom*," she said softly.

His apartment looked the same but felt different. The proportions had shifted, or he had. The IKEA furniture, the startup swag on the bookshelf, the smart speaker blinking its patient blue eye—all of it suddenly provisional. Temporary. A life assembled from convenience rather than conviction.

The candlesticks sat waiting on the dining table, tarnished silver ready to hold light.

He set the psalm beside them, Hebrew letters mysterious but insistent. The paper was still warm from his pocket.

His phone buzzed. Aaron: "Where are you? Missing trivia night."

"Can't make it."

"Everything okay?"

He looked at the psalm, the candlesticks, his reflection in the dark window. Himself but different.

Ethan selected his Thursday meetings, clicked decline.

"Israel," he whispered. First time in twenty years.

His phone buzzed. Aaron again. He let it ring.

He whispered his name once more into the quiet apartment.

"Israel."
