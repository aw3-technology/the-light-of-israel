# Chapter 33: Vision on the Mount

The Temple Mount at dawn, empty except for birds, and I saw what was, what is, and what might be.

He hadn't planned to be there. Jews rarely received permission to ascend, and when they did, it came with restrictions. But around 6 AM on the last day of *Sukkot*, walking the Old City walls in the half-light, a pull he couldn't name drew him toward the Mughrabi Gate. The morning air tasted of sage and dust. His footsteps echoed wrong. Too loud, then too soft, as if the sound were being filtered through time.

The guard looked up from his phone. His name tag read Ahmad al-Rashid. A wedding ring worn thin from years of nervous twisting. The booth smelled of Turkish coffee gone cold, cigarette smoke clinging to fabric.

"You don't have permission," Ahmad said in accented English, but without conviction. His shoulders sagged under a weight that went deeper than fatigue.

"No."

They stood there. Ahmad glanced at the security camera, back at Ethan, rubbed his face with both hands. A long silence. The kind that happens between strangers at an hour when the usual scripts haven't started yet.

"My daughter is sick," Ahmad said, not to explain, just to say it. "Leukemia. Hadassah hospital. The Jewish doctors are good." He said this plainly, without irony, the way you say a thing that is true regardless of everything else that is also true. "I sit here every morning and I think about her, and some mornings I am tired of thinking about her, which makes me a bad father, which makes me think about her more."

Ethan said nothing. There was nothing useful to say.

Ahmad studied his face. "You come here to pray?"

"I don't know."

"That's an honest answer." Ahmad almost smiled. "Everyone else who comes here knows exactly what they want. That's the problem with this place. Too many people who are certain." He glanced at the security camera. "Morning shift arrives in twelve minutes."

He stared at the security pad, numbers worn smooth by years of entry. "If they check the logs... I have six years until retirement. Pension for my daughter's treatments." He looked at Ethan. "Why should I risk that for you?"

Ethan had no answer.

Ahmad laughed bitterly. "My grandfather called prayer here forty years. Now my son won't visit from Berlin. Says he's ashamed of us all." He looked up at the security camera. "Maybe he's right."

His finger hovered over the keypad. "I don't know why I'm doing this. Maybe because it's early and I'm tired and my daughter is sick and you looked like someone who needed something, and I'm tired of not giving people things." He punched in the code. "Coffee spill on the keypad. Happens all the time."

The lock clicked.

"Quickly."

Ethan climbed the wooden ramp, each plank creaking a different note. His legs felt unsteady, as if the ground were tilting.

At the top, the plaza opened. Limestone worn smooth by millions of feet. Jerusalem sage sharp in the October air, but underneath it: frankincense, myrrh, the ghost of temple incense that shouldn't still linger after two thousand years.

A cat watched from beneath an olive tree, its eyes reflecting light that wasn't there yet.

The Dome of the Rock caught first light, gold burning against blue sky. But the gold moved. Rippled. Breathed. The blue tiles sang, each one a different note in a harmony that made Ethan's teeth ache. Al-Aqsa stretched elegant to the south. Between them, the vast plaza where everything had happened and was still happening. He could see it all at once, layered: Solomon's temple, Herod's courts, Crusader churches, Ottoman fountains. All existing simultaneously.

Birds erupted. Dozens of doves, their wings making sounds that resolved into Hebrew letters. Each feather left silver-white script in the air that hung for a moment before dissolving.

Ethan's vision sharpened until everything became too clear. The stones beneath his feet pulsed with their own heartbeat. He could feel it through his shoes, up his legs, synchronizing with his own pulse until he couldn't tell where he ended and the Mount began.

Then fire.

Not metaphor. Actual heat on his face, cedar smoke thick in his throat.

The sound of stones falling carried the salt taste of tears.

Grief as physical weight in his chest.

He was watching it burn, but also burning with it. Rome taking what couldn't be taken, only transformed.

*What is this? Am I dying? Having a stroke?*

This place demanded everything at once. It was breaking him open.

His grandmother's voice, wordless this time. Just the sound of her breathing, the way she'd breathed while standing at the stove, while gripping his hand, while watching news she wouldn't explain.

This wasn't hurt. This was dissolution.

The Dome blazed golden, and the gold rang. Blood from his nose mixed copper with cedar ash. Men arriving for *Fajr* moved in ripples he could see. Concentric circles of faith spreading outward, passing through him.

Children played where the Temple courtyards had been. Arab and Jewish children together, their voices harmonizing.

Then walls rose between them, each brick a small death in his chest.

Then the walls crumbled, the reunion sharp with joy and salt.

He fell through layers of time, each century a different weight pressing down. Gravity pulling him through Solomon's gold, Herod's marble, crusader stone, all the way down to bedrock that hummed with accumulated prayer.

When his sight cleared, he was sitting on stones. Ahmad pressing water to his lips, the liquid shocking cold. "You fell. Hit your head." Ahmad's voice sounded flat after the symphonies he'd been hearing.

"Did you see—"

"I saw you fall." Ahmad's face was careful, neutral. Then, quieter: "My grandfather had visions here. Before the medications. Said this place..." He gestured helplessly. "Shows you things."

"What did you see?"

Ahmad was quiet for a moment, eyes on the golden Dome. "A man who needed water. A father thinking of his sick daughter." He paused, almost smiled. "Birds flying in circles. Could mean anything. Or nothing."

Ahmad pulled out a worn photo from his wallet: his grandfather and another man in 1940s Haifa, arms around each other's shoulders, both smiling. "They owned shops next to each other. Made some kind of promise about..." He trailed off, studying Ethan's face. "Probably nothing."

He put the photo away. "My grandfather had strange ideas about time. Said some debts take generations to pay."

They traded numbers without talking about why.

Ethan walked back as Jerusalem woke. Shops opening, children heading to school. His head throbbed, a deep pulse behind his right eye that sent sparks across his vision with every heartbeat. The nosebleed had stopped but his upper lip was crusted with it. His legs worked but badly, each step deliberate and uncertain.

Halfway down the Via Dolorosa, he had to stop and lean against a wall. His hands were trembling -- a coarse, visible tremor, his fingers jerking independently. A shopkeeper opening his stall eyed him with concern. "*Sababa*?" You okay?

Ethan nodded, but when he pushed off the wall, the street tilted and he grabbed for the doorframe. His body felt hollowed, as if the vision had burned through his reserves and left nothing but the shell. Every sound was too loud. The scrape of a metal gate rang in his skull. The smell of coffee from a nearby cafe hit his stomach and he had to breathe through his mouth to keep from gagging.

A thought broke through: he could have died up there. Not from the fall, though the bump on his head was real enough. From whatever had happened. Seizure, stroke, cardiac event. He was a father of twins. He had no business climbing to contested holy sites at dawn. The fear arrived late but total: Sera getting a phone call, her husband found unconscious on the Haram al-Sharif. The paperwork. The questions. The boys growing up with a story about their father that was either miraculous or pathetic depending on who told it.

At home, Sera was feeding the twins. Ari fussing, Lev patient. She looked up, saw his face.

"Where were you?" 

"Walking. The Old City." 

"You look awful." Her voice sharpened. "What happened? Ethan, you're scaring me."

"I think... I don't know what happened." He dropped into a chair. "I was on the Temple Mount somehow and I had some kind of... vision? Seizure? I don't know."

"The Temple Mount?" She set down Avi's bottle hard. "How did you—" She stopped, stared at him. "You're bleeding."

He touched his nose. Dried blood.

"Jesus, Ethan. What if something had happened? What if you'd had a real seizure up there? What if they'd arrested you?" Her voice rose. "I'm here with two babies and you're off having mystical experiences in the most contested piece of real estate on earth?"

"Sera—"

"No." She rose, Avi still in her arms. "You get to search for meaning. You get to fall apart and rebuild yourself. But I don't get to fall apart, do I? I just get to hold everything together while you figure out who you are." Her eyes were bright with tears she wouldn't let fall. "I need you present. Not transcendent. Present."

She handed him Ezra, who immediately spit up on his shirt. "You're burning up. Eat something. Be a father."

He tried. He ate toast and it tasted of nothing. He drank water and his hands shook so badly it sloshed over the rim. The headache had settled into a steady migraine, light sensitivity turning the windows into instruments of torture. He closed the blinds, held Lev against his chest. Ten pounds of undeniable physical fact.

By noon he was shivering under a blanket on the couch despite the October heat. Sera took his temperature: 38.4. "You're going to a doctor."

"It's just exhaustion."

"You had a seizure on the Temple Mount and you're running a fever. You're going to a doctor or I'm calling an ambulance. Pick."

The doctor at the clinic, a Russian immigrant who'd been an ER surgeon in Moscow, shone a light in his eyes, checked his reflexes, asked about the fall. "Mild concussion, probably. The fever could be stress response, could be infection from the head wound." She prescribed rest and ibuprofen. "No spiritual revelations for at least a week," she added, straight-faced.

He slept fourteen hours and woke at 3 AM drenched in sweat. The vision came back in fragments: the singing tiles, the children playing, the walls rising. Each one brought a wave of nausea. He barely made it to the bathroom.

The next three days blurred together. Fever, sleep, the twins' cries pulling him to the surface. Sera's hand on his forehead checking temperature. The ibuprofen wearing off and the migraine returning in waves.

On the fourth day, the fever broke. He woke clear-headed for the first time, weak but present, and found Sera asleep in the chair beside the bed, both boys in their crib, the apartment quiet.

That evening, Ahmad texted: "Daughter same. No change. Doctors say wait."

Then: "My grandfather used to say this place makes people crazy. I think he was right."

Then: "Better delete these."

Ethan deleted them, but the words remained.

He thought of Samira's students, of the girl at the checkpoint. Ahmad's daughter belonged beside his own boys now.

He put the boys to bed, started the *Shema*, then stopped. The words felt suddenly foreign in his mouth, too big, too certain.

"*Shema*..." he began again, then just hummed the melody.

Avi watched him with dark, serious eyes. Ezra had already fallen asleep.

Ethan touched where his head had hit stone. The bump was real.

His phone buzzed. Ahmad: "Deleted everything. But kept the photo."
