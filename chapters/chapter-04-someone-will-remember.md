# Chapter 4: Someone Will Remember

The smell hit him first. Brisket and rosemary and a warmth that belonged to no restaurant he'd ever entered.

Friday morning, October 6th. Court Street hummed with its usual contradictions. A woman in Lululemon arguing about oat milk specifications while a homeless veteran counted change against a Citibike rack. The day before his grandmother's *yahrzeit*, though Ethan was trying not to think about that.

But then the smell from Rosen's Bakery, unchanged since 1947 according to the sign. That smell reached across thirty-three years and caught in his throat. The seven-year-old hand holding his grandmother's as she led him into a shop just like this one on Long Island. *Challah* and childhood and Friday afternoons he'd trained himself to forget.

Her voice came back to him, clear as the morning air. *Ethan Israel, we get the challah for Shabbos.* He turned, half-expecting to see her. But there was only his reflection in the bakery window.

His coffee tasted wrong. The barista had drawn a leaf in the foam. $7.50 for coffee that reminded him of nothing.

Friday nights, age seven. His grandmother's apartment in Kew Gardens smelled of chicken soup and furniture polish and a faint oldness he'd later recognize as Europe. The candles on her dining table, two flames that seemed enormous to his child eyes. Her hands circling them three times, covering her face, whispering Hebrew that sounded like singing.

He'd asked why she covered her eyes. To separate the regular from the holy, she'd said. When she opened them, *Shabbos* was here. But it's just Friday night, he'd protested. No, *bubbeleh*. When we light, it becomes more.

More. His parents would pick him up an hour later, his mother checking her watch, his father jingling car keys. Friday would become just Friday again.

Borough Hall platform. A busker played violin. Maybe *klezmer*, maybe just broken.

The train arrived already full. He squeezed in, found himself face to face with an elderly woman. Her sleeve rode up as she gripped the pole, revealing numbers tattooed on her forearm. Faded blue-black, six digits. She caught him looking. He stood, gestured to his seat. She sat.

*"A dank,"* she said quietly. Thank you in Yiddish.

He nodded, not trusting his voice.

The woman's perfume, violets and age, triggered it. His bar mitzvah. Temple Beth Shalom. Phonetic Hebrew tumbling from his mouth while his grandmother wept in the front row.

Afterward she'd told him he did good. His Hebrew, not so bad. He'd shrugged it off. Just a bar mitzvah. Everyone has one. But her voice had gone quiet. *Everyone?* Did he think everyone got to stand up there, to read from the *Torah* in broad daylight? She'd adjusted his tie. He didn't know. That was okay. Dance at your party. Eat too much cake. *This is what we wanted.*

But later that night, he'd found her in the coat room, furious. His mother had served shrimp cocktail at the reception. Rivka's face was flint.

*"Shrimp. At a bar mitzvah. Your mother does this on purpose."*

*"Grandma, it's just food—"*

*"It is never just food."* Her voice had an edge he'd only heard when she spoke about the past. *"You think I'm a crazy old woman. Superstitious. Your parents think so too. But I carried these rules through Shanghai, through two oceans, through fifty years of your grandfather telling me it didn't matter anymore. It matters. It will always matter. And someday you'll know why, or you won't, and if you won't—"* She'd stopped herself. Smoothed her dress. Returned to the party with a smile that frightened him more than the anger.

She could turn cold. Weeks of silence after his mother skipped *Yom Kippur* for a business trip. The way she'd look through you, not at you, when you said anything that revealed how little you understood what she'd survived to give you. Rivka's warmth was real, but it was earned, and it could be withdrawn.

It wasn't enough. He could see that now, standing in the subway, holding the pole where the woman with numbers had held it.

West 4th Street. Bodies shifting. Through the window he saw a gray coat, a familiar walk. Not her. Never her. Dead fourteen years this September.


Spring Street station. He climbed toward daylight. Behind him, conversation fragments:

Behind him, conversation fragments. Someone was sitting *shiva* for her mother. What's *shiva*? Jewish thing. Seven days of mourning.

The 10 AM standup would start without him. Third time this week. Marcus would notice. He'd already pulled Ethan aside Tuesday about his "engagement levels," as if grief could be scheduled around sprint planning.

Broome Street. His phone buzzed again. Probably the standup meeting wondering where he was. He ignored it.


Age eight, Thanksgiving. He'd found her crying in the bathroom. She held a photograph of three children in sailor suits with yellow stars on their coats. My brothers, she'd said. My sister. Where are they? he'd asked. The look she gave him was empty. Gone. He'd never seen the photo again.

Houston Street. His phone buzzed a third time. Rachel, maybe? He'd texted her Tuesday about coffee, but she hadn't responded. Or maybe Marcus, wondering why his assigned tickets weren't moving. The standup meeting would be over by now. His absence noted, judged, filed away for performance reviews.


*"These were my mother's. And her mother's. Heavy, no? Feel how heavy."*

*"Grandma, I don't even light Shabbat candles."*

*"So? You think I always lit candles? In Shanghai, we had no candles. We used cooking oil, little wicks made from shoelaces."* She'd laughed, not happily. *"God doesn't care about perfect candles, bubbeleh. God cares that someone tries."*

Then, sharper: *"You'll light them. Or you won't. But don't pretend you have the luxury of not choosing. Your great-uncle didn't choose. They chose for him."*

She'd thrust the candlesticks into his hands with a force that surprised him, then left the room. He'd heard her crying in the bathroom. Hard, angry sobs. A woman who knew everything she cared about was being lost, generation by generation, in the warm American bath of forgetting.

Someone will remember. The candlesticks were in his closet, wrapped in yellowed tissue paper.

His phone buzzed. Definitely work now. He checked: Marcus. "Where are you? Client meeting moved to 2 PM." The client meeting he'd forgotten about completely. The one that could save his job or end it.


Delancey Street. A melody caught in his throat. He'd been humming for blocks without noticing.

*Shabbat shalom, Shabbat sha*—

The lyrics were gone. His grandmother had tried to teach him, Friday after Friday, but he'd been more interested in television.

Home. The door stuck. The Thai container had leaked overnight. Through his window, his neighbor's kid practicing violin. Wrong notes. Starting over.

The closet door was stuck. He yanked it open. Behind shoes and jackets he'd forgotten about, the tissue paper from his grandmother's things.

The tissue paper crumbled at his touch. Forty years of waiting had made it brittle. The candlesticks inside were tarnished black, one base dented, heavier than he remembered. They smelled of metal and age and his grandmother's apartment--Murphy's oil soap and boiled chicken and a faint acrid trace that clung to everything she'd owned.

He set them on the kitchen counter next to his coffee maker. One wobbled on its dented base. In the overhead light, scratches in the silver looked almost like Hebrew letters. Almost.

No candles. He checked the junk drawer anyway. Birthday candles from years ago, a lavender aromatherapy candle from an ex. Nothing that belonged.

Outside, Brooklyn's Friday night was beginning. Through the walls, that Hebrew melody again, clearer now. A child's voice joining in, learning the words.

From somewhere below, a child's voice. "Mommy, why do we light candles?"

Muffled response: "Because *Bubbe* did."

"But why did *Bubbe*?"

Silence. Or an answer too quiet to hear.

Ethan stood in his kitchen, holding his grandmother's candlesticks. Empty, waiting. He opened the drawer, found matches left from winter blackouts. Struck one. The flame caught, steady and warm.

He held it over the candlestick, watching old wax soften in the base. Remnants from some Friday night decades ago. The flame reflected in the tarnished silver, multiplying.

His phone buzzed on the counter. Marcus again. 1:47 PM.

The match burned down to his fingers. He dropped it, watched it die on the kitchen floor.

The candlesticks remained on the counter. Tomorrow was his grandmother's *yahrzeit*. Today he had a meeting to save his job, emails to answer, a life to live in the present tense.

He didn't put the candlesticks away.

Outside, that child's voice again, learning the *Shabbat* melody. Getting the words wrong, starting over. Learning.
