# Chapter 36: Aleph

The key sticks in the lock the way it always does, a quarter-turn past where it should catch, then gives. Ethan shoulders the door open with his hip, reaches for the light switch he could find blindfolded. The fluorescents take their time. They buzz, flicker twice, settle into their pale hum over Rabbi Levi's study.

His study. He still thinks of it as Rabbi Levi's.

The radiator needs a few minutes. He turns the valve and the pipes groan through the wall, the building's old bones waking up. November now, and Jerusalem's evenings carry a chill that still surprises him. Four years and he hasn't learned to bring a jacket.

He moves through the room the way you move through a space that has become yours without your permission. Cushions from the stack in the corner, arranged in a rough circle on the floor—the velvet ones are Sera's addition, the plain cotton ones from the original set. The whiteboard wiped from last week, a ghost of Hebrew letters bleeding through the surface no matter how hard he scrubs. The green-shaded lamp is the same one, rewired twice now, the brass base dented where the rabbi once knocked it with his elbow reaching for a Talmud volume. The cat is gone—died two years ago, quietly, on her favorite stack of Tractate Berachot. Ethan keeps the stack there, undisturbed, a small monument to a creature who'd been better at stillness than any of them.

Rabbi Levi's bookshelves line the north wall, untouched except for the additions. Talmud, Zohar, a water-stained copy of Heschel that falls open to the same page every time. Ethan has added his own books—a Hebrew-English dictionary worn soft at the spine, three volumes of Buber he bought at a stall in the shuk, a collection of Yehuda Amichai's poetry Sera gave him their first anniversary. His grandmother's Hebrew Bible sits on the lowest shelf, her annotations still legible in the margins, her handwriting neater than his will ever be. He touches its spine when he enters the room, the way you touch a *mezuzah*—not thinking, just reaching.

His phone buzzes. Sera: *Noa finally down. Twins won't stop arguing about whose turn to feed the cat. Your problem when you get home.*

He types back: *Tell them both it's the cat's turn to feed them.* Then, after a moment: *Love you. Home by ten.*

The sertraline is in his pocket. He'd taken it at five, same as every day, with coffee and a handful of almonds while Ari showed him a drawing of a tank that might have been a horse. Seventy-five milligrams. The dose Dr. Yael settled on eighteen months ago. It doesn't make him faithful. The faith doesn't make him well. He is held up by both and honest about neither being sufficient alone.

The radiator clanks. That same clank.

Footsteps on the stairs. Voices.

Yael arrives first—the Ethiopian nurse, still in scrubs, surgical mask dangling from one ear. She drops onto a cushion and closes her eyes. "Fourteen hours," she says. "Don't ask." She'd come originally because her roommate dared her. Stayed because, she said, the study helped her sleep. She has a way of listening that makes the room slower, more careful—she asks the questions no one else thinks to ask, the ones that start with "But what about the person who..." She's the group's conscience. She doesn't know this.

Dmitri, the Russian programmer, appears behind her. He said he wouldn't come back after the second week. That was eleven months ago. He nods at Ethan, already pulling out his phone, then catching himself and putting it away. Progress. Dmitri argues with every text they read—not out of resistance but out of a fierce need to understand why something should matter. He once spent forty minutes questioning a single word in Psalms, and the argument that followed was the best session Ethan ever led. He carries a thermos of black tea and never shares it.

The artist—Tamar—carries her sketchpad as always, cerulean paint under her nails, a pencil tucked behind her ear that she forgets is there until it falls into her lap. She's started drawing during the study sessions, and the drawings have become part of the teaching, accumulating on the wall by the window: the group in loose charcoal, Jerusalem through the glass, a pair of hands holding a book, the radiator rendered with the attention most artists reserve for human faces. She pins last week's sketch to the board without comment. It shows the circle of cushions from above, the figures faceless, the light pooling in the center.

Moshe comes in checking his watch. His grandson has a game tonight but it doesn't start until eight-thirty. He'll make it.

They settle. The room warms. Thursday evening in Jerusalem, the muezzin's call fading as the city turns toward night.

A figure appears in the doorway.

Young. Twenty-four, maybe twenty-five. Dark hair that hasn't been cut in a while, the kind of neglect that comes from not caring rather than from style. A backpack slung over one shoulder, the canvas fraying at the straps, airline tags from JFK still dangling from the zipper. Sneakers too white and too clean for the stone streets outside—bought recently, maybe at the airport, the laces still stiff. His posture says East Coast—the slight hunch, the defensive shoulders, the way he holds himself like he's still navigating subway crowds. A sunburn on his nose, uneven, the kind you get from falling asleep outside and not meaning to. He's gripping his phone in his right hand with the desperate focus of someone holding the last solid thing in a liquid world. His eyes are red-rimmed. Not crying. Not sleeping.

He hesitates at the threshold.

"Is this -- someone said there's a class. A study thing." His accent is unmistakable. Brooklyn, or close to it. "I don't know if I'm in the right place."

"You're in the right place," Ethan says. "Come in. Sit anywhere."

The kid steps inside but doesn't sit. His eyes move over the bookshelves, the cushions, the whiteboard, the circle of strangers. He looks like he wants to bolt.

"I'm not religious," he says. "I should probably say that up front."

Dmitri snorts. Yael opens one eye.

"Noted," Ethan says. "Sit."

He sits. Cross-legged on the edge of the circle, backpack still on, as though he might need to leave quickly. He smells like airport -- recycled air and exhaustion metabolized through the skin. A tag from Ben Gurion still hangs from his backpack zipper.

"What's your name?" Ethan asks.

"Jake." A pause. "Jacob. My grandmother called me Yaakov but nobody -- I don't really go by that."

Something turns over in Ethan's chest. A rhyme, almost. The posture, the deflection, the hunger visible beneath the discomfort. The way the kid said *grandmother* without meaning to and then tried to walk it back.

"What brought you here, Jacob?"

"I don't know. My ex is Israeli. We broke up in August. I told myself I was coming to return her stuff." He gestures at the backpack. "There's a box of her books in my hostel. I haven't texted her." He laughs, a short, bewildered sound. "I don't think the books are why I'm here. But I don't know what is."

The room is quiet. Ethan waits for the recognition to settle — the familiar shape of someone arriving under false pretenses, the cover story dissolving — but it doesn't come cleanly. The kid's story isn't his story. The entry point is different, the wound is different. Not everything rhymes.

"It doesn't have to make sense yet," Tamar says quietly, not looking up from her sketchpad.

Ethan pulls a cushion closer. The room waits. He can feel the others giving the kid space, the way they've learned to over these months. Not pushing. Not retreating.

"Tell me what you remember," Ethan says.

Jacob blinks. "Remember about what?"

"Anything. A grandmother. A melody. A word in a language you almost know."

The kid's face does something complicated. The blankness cracks, just slightly.

"My grandmother used to sing something on Friday nights," he says, slowly. "I don't know the words. I was little. She'd cover her eyes."

The room is quiet.

"I can't remember the melody," Jacob says. "But I remember how it felt." His voice has changed. The bravado gone, the protective irony stripped away. What's underneath is raw and young and afraid. "It felt like the house was safe. Like nothing could get in while she was singing." He stops. Swallows. "That's stupid."

"It's not stupid," Yael says, without opening her eyes.

The room is quiet. The radiator clanks. Someone's phone buzzes in a pocket and is ignored.

Ethan nods. "That's enough. That's where we start."

He opens the book. His grandmother's Bible, the one with her annotations, the binding soft from use. He finds the page without looking, because the book falls open here on its own, the spine broken in by years of hands before his.

"Aleph," he says, writing the letter on the whiteboard. The marker squeaks. "The first letter. It makes no sound by itself."

Jacob stares. "Then what does it do?"

"It holds the space open." Ethan caps the marker. "It's the breath before the word."

The circle draws tighter. Dmitri leans forward. Yael opens both eyes. Moshe forgets his watch. Tamar's pencil moves.

Ethan begins to teach. He is not sure he is doing it right. He is not sure Rabbi Levi did it right either, or Rabbi Levi's teacher before him. The chain of transmission is not a chain — it is a series of people handing imperfect things to other people in rooms that are too cold or too hot, hoping something survives the transfer. Some nights it feels like prayer. Other nights like pretending. Tonight he can't tell which, and he decides that not knowing might be the most honest thing he has to offer.

Halfway through the passage, Jacob stands up. The backpack is already on his shoulder. "I'm sorry," he says. "I can't — I thought I could, but I can't do this."

The room goes still. Ethan watches him move toward the door and feels the old helplessness — the same helplessness Rabbi Levi must have felt, the same gap between what you offer and what someone can receive. He has no guarantee the kid will come back. No guarantee any of them will come back. Last month a woman named Rivka — not his grandmother's name, he'd checked, but close enough to make his chest hurt — had attended three sessions and then stopped answering texts. People came and went. The circle was not permanent. Nothing here was permanent.

"The door's always open," Ethan says.

Jacob pauses in the doorway. He doesn't turn around. The stairwell is right there, and the street, and the airport, and the flight back to a life that made sense.

"Thursday?" Jacob asks, without turning.

"Thursday."

Jacob goes down the stairs. The door at the bottom opens and closes. Maybe he'll come back. Maybe he won't. The room can hold either outcome.

The others leave slowly. Yael first—her shift starts at six, she says, but she squeezes Ethan's arm at the door. Dmitri next, arguing with Moshe about something in the text as they take the stairs together, their voices fading into the stairwell. Tamar last, pausing to adjust her sketch on the wall, tilting it a degree to the left, then leaving it. "Thursday," she says, and it's not a question.

Ethan is alone in the study. The cushions in their circle, the whiteboard with its indelible ghosts, the books on their shelves. The radiator clanks once, settles. He turns off the green-shaded lamp and stands in the dark for a moment, the way he used to stand in his Brooklyn apartment after lighting a single candle. The room holds the warmth of the bodies that were in it.

He picks up his grandmother's Bible from the shelf. Opens it to the first page. The *aleph* she drew in the margin—her *aleph*, leaning forward, always hurrying—catches the streetlight through the window.

Outside, Jerusalem settles into its layers of dark. Stone cooling, stars assembling over the Old City, the distant sound of traffic and prayer tangled together. Inside, the radiator clanks, loses its rhythm, finds it again.
