# Chapter 11: Gathering the Sparks

The Zohar fell open to a page about hidden light, and Ethan felt his grandmother's presence in the room.

Rabbi Levi's study. The same room, but different at night—the green-shaded lamp casting everything in the color of old money, the bookshelves taller in the shadows, the Talmud volumes on the corner shelf catching light on their cracked spines. The gray cat was asleep again, this time on the radiator, its body draped across the metal ribs like something boneless. The pipe tobacco smell was fainter tonight, replaced by tea—black, Russian-style, the metal holders clicking when the rabbi shifted his cup. The window behind his desk was dark. October pressed against the glass, and the room felt smaller for it, sealed against the world.

"You asked why you feel broken." Rabbi Levi squinted at the text, his finger following right to left. "*Shevirat HaKelim*."

Ethan shifted in his chair. His mind wanted a problem statement, not mysticism. "Rabbi, I need something practical--"

"You design products, yes? First you create space. Remove features. Simplify the interface."

"Yes, but what does that have to do with--"

"*Tzimtzum*." The rabbi's arthritic fingers struggled with the heavy Zohar. "Divine contraction. God had to make room for creation."

Ethan helped separate the stuck pages, feeling their texture. Rough, almost alive. A margin note in faded brown ink caught his eye: "The breaking is the beginning."

"So depression is... withdrawal?" Heat flared in Ethan's chest. "Because my psychiatrist says it's a serotonin deficiency. My therapist says it's unprocessed grief. And you're telling me it's--what--God making room?"

"Maybe your depression is exactly that. A contraction. The divine making space inside you for something that hasn't arrived yet."

The words landed wrong. Not gently wrong—offensively wrong. Ethan felt it first as heat in his hands, then pressure behind his eyes. He'd come here for something real. He'd come here because his apartment was quiet and the sertraline wasn't enough and he needed a person in a room to look at him and say something that wasn't a metaphor. And the rabbi was giving him metaphors.

"That's—" He stopped. Tried again. "Rabbi, with respect. I have a clinical diagnosis. I have medication. I have a therapist who bills my insurance two hundred dollars an hour to help me not—" He gestured vaguely. "Not disappear."

"I understand—"

"Do you? Because what I'm hearing is that my serotonin deficiency is actually a spiritual event. That God needed to hollow me out to pour something in." His voice was rising. He heard it and couldn't stop it. "My psychiatrist says it's chemistry. My therapist says it's grief. And you're telling me it's—what—divine interior decorating?"

"I'm saying it can be all three—"

"Don't," Ethan said. "Don't spiritualize my illness."

"I'm not--"

"Yes, you are. You're sitting there telling me that the thing that makes me want to die is God clearing his throat. That's not a story, Rabbi. That's an insult."

Rabbi Levi's hand stopped on the page. He didn't look away. "You're right."

"What?"

"That was a bad sentence. A lazy one." He rubbed his eyes. "I'm an old man who reads too much. Sometimes the framework gets ahead of the person in front of me. I apologize."

The apology was so plain it disarmed him more than the theology had. He sat back. His pulse was still going.

"I'm telling you one story about it," the rabbi continued, quieter now. "Your psychiatrist has another. Your therapist a third. Depression is too big for one story. I shouldn't have made it sound like mine was better."

"But you're implying it's spiritual. That if I just--meditate, or study enough Zohar--"

"I'm implying nothing of the sort. You take your medication?"

"Every morning."

"Good. Keep taking it. What I'm offering is another layer. Not a replacement. Some of my students find it helps. Others find it's mystical escapism. A former student told me the Kabbalah was just my way of aestheticizing suffering." He paused. "She wasn't entirely wrong."

"The vessels meant to contain divine light," the rabbi said, turning the page. "They shattered."

"They weren't strong enough?"

"They were exactly as strong as needed. Breaking wasn't failure--"

"How is this supposed to help?" Ethan's voice came out louder than he meant. "I can barely get out of bed some mornings, and you're talking about vessels. Shattered vessels, hidden light, divine contraction--it's poetry, Rabbi. It's beautiful poetry. And it doesn't change a single thing about my life."

The rabbi didn't answer.

Ethan stood. The chair scraped against the floor. "This is exactly what I was afraid of. Beautiful bullshit dressed up in ancient languages."

He picked up his jacket from the back of the chair. The rabbi watched him but said nothing. Ethan crossed the small study in four steps and reached the doorway.

"I had a student," Rabbi Levi said behind him. "Twenty years ago. Came to me the same way you did. Angry, depressed, looking for answers I don't have."

Ethan stopped but didn't turn around.

"He said the Kabbalah was nonsense. He was probably right. But he kept coming back on Tuesdays because his apartment was very quiet and this room was not. That's not theology. That's a man who needed a place to sit that wasn't his own head."

Ethan stood in the doorway, jacket half on.

"I can't fix you. The Zohar can't fix you. But it's Tuesday, and your apartment is quiet. So."

The chair was still warm when Ethan sat back down. Neither of them acknowledged what had just happened.

"Your grandmother understood broken vessels." The rabbi's eyes sharpened. "Every survivor does."

Coffee stains marked the paper from previous lessons. Real stains on real paper, not pristine PDFs. "I still don't see how this helps me feel less empty."

"Every late-night scroll through your phone--you're searching for something. But looking in the wrong place."

The rabbi stood with effort, knees cracking, and pulled down a book that shed leather flakes. "Rivka brought you here once."

"When?" But memory flickered. It came in pieces—dark wood paneling, a hallway that smelled of furniture polish and old carpet. His grandmother's hand wrapped around his, her grip firm, her rings cold against his fingers. She smelled of powder and rosewater and the butterscotch candy she always carried. He was seven, in his school clothes, and the hallway was longer than any hallway should be, lined with photographs of bearded men he didn't know. She knelt to his level outside a door with a brass *mezuzah*. "*Shayna punim*, this is important." Her eyes were serious in a way he'd never seen directed at him before—the seriousness she reserved for phone calls in Yiddish, for the numbers on her arm she covered with long sleeves even in July. "This man is my teacher. You will sit quietly and listen." He'd nodded, not understanding, and she'd opened the door to a room that smelled exactly like this one—books and pipe smoke and something else, cedar or sandalwood, the scent of rooms where time moved differently.

"You were seven. She understood gathering sparks in darkness."

The rabbi opened to a marked page, binding cracking. "Try reading this."

Ethan struggled with the Hebrew. "Or... Or Ha..."

"Or HaGanuz. Hidden Light."

"In me?" Ethan laughed, bitter. "I eat bacon. I work on Saturdays. I haven't been to synagogue since my bar mitzvah."

"And yet you're here."

"Because I'm desperate. Not because I'm... divinely sparked or whatever."

"What's the difference?" Rabbi Levi studied his face. "Your depression: judgment without mercy. But breaking is the beginning of repair."

"That's a beautiful sentence," Ethan said. "It doesn't make me less depressed."

"No. It doesn't. The sertraline will do more for your serotonin than ten thousand pages of Zohar. Don't let anyone, including me, tell you otherwise."

Ethan's throat tightened. "My grandmother used to say that. About breaking. I never understood." His voice cracked. "What if I'm too broken to repair?"

"Start with candles. See what happens."

Rabbi Levi wrote out Hebrew letters, slowly, painfully. His handwriting was barely legible. "Study this before sleep. Friday, light *Shabbat* candles."

"I don't know the prayers."

"So learn. Or don't. The light doesn't care about perfect pronunciation."

Walking home, Ethan felt different. Not fixed—just oriented, the way a compass needle settles after spinning. The October air was sharp in his lungs, and he walked slower than usual, his feet finding a rhythm he didn't set. The city moved around him in its usual Tuesday-night current—couples heading to dinner, a man arguing into his phone, the 7 train rattling over the intersection. All of it continuing, indifferent to whatever had just happened in that room.

He carried the rabbi's photocopied Hebrew in his coat pocket. The paper was already softening at the folds.

In his apartment, behind old cables and dust in the back of his hall closet: his grandmother's candlesticks. He hadn't looked at them since moving in. They were wrapped in newspaper from 2019, ink transferring to his fingers as he unwrapped them. Tarnished green-black at the joints. One was dented, real damage on real brass—dropped once, maybe, or carried too roughly in the flight from somewhere to somewhere else.

He set them next to his coffee maker. Sacred beside mundane. The coffee maker hummed. The candlesticks stood in silence.

That night he studied Rabbi Levi's barely legible Hebrew, guessing half the letters. The passage was about gathering sparks. Or maybe lifting fallen things. His Hebrew wasn't good enough to be sure.

He tried to light a practice candle. The match broke, cheap wood snapping between his fingers. The second burned too fast, heat biting his fingertips. The third lit but wouldn't catch. The wick was buried in wax from years of neglect, a tomb of accumulated Friday nights never observed.

He dug it out with a kitchen knife, waxy residue building under his fingernails. The knife scraped against brass, a sound that made him wince—metal on metal, the sound of forcing something open that had sealed itself shut. Finally, a blackened thread of wick exposed. The match trembled in his hand.

Contact. A flame, tiny, fragile. It guttered, almost died—then steadied.

The apartment was quiet around the flame. The refrigerator's hum, the building's settling, the distant complaint of a neighbor's television through the wall—all of it fell away. The light made a small warm circle on the counter, catching the brass of the candlestick, the kitchen tiles, the edge of his coffee mug. Such a small thing. Such an ordinary miracle—a wick and wax and a struck match, the same technology Abraham would have known.

For a moment, watching that small light hold against the darkness, he thought about his grandmother. Her life, not her death. Friday nights when he was small, the way she'd cover her eyes and circle her hands three times over the flames, gathering the light toward her face. The way the candlelight softened the lines around her mouth and made her look like the woman in the photographs—young, fierce, newly arrived in a country that didn't know what to do with her. Had she felt this same uncertainty, this fraudulence? Or had faith come easier to someone who'd seen real darkness?

No. He knew the answer. Faith had come harder to her. That was the point. She'd lit candles on Friday nights in a displaced persons camp in Austria. She'd lit them in a shared apartment in Flatbush with a husband who couldn't sleep without screaming. She'd lit them after every reason not to, and the not-quitting was the faith, not the flame itself.

He picked up his phone to text Rabbi Levi, typed "I think I'm beginning to--" then deleted it. Beginning to what? He didn't know. The candle burned. That was all he had.

The flame lasted eleven minutes. He timed it on his phone, which defeated the purpose, probably. When it went out, the apartment was darker than before. His eyes had adjusted to the light and now had to adjust back. The afterimage lingered—a small orange ghost floating in his vision, refusing to fade.

He sat at the counter in the dark for a while. The wax smell hung in the air—warm, faintly sweet, carrying something he couldn't name. Friday was two days away and he didn't know if he'd light them again. But the candlestick was still warm under his palm, and he didn't move his hand.
