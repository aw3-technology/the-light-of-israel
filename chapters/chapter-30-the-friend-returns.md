# Chapter 30: The Friend Returns

Aaron Gold stood in Ethan's Jerusalem kitchen like an argument waiting to happen.

Tuesday morning, July. 7:20 AM. The doorbell rang. Aaron with a backpack, sweat stains spreading dark across his gray t-shirt from the walk up. The familiar cologne he'd worn since college mixed with travel sweat, Jerusalem dust, the sourness of a man who'd been rehearsing apologies on the plane. A year since they'd really talked.

"I should have called."

"Yeah."

Aaron looked exhausted. Deeper than jet lag. *He's lost weight. And when did he start biting his nails?* New lines around his eyes. Two years of separation visible in the slight stoop of his shoulders, the defensive way he held himself in the doorway.

Sera appeared with Lev on her hip, Ari at her feet. The kitchen smelled of cardamom from the coffee she'd been making, layered over breakfast: eggs, za'atar, honey cake. She studied Aaron protectively, hand tightening on Lev's back, then softened.

"You must be Aaron. Ethan's mentioned you."

"Good things?"

"Things."

Coffee grounds from breakfast scattered on the counter. Toy blocks in primary colors across the tile floor. The sound of Jerusalem morning filtered through open windows: Arabic calls from the neighboring mosque, church bells in the distance, someone's rooster that didn't understand urban living. Ethan made coffee on the Italian machine from the *shuk*, the hiss of steam loud in the awkward silence.

Baby Ari crawled toward Aaron, the soft shuffle of palms and knees on tile, and seized his finger. Aaron froze mid-reach for his coffee, staring at the tiny hand. Warm, impossibly soft, trusting. His hand trembled slightly.

"Shit." Aaron's voice cracked. "Sorry. Just—my grandmother used to..." He swallowed. "She used to say babies choose their angels before birth. Said that's why they stare at nothing—they're saying goodbye to heaven." He looked embarrassed. "I don't even believe this crap, but she did."

Ethan stared. "You never mentioned your grandmother believed in angels."

Aaron gently freed his finger. "She survived Auschwitz by talking to them. I thought it was trauma. Maybe trauma is just—" He stopped. "I don't know what trauma is."

Ari gurgled, crawled away.

"You look different," Aaron said, clearing his throat. A nervous habit from law school.

"The water."

Neither smiled. The tension between them had texture, rough and uncertain.

"How did you find me?"

"Your mother. Made me promise to check you're eating."

"And are you? Here to talk sense?"

"I don't know why I'm here." Aaron gripped his coffee cup until his knuckles went white, the ceramic hot enough to hurt but he didn't let go. "No. I'm seeing someone. Six months. Her name is Layla."

The way he said her name.

"She's Palestinian."

The word sat between them.

"Columbia protest. Four months ago." Aaron's voice went quiet. "She asked me something: 'What kind of ally shows up to protests but can't name his great-grandparents? Knows every Palestinian village destroyed but not his own family's shtetl?'"

He paused.

"Her parents were worse. 'What kind of Jew defines himself only by what he opposes?'"

Ethan said nothing. The twins built towers. Sera moved through the kitchen with a soldier's instinct for when to hold ground and when to withdraw.

"I used Palestine to—" Aaron stopped, rubbed his face. "Fuck. I used Palestine to run from—"

Lev started crying from the other room. Sera's footsteps, then quiet.

"—from being Jewish," Aaron finished quietly.

"And I—" Ethan's phone buzzed. He ignored it. "I used Israel to—" The phone buzzed again. "Shit, sorry." He glanced at it. Spam call. "I used Israel to run from being human."

They sat with that. The twins babbled in Hebrew-English mix, oblivious. Somewhere, a door slammed. The coffee grew cold between them.

"Go walk," Sera suggested, her hand briefly on Ethan's shoulder. "Show him the market."

But first, Aaron said he needed to show Ethan something. He pulled out his phone, scrolled to a video. "Layla filmed this. Two months ago. Her grandmother's village."

On the small screen: Aaron standing in an olive grove somewhere in the West Bank, surrounded by Palestinian extended family. An old woman—Layla's grandmother—held his face between her palms, studying him. Her Arabic came through tinny speakers, and Layla's voice translated off-camera:

"She says you have sad eyes. She says you carry your dead like we carry ours."

Aaron on the screen stiffened, then softened. "Tell her—tell her I'm learning."

The grandmother spoke again, longer this time, her voice rising and falling. Layla translated: "She says her neighbor in 1947 was a Jewish baker named Shlomo. Every Friday he brought challah. She still remembers the taste. She asks if you know how to make challah."

Aaron on the screen laughed—a startled, genuine sound. "No. But I can learn that too."

The grandmother patted his cheek, said something that made everyone laugh. Layla's translation came through the laughter: "She says you're too skinny for a Jew. She's going to feed you."

The video ended. Aaron pocketed his phone. His hands were shaking slightly.

"Her grandmother fed me for three hours," Aaron said. "Hummus, lamb, rice, more hummus. Her uncle played oud. Her cousins argued about football." He rubbed his face. "The grandmother kept pushing more food. I kept accepting. By the third plate something in my chest just--" He made a gesture like something cracking open. "Same food. Same grandmother who won't let you leave the table."

"Aaron—"

"I know it's more complicated than that. Layla reminds me every day. But that dinner--" He rubbed his face. "That sounds like bullshit. It felt like truth."

*Machane Yehuda* at mid-morning. The assault of smells: cumin, coriander, fresh pita, overripe fruit, diesel from delivery trucks. Vendors shouting in Hebrew-Arabic-English chaos. Tourists with cameras blocking narrow passages. Locals with wheeled carts navigating around them with practiced irritation.

Aaron's eyes darted everywhere, overwhelmed. Everyone arguing about tomatoes as though the fate of nations hung on ripeness. The vendor's hands gesturing wildly, the customer's voice rising to match. Both somehow enjoying the conflict.

They walked. Aaron talked about Layla. She was a veterinarian, specialized in large animals, horses mostly. She'd grown up in Ramallah but studied at Cornell, seven years in Ithaca she described as "freezing and formative." She played competitive backgammon. Read mystery novels in three languages. Had a complicated relationship with her older sister, who'd married a Hamas official's nephew and stopped speaking to Layla after she started dating Aaron.

"She makes this thing," Aaron said, hands gesturing, "with eggplant and pomegranate molasses. I don't even like eggplant, but hers." He shook his head. "Her father still won't eat with me. Perches in the next room during dinner. But her mother puts out an extra plate anyway. Just in case."

"What does she do for fun? Besides backgammon?"

"She runs. Seriously runs. Marathons. She did the Bethlehem marathon last year—there's a marathon in Bethlehem, did you know that?—and her route took her past the separation wall three times. She said it was the only time running felt political." Aaron laughed. "She's also terrible at cooking rice. Catastrophically bad. Burns it every time. The vet who can deliver a breech foal at midnight cannot make rice."

"Her grandmother from Bethlehem had a music box," Aaron said then, quieter. "Plays '*Hatikvah*.' I asked why. She said, 'A Jewish neighbor gave it to me in 1948, before we left. She said, "Keep our song safe until we can share it again."' Sixty years, Layla's family kept that music box."

He rubbed his face. "When I heard it playing, I started crying. Layla said, 'This is what we've been trying to tell you—we're not against your music. We just want ours played too.'"

"She made me realize." He paused, still working it out. "I was using justice to avoid figuring out who I actually was."

German Colony cafe. The air conditioning too cold after the market's heat. Smell of coffee competing with cigarette smoke from the patio. Aaron tried to order in Hebrew. "*Ani rotzeh*..." then gave up, switched to English. "Coffee. Please." The waiter answered in rapid Hebrew, then switched back with visible annoyance. Aaron's face reddened.

"Layla's family asked: what kind of Jew are you? They assumed I'd stay a Jew. Just asked what kind."

"And?"

"The wrong kind. Knows Palestinian history but not Jewish history. Can name every village destroyed in '48 but not one Yiddish poet."

Aaron was sweating, drops visible on his forehead despite the air conditioning. Not from heat. His hands shook slightly as he lifted his coffee cup. It was cold. He drank it anyway.

"After October 7th, I posted about Gaza immediately. You disappeared. I thought you'd chosen tribe over truth."

"I thought you'd chosen politics over people."

"Maybe we both did."

Aaron swallowed hard. "She says Palestinian suffering is real. Jewish fear is real. Both." He paused. "I mean, that's what she says. I'm still... I'm still figuring out what I think."

"That's it?"

Aaron's jaw tightened. "Don't mock this."

"You protested at synagogues, Aaron. After October 7th. While—"

"I know what I did."

"—while Jews were mourning."

"While Palestinians were dying in Gaza. Were being—"

"Both. Both were fucking happening."

Ethan leaned forward. "You want to know what changed me? It wasn't some big revelation. It was holding my grandmother's prayer book and realizing I'd inherited a whole civilization I'd never bothered to... to unwrap, you know? Being Jewish wasn't just about--" He gestured vaguely. "What happened to us. It was what we built. Despite everything. Languages, melodies, three thousand years of arguing about God."

Aaron started to interrupt.

"Let me finish. You can't outsource that. You can't protest your way into belonging somewhere else and call it justice."

Aaron sat back. "Jesus."

"Yeah. And I got tired of being ashamed of that. Tired of apologizing for taking up space in my own story."

The waiter brought more coffee, slammed it down. Steam rose between them, then dissipated. At the next table, someone's phone rang. "*Hatikvah*" as a ringtone. Aaron winced.

"After October 7th, when I saw your posts about vigils, about suddenly being—"

"Suddenly being what?"

"Jewish. I thought you'd been brainwashed."

"You posted about Gaza hours after the massacre."

"Because I knew—" Aaron's voice cracked. "I knew what would come next. And I was right."

"You were right. Congratulations." He heard himself, hated the bitterness.

"Now I light candles Friday night."

"Layla taught you?"

Aaron shifted. "She bought them. I still... I still read the blessing off my phone. Every week." He rubbed the bridge of his nose. "She said I had to love myself to love her. For the record, that's how she put it. Sounds better when she says it."

"Stop."

"Stop what? Being angry? You disappeared because I went to synagogue."

"You disappeared to Jerusalem. You're raising Jewish kids in a—" Aaron stopped. His face went pale. "I don't want to fight like that."

"Say it."

Aaron's hands started shaking. "I can't—"

"Say what you came here to say."

"In an apartheid state." The words came out jagged and wrong-shaped.

Ethan set down his coffee so hard it sloshed. The café went quiet around them. Other patrons glanced over.

His first impulse was to throw Aaron out. The word *apartheid* reduced his children, his home, his entire new life to a political slogan. But he stayed in the chair. Maybe because of Mahmoud the tile-setter, forty years building the Jewish Quarter and now called a security risk by the man whose apartment he was tiling. Maybe the checkpoint near Be'eri where Samira's students passed through turnstiles every morning, carrying their notebooks as armor. Maybe just honesty.

"You know what that word does to me," Ethan said, his voice low and dangerous. "It erases everything—the history, the survival, three thousand years of—"

"And you know what ignoring it does to them," Aaron said, not backing down. "To the people who live twenty minutes from here and can't vote, can't build, can't—"

"It's not that simple."

"No. It's not. But you know it's not nothing either."

They stared at each other. Ethan felt a seal break he'd been holding shut since he signed the *aliyah* forms. The knowledge he carried privately, the thing he couldn't say at *Shabbat* dinner or at the construction site or to his parents: he'd chosen to build his life in a place that was simultaneously his ancestral homeland and someone else's ongoing displacement. Being a Zionist -- and he was one, he could say that now, at least to himself -- meant living inside a contradiction no side could resolve. *I believe in this country's right to exist. I also see what it costs. Both true. Neither cancels the other.*

"I'm a Zionist," Ethan said. The word tasted like iron. "I hate that word and I am it. I believe this state has a right to exist. I also know." His voice caught. "I know that right came at a price that other people are still paying. And I don't know how to hold both of those things. But I'm trying. Which is more than your Columbia friends are doing."

Aaron was quiet for a long time. "That's the first honest thing you've said to me in two years."

"It's the first honest thing I've said to myself."

The silence held. Neither of them walked out.

*Two hours later.*

Ethan found Aaron sitting on a bench outside the King David Hotel, staring at his phone. The American Colony had been full.

"Sera says I'm an asshole." What she'd actually said was: "Go find him before I lose respect for both of you." She'd pushed him toward the door. "Twenty years of friendship and you're going to let it die over one stupid word? Move."

Aaron looked up, eyes red. "She's right."

"She also says you're an asshole."

"Also right."

Ethan dropped onto the bench beside him, leaving space between them.

A street cleaner pushed his cart past, bristles scraping stone.

"So what do we do?" Aaron picked at a scab on his knuckle. "Do we just pretend that didn't happen? Because I've been sitting here for two hours and I don't—I can't figure out if we're still—" He waved his hand at the space between them. "This."

"I don't know."

"Great. Cool. Two Jews who can't figure out how to talk to each other. Really breaking the mold." He laughed, bitter and short. "Layla would have a field day."

"Don't."

"Sorry. I just—I default to jokes when I'm—" He rubbed his eyes. "I'm scared I lost you, man. And I'm scared that's my fault. And I'm scared it's yours. And I'm scared that whose fault it is doesn't even matter."

They walked toward the Old City.

That evening, Aaron stayed for dinner. Ethan lit candles, still reading the blessing from a card. Aaron watched, shifting his weight.

"I brought something." Aaron pulled out two children's books, one English, one Arabic. "From Layla. For the twins."

"Bridge stories. Subtle."

"She says—" Aaron shrugged. "She says kids here need them. I don't know. Maybe they do."

On the balcony. Jerusalem's Friday night. Religious neighborhoods dark, secular bars loud. The segregation visible even from here.

"I never understood '*L'shanah haba'ah b'Yerushalayim*,'" Aaron said. A siren wailed in the distance. "Now I'm here and I still—"

"Still don't," Ethan finished.

"Yeah." Aaron pulled at his shirt collar. "Maybe that's... I don't know. Maybe that's the point? Not understanding. Just—"

"Just being here anyway."

Below, a muezzin's call, Hebrew music, Russian conversation. The sounds didn't blend.

"I'm not making *aliyah*."

"I didn't ask you to."

"I think you're wrong."

"I know."

"But I missed you."

"Yeah. Me too."

Aaron shouldered his backpack. Heavy. He'd overpacked, as always. "Layla wants to visit her grandmother's village. What's left. She wants me to come."

"Will you go with her?"

"If she comes to Lithuania with me. To see where my grandfather's from."

"Trading ruins."

"Grief tourism with a purpose."

They embraced at the door. Brief. Careful. Real.

"Tell her thanks for the books."

Aaron paused at the elevator, then turned back. "We're okay?"

"We're working on it."

"But not—not enemies."

"No. Not enemies."

After Aaron left, Sera came out. "You're still friends?"

"I don't know. Maybe."

"Jews," she said, already turning back toward the kitchen.

"What?"

"You're Jews. You fight like family. Terrible and loud and then you eat together." She started running water for the dishes. "It's fine. Clean up the coffee he spilled on my table."

Inside, the twins slept. The books Aaron brought lay on the coffee table. Ethan picked up the Arabic one, couldn't read a word.

From the bedroom, Lev stirred. A soft cry, then quiet.
