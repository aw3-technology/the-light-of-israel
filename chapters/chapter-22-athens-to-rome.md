# Chapter 22: Athens to Rome

November light hit the Parthenon and Ethan understood, in his body before his mind, why some Jews had once chosen beauty over truth.

The thought came unbidden as he climbed marble steps worn smooth by centuries. His legs shook, not from exertion but from temptation. How easy it would be to dissolve into this universal embrace, to let Athens wash the weight of Jerusalem from his shoulders.

His phone showed seventeen unread messages from Sera. "Landed safe?" and "Remember to eat" and finally just a heart emoji sent at the moment his plane crossed from Asia to Europe. Below those, a cascade of Slack notifications from Nolan, still cc'ing him on standup threads, as if Ethan might weigh in from the Acropolis. He swiped them away, but his thumb hesitated over one: "Roadmap deck due Monday, ping me?" The old muscle memory of urgency. He locked the phone.

The taxi from the airport had crawled through Syngrou Avenue, past shuttered kiosks and apartment blocks whose balconies sagged under laundry and satellite dishes. The driver had been Ethiopian. "First time in Athens?"

"Yes."

"You coming from Israel." Not a question. "I can tell. Jews from Israel carry Jerusalem in their shoulders."

Now, standing before the Parthenon's columns, Ethan felt those shoulders. Weighted by weeks in the Old City, marked by *tefillin* straps.

"You're early."

The man who stepped out from behind a column was in his sixties, silver-haired, wearing a linen shirt that hung off him as if he'd been larger once. Professor Yannis Levy. "Rabbi Levi said you'd be at the university at three."

"Strikes. Students protesting. Someone spray-painted my office door after October 7th. 'Zionists Out.' I left it." He shrugged. "Easier than repainting."

They took the metro from Monastiraki. On the platform, a busker played bouzouki badly, and a stray dog with a clipped ear lay against the tiled wall, indifferent to everything. Near the university, students blocked the main gate, banners demanding higher adjunct pay and an end to austerity cuts. A police van across the street, two officers leaning against it, drinking frappes.

"They've been at it since September," Yannis said. "Before October 7th it was budget cuts. After, someone added Palestine to the list. Now it's about everything." He sighed. "A student I'd supervised for two years — good student, sharp — told me she couldn't work with a Zionist. I said, 'I'm a Greek citizen who happens to be Jewish.' She said that was the same thing." He adjusted his glasses. "I didn't have an answer for that."

They walked past the strike. Yannis led him up toward the Acropolis, pointing toward the modern city below. "You know what happened here twenty-three hundred years ago? Jewish boys reversed their circumcisions to compete naked in the gymnasiums." He adjusted his glasses. "Imagine wanting to belong so badly you'd cut yourself twice."

"Some did. Not all."

"The ones who didn't became Maccabees." He wiped sweat from his forehead with a handkerchief, gestured at the Parthenon. "But look. Greek carries the New Testament. The Talmud borrows Greek logic." A dry laugh. "We keep winning arguments with their tools."

The walk down to Plaka took them through streets that narrowed and bent. At a corner, Ethan stopped. A bar with its doors propped open, amber light spilling across a marble step, a bartender wiping glasses behind a zinc counter while two men argued over backgammon. It hit him in the sternum. Teddy's on Berry Street, a Thursday, the regulars leaning into their drinks. Brooklyn muscle memory triggered by a bar in Plaka.

"You know this place?" Yannis asked.

"No. It just—reminded me of somewhere."

The synagogue in Plaka, tucked between a souvenir shop and a restaurant. The facade unmarked except for a small bronze plaque worn smooth.

"After October 7th, we posted security," Yannis said, nodding at two men smoking outside whose eyes tracked every passerby. "Though what two old men could do."

Inside, smaller than Jerusalem's grand spaces. The memorial wall hit first. Names organized by family, by neighborhood, by the efficiency that removed 83% of Greek Jewry.

"Sixty-seven thousand. We count them every *Yom HaShoah*. Youngest eight, oldest ninety-three." He gestured at the small sanctuary. "Maybe five thousand Jews in all Greece now. We barely make a *minyan* some mornings. But we gather. We cling to the edges."

Later, climbing stairs to Yannis's apartment: "My daughter Anna will be there. PhD in philosophy. She'll challenge you. It's her love language."

A young woman looked up from her laptop, closing it without hurry. She drank from a mug that read 'Socrates Died For This Shit' in Greek, and studied him the way she might study a footnote that didn't belong.

"You came from Jerusalem?" she asked.

"Yes."

"And?"

He waited for the question behind the question, but she just shrugged and opened her laptop again, though he noticed she wasn't typing.

They ate, and Anna directed the conversation toward plumbing schedules and university gossip. Mundane things, said in a way that felt deliberate. But then Ethan's phone pinged. Sera's sunset photo from Jerusalem. Anna glanced at it sideways. Ethan turned the phone face-down on the table.

"Your girlfriend?"

"Someone I met in Jerusalem. She lost three friends at Nova."

Anna's fork paused. "Everyone lost someone at Nova." She said it flatly, correcting a fact, but her hand moved to her throat.

"I cried at the Wall," Ethan said, not sure why he was volunteering it. "You probably think that's ridiculous."

"I don't think anything about it." She tore bread. "My father cries there too. Started after October 7th. Before that he was the most rational man in Greece." A slight roughness in her voice. "He wants me to light candles now. I wrote my dissertation on patriarchal monotheism."

"That must make *Shabbat* dinners interesting."

She almost smiled. Caught herself. "The rules were we were Greek. Universal. Then suddenly we're Jews again? Because Hamas decided?"

"I didn't choose this either," Ethan said. "I was fine not being Jewish. But fine isn't the same as—" He stopped. He didn't have the words for what it wasn't the same as.

Ethan took candlesticks from his bag, centered them between wine and bread. Anna stared like they were unwanted evidence, then looked away, then back.

"My grandmother lit candles every Friday for seventy years. Never missed once." He struck a match. His hand shook slightly. "I don't totally understand why I do this. She just—it felt important to her. So."

The flames caught, small but insistent.

"So it's obligation?"

"Maybe. Or habit. Or—" He watched the flames. "I don't know what to call it."

Anna laughed, short and sharp. "You're funnier than most Americans who find themselves."

"I haven't found anything. I'm just... here. Doing this." He gestured at the candles. "That's all I've got."

Later, Yannis walked him to his hotel. "Forty years I taught Plato, Aristotle," he said, nodding at a bookshop window where philosophy titles caught the streetlight. "Beautiful systems. Everything in its place. Then October 7th, and I'm watching the news, and Aristotle has nothing to say to me. Not one word." He stopped at the hotel entrance. "Anna thinks I went crazy. Maybe. But my crazy has candles and my sane had nothing."

In his room, Ethan set candlesticks on the desk. Friday night. He lit them without the blessing. His mouth shaped words he didn't yet know; the body remembered first.

The flames reflected against Athens night.

His phone rang. Anna.

"I might... maybe I'll try tomorrow. The service." A long pause. "Don't make a big deal if I show up. And don't make a big deal if I don't."

"Okay."

"Those cookies you brought." Her voice was different now, smaller. "My grandmother used to—" A long pause. Breathing. "Never mind. It's stupid."

"It's not."

"You don't know what I was going to say."

"You're right."

Another pause. Then she hung up without saying goodbye. The candles burned, small but stubborn against the Athens dark.

---

Saturday evening. The synagogue fuller than expected. Thirteen, enough. Anna in the back row, arms crossed tight against her chest, but there. During *Lecha Dodi*, her lips moved slightly, remembering words from somewhere.

At *kiddush*, she took a cookie, bit carefully. When Yannis introduced Ethan as "our visitor from Jerusalem," she looked down at her hands.

"Not terrible," she said after, helping stack books. "Though the old rabbi's voice..."

"Cracks on the high notes?"

"Yeah." She shrugged, then was quiet for a long moment. "You leave tomorrow?"

"Rome. Then Berlin."

"Collecting cities." She handed him the tin without looking at him. "Whatever. Take these to Rome, I guess."

Outside, the Parthenon blazed against the night sky. Perfect columns, floodlit marble.

Behind him, the small synagogue went dark except for the *ner tamid*.

He walked back to the hotel. The freedom of being nobody, walking through a city where no one cared if he was Jewish or American, where he was just a man in the evening air. Anna was right to resist. And he was walking away from it anyway.

---

The flight to Fiumicino was two hours over the Adriatic. Ethan watched the water from a window seat, Anna's cookies in his carry-on, the candlesticks wrapped in a dirty shirt. He pulled out his notebook and looked at what he'd written so far. Jerusalem, Tel Aviv, the Dead Sea, Athens. A list. He was making a list of places where Jews had survived, collecting them like stamps. The recognition made him close the notebook. Anna had called it. *Collecting cities.* He dozed, and when he woke the plane was banking over the Tiber, brown and slow, threading through a city that had swallowed every empire that tried to own it.

---

Rain was coming. Ethan could smell it in the Forum before he saw the Arch of Titus, and when he did see it, two thousand years collapsed into now.

November rain cleared the tourists, leaving him alone with the monument. Marble the color of old bone. Inside the arch, Roman soldiers carrying the Temple's golden menorah, seven branches as tall as men. A crack ran through the marble beneath the carved menorah, splitting a soldier's arm from his shield. SPQR above. Below, barely visible, modern Hebrew scratched: *Am Yisrael Chai*. The people of Israel live.

He stood where Jewish slaves had been marched through. His chest tightened, ribs pressing against a pressure that wanted out. The easy Jerusalem tears were gone. This was heavier.

"We don't walk under it."

The voice came from behind him. American accent wrestling with Italian intonations. A group of Israeli tourists, their guide pointing at the arch with casual authority.

"Jews don't walk under the Arch of Titus. Two thousand years. We walk around." He gestured at teenagers draped in Israeli flags. "They thought they destroyed us. We're here. Rome's a tourist site."

One teenager started humming *Hatikvah*. The others joined. Water streamed down Ethan's face. Rain or tears, impossible to distinguish.

"You're Jewish." Not a question. The guide had approached. "First time?"

"First time really understanding what it means."

The guide, Daniel from Queens, nodded. "There's someone you should meet. Michele Della Rocca. Family here since before the destruction. Tell him Daniel sent you."

He walked from the Forum toward the ghetto along Via del Teatro di Marcello, where the Roman theater rose up, half-consumed by medieval apartments built into its arches. The rain had stopped. Cobblestones dried in patches. The city didn't pause for its own history. Ruins weren't ruins here — they were retaining walls for apartment buildings, foundations for restaurants, shortcuts for commuters.

Via del Portico d'Ottavia still bore the ghetto's shape, compressed by centuries of forced density. But it was also just a neighborhood. A woman shaking a tablecloth from a second-floor window. Two boys kicking a soccer ball against the wall of the Tempio Maggiore. A kosher bakery with its door propped open, the smell of warm *challah* drifting alongside exhaust from a double-parked delivery van. The Great Synagogue's aluminum dome caught late sun. A plaque on the corner marked the October 1943 roundup, but someone had parked a scooter in front of it. A flyer for a DJ night was taped to the lamppost above.

Piperno occupied the corner, same location since 1860. Wall photographs showed the restaurant boarded up in 1938, American GIs eating there in 1944.

"Daniel called. Said you stood at the arch and..." Michele emerged from the kitchen, flour under his fingernails, oil burns mapping years at the fryer. Roma tattoo under his rolled sleeve. "Ah, you did more than weep. I can see it."

"I don't know what I—"

"Doesn't matter. Sit. You need to eat."

*Carciofi* arrived, artichokes deep-fried until they opened into bronze flowers. "Oil must be exact," Michele said. "We've made these since... eh, who knows? Before your Jesus, before their Christ."

Ethan bit into the artichoke. The texture was wrong. Too tender, almost meaty. "This doesn't taste like..."

"What you expected? Good. Nothing here is what you expect." A crash of plates from the kitchen. Michele paused, listening, then relaxed. "Giulia. Still breaking things at twenty-six."

"But the destruction—the Temple—"

"Yes, they destroyed it. Carried the treasures through that arch. You know where those treasures are? The Vatican maybe. Or the Tiber—fell in during some riot." He shrugged. "But the real treasure got out."

Ethan set down his fork. "But what if it didn't? What if we're just telling ourselves stories to make exile feel like choice?" The words came out sharper than he intended. "I stood at that arch today and all I felt was... loss. Not triumph. Not survival. Just two thousand years of loss."

Michele wiped his hands on his apron. "You want to know something I don't tell the tourists? Sometimes I think we stay because we're too scared to leave." He paused. "But then someone like you walks in, crying at the arch, and I think: no, we stay because someone has to."

"Tomorrow, *Shabbat*. You come. Tonight we cook."

Ethan hesitated. He almost said no. Almost walked back to his hotel to sit alone with the weight of the arch still in his chest.

But Michele was already handing him an apron.

That evening, Michele's grandmother held court at table seven. Ninety-three, sharp eyes, hands that moved when she talked. She'd survived October 16, 1943, hidden in the hospital on Tiber Island.

"I was twenty," she said in practiced English. "Pretty. My Samuel fixed watches. The Germans came at 5:30 AM, *Shabbat* morning. Took over a thousand. Sixteen came back. Not Samuel."

She grabbed Ethan's hand, fingers dry and strong. "You're searching. All young Jews who come here are." Her voice changed, became smaller. "Sometimes I think we stayed out of stubbornness. Pure spite. The Romans said we'd disappear, so we didn't. But what does that prove? That we were right? Or that we were too stupid to know when we were beaten?"

Ethan resisted even this. "What if persistence isn't enough? What if we're just stubborn survivors clinging to a tradition that should have died naturally?"

The grandmother's grip tightened. "Empty? You think Samuel died for empty?" Her voice hardened. "You think I hid in a hospital for three months, eating floor scraps, for empty?"

Then, quieter: "But maybe you're right. Maybe empty is all we have left. Maybe that's enough."

Giulia set down her phone. "Nonna, he's not asking about quitting. He's asking about meaning." She turned to Ethan. "Persistence for what? To prove Romans wrong? To spite Christians?" She laughed sharply. "Half my friends are intermarried. Their kids eat prosciutto and light *Shabbat* candles. Are they failing at being Jewish, or succeeding at being Italian?"

Michele's oil-stained hands were gentle on his mother's shoulder. "She asks the questions we don't want to hear." He looked at his daughter. "Tell him what you told the rabbi last week."

Giulia's confidence faltered slightly. "I said maybe Judaism survives best when it stops trying to survive. When it just lives." She gestured toward the window, toward Rome flowing past. "We're not museum pieces. We're not carrying sacred fire. We're just people who happen to make excellent artichokes and remember some very old stories."

Friday afternoon, Ethan helped in the kitchen, both women's words still working under his skin. Family arrived in waves. Arguments about football, traffic, whether cousin David's girlfriend was Jewish enough. A kid ran through the kitchen and Michele caught him by the collar without looking up from the stove. Same gesture Ethan's uncle used at Thanksgiving in Sheepshead Bay. Different continent, same muscle memory.

Giulia appeared beside him, drying wineglasses. "Overwhelming?"

"I came here looking for solid ground. Everyone keeps giving me more questions."

She smiled, but didn't finish the thought. Ethan waited. She just dried another glass.

When the sun set, Michele's wife lit candles. Same words he'd heard in Jerusalem, Tel Aviv, Brooklyn. Now with Roman intonation. Through the window, couples strolled along the Tiber.

After dinner, after songs, Michele poured one more glass and said nothing.

Saturday night, Ethan returned to the Forum. The arch stood lit, tourists taking photos. He walked around it, not under.

Sunday morning, Michele handed him a package without ceremony. Recipe cards in faded handwriting. He didn't explain them. Ethan didn't ask.

The train pulled from Termini toward Germany. In his bag, recipes he hadn't read yet, oil-stained, annotated in languages he couldn't decipher. He thought about opening them and didn't. Outside the window, Italy blurred into hills and vineyards, then into tunnels through the mountains. In the dark glass his own face stared back, looking tired.
