# Chapter 13.5: Tuesday

The laundromat on Bedford smells like lavender detergent and warm lint. Ethan feeds quarters into machine number seven, the one with the handwritten sign taped above it: "DO NOT OVERFILL. WE MEAN IT. --Mgmt." The quarters are cold from his pocket, and two of them stick before dropping. He slaps the side of the machine. The trick works.

He dumps in his clothes without sorting. Darks and lights together, the way his grandmother would have scolded him for. Jeans, T-shirts, a pair of boxer briefs with a hole near the waistband he keeps meaning to throw out. The detergent cap is cracked, and he pours by feel, watching the blue liquid ribbon across the fabric before the water fills in, clouding it all away.

Tuesday. October 31st. No particular significance. Outside, Bedford Avenue moves at its regular pace, delivery bikes threading between parked cars, a woman walking three dogs at once like a charioteer. The laundromat's fluorescent lights hum a half-tone below the dryers. The combination is oddly calming -- the kind of white noise his meditation app tries to replicate with less success.

He sits on the wooden bench bolted to the wall and watches the porthole window of his machine. The clothes rise, tumble, fall. Rise, tumble, fall. A sock presses against the glass like something trying to escape, then gets pulled back into the churn.

Across the room, a woman folds laundry at the long table. She is maybe fifty, broad-shouldered, deliberate. She takes each shirt from the basket and shakes it once, crisp, before folding it into thirds. Men's shirts, all of them. Button-downs in blues and whites. She smooths each collar with her thumb before folding it under, the gesture so practiced it must be invisible to her. Ethan watches her hands and feels something he can't name. Tenderness for a stranger's tenderness. The woman notices him looking and he glances away.

At the table's other end, a boy of maybe twelve does homework. His notebook is open to math -- Ethan can see the long division from here, the careful erased lines and re-drawn numbers. The boy chews his pencil eraser, stares at the ceiling, writes something, erases it. His backpack sits on the chair beside him, one strap duct-taped where it tore. The dryers are his background music, and he does not seem to mind them at all.

Ethan's clothes tumble. He takes out his phone, scrolls without seeing, puts it back. The bench is warm from the dryers behind the wall. He stays until the cycle ends, pulls his clothes out damp, and feeds the dryer. The heat blooms when he opens the door -- a gust of it, almost alive, carrying the smell of someone else's fabric softener from the load before.

He waits.

---

The grocery store is a problem.

Not the store itself, a narrow C-Town on Grand Street with aisles tight enough that two carts can't pass without negotiation. The problem is the recipe card. His grandmother's chicken soup, written in her jagged cursive on a three-by-five index card from the recipe box his mother sent after the funeral. The card is soft at the edges, stained with something yellowish he hopes is chicken fat. The ink is ballpoint, fading to gray.

He reads it again in the produce section, holding the card in one hand and a bunch of dill in the other:

*Whole chicken, not too big. Carrots (4-5, good ones). Celery with the leaves. Parsnip if you can find. Onion, skin on for color. Dill, a big bunch. Salt -- a pinch. Peppercorns. Water to cover. Patience.*

He doesn't know what "not too big" means for a chicken. He stands in front of the poultry case for a long time, looking at packages. Four pounds? Six? His grandmother's hands were small. Her pot was the tall one, white enamel with the blue rim, and he tries to picture the bird fitting inside it but can't remember the pot's dimensions. He picks up a four-pound chicken, puts it down, picks up a five-pound one. Settles on the four.

Carrots next. "Good ones" offers no guidance. He picks through the bin, looking for something, he doesn't know what. Straightness? Color? Firmness? He squeezes one and it feels like a carrot. He bags five, because the card says four-to-five and he'd rather have the extra.

The parsnip takes him longer. He has to ask the stock boy, a teenager with earbuds in, who points wordlessly to a bin Ethan has already walked past twice. Parsnips look like pale carrots that gave up on themselves. He grabs two.

Celery with the leaves. Every bunch of celery he picks up has been trimmed clean, the leaves removed. He digs to the back of the display, where a bundle still has its pale green leaves intact, slightly wilted. Good enough.

The dill smells sharp and green when he holds it to his face. Like summer, even in October. The bunch is enormous; "a big bunch" might mean half this or all of it. He takes the whole thing.

He pays and carries the bags out to Grand Street, the plastic handles cutting into his fingers. The chicken is heavy against his thigh as he walks. The air outside is colder than when he went in -- the kind of late October shift that catches you wrong. He wishes he'd brought a heavier jacket. Leaves skitter along the gutter, brown and dry, making a sound like paper shuffling.

---

McCarren Park at noon holds the last warmth of the season.

Ethan sits on a bench near the running track, a turkey sandwich from the deli on Driggs in his lap. The bread is too thick and the mustard is the wrong kind, yellow when he wanted brown, but he eats it anyway. Pigeons gather at his feet with the shameless confidence of creatures who have never once been turned away.

The park is half-full. Joggers orbit the track in their determined loops, earbuds in, faces fixed in that expression of pleasurable suffering. Dogs chase each other across the grass -- a lab and something smaller, scrappy, unidentifiable -- their owners standing together, talking, not watching. A woman sits cross-legged on a blanket, typing on a laptop. Two kids kick a soccer ball, badly, with more enthusiasm than aim.

Near the chess tables, a man plays alone. He is perhaps sixty, gray-bearded, wearing a corduroy jacket too warm for the afternoon but somehow right for him. He sits on one side of the stone table, makes a move, then stands, walks around, and sits on the other side. Studies the board. Makes a move. Stands. Walks around. Each transit takes him maybe eight seconds, and he does not rush. His face changes depending on the side: one way he looks aggressive, leaning forward, chin out; the other way, contemplative, almost sad.

Ethan watches him through three complete cycles. White bishop takes black pawn. Black rook advances. White retreats. There is something deeply private about the performance, a man so committed to his own game that the park ceases to exist around him.

A pigeon lands on the bench beside Ethan and regards him with its one visible eye. He tears off a corner of bread and drops it. The pigeon takes it and leaves. No gratitude, no ceremony. Transaction complete.

The wind picks up. The trees along the park's edge have gone orange and rust, and the leaves that come loose drift at an angle, traveling, as if they have somewhere to be. The light belongs to late October in Brooklyn -- low, golden, slanting through the remaining canopy in columns that feel almost solid. By four it will be gone. By five, dark.

He finishes the sandwich. Folds the wax paper into a square and pockets it. Sits a while longer, hands in his jacket, watching the chess player argue with himself.

---

The bakery is on Lee Avenue, deeper into the Hasidic part of the neighborhood than Ethan usually walks. He isn't sure why he came this way. His feet chose the route while his mind was somewhere else. Now he is standing in front of a shop with Hebrew lettering on the glass and a display case of challahs and rugelach and something dark and dense that might be babka.

Inside, the air is warm and yeasty, sweet with cinnamon. A woman behind the counter slides a tray of cookies from the oven -- the blast of heat reaches him two steps from the door. The floor is checkered linoleum, scuffed and clean. A small table in the corner holds a jar of instant coffee and a stack of paper cups.

He orders a black-and-white cookie and a coffee. The man at the register has a thick beard and thick fingers and does not attempt conversation. The cookie is enormous, bigger than his hand, and it bows under its own weight when he lifts it.

He sits at the small table. The coffee is weak and barely warm. The cookie is perfect -- the vanilla side chalky and sweet, the chocolate side bitter and glossy. He eats the chocolate side first, the way he always has, saving the vanilla. Outside the window, two boys in *peyot* run past, their *tzitzit* flying behind them, one chasing the other over some dispute too urgent and too young to understand from here.

The door opens and a woman enters. She is Hasidic -- dark wig, long skirt, a baby on one hip and a toddler gripping her other hand. She orders in Yiddish, quick and familiar, and the man behind the counter smiles for the first time since Ethan has been watching. He bags something -- bread, from the shape -- and pushes it across the counter. She pays, tucks the bag under her arm.

Then she sits at the table beside Ethan's.

She settles the baby on her lap, frees a hand, tears a piece from the bread. Holds it for a moment. Closes her eyes. Her lips move -- barely, just a murmur, a few syllables that Ethan recognizes by their rhythm more than their sound. *Hamotzi lechem min ha'aretz.* The blessing over bread. She opens her eyes and eats.

That is all. No ceremony. No performance. The toddler tugs at her sleeve and she tears him a piece too. The baby reaches for it and misses. She adjusts the bread to a smaller piece, places it in the baby's fist. The baby gums it solemnly.

Ethan holds his coffee and does not move. The closed eyes, the few murmured words, the opening of eyes, the return to the ordinary -- something about the quietness of it holds him still.

The woman eats her bread. The baby drools on his. The toddler has already lost interest and is pulling napkins from the dispenser one by one. Outside, the two boys have resolved their dispute or abandoned it, and they walk together now, kicking a bottle cap between them down the sidewalk.

Ethan finishes his cookie. Drinks his bad coffee to the bottom. Leaves.

---

The apartment smells like raw chicken and cold air.

He unpacks the groceries on the counter, lining up the vegetables beside the cutting board. The recipe card goes on the windowsill, propped against the glass. The chicken sits in its plastic wrap, pale and heavy, leaking something pinkish onto the counter. He wipes it up with a paper towel.

He doesn't know what he's doing. The card says "whole chicken" but doesn't say whether to rinse it, and he vaguely remembers a debate about rinsing poultry that he's seen online but never paid attention to because he never cooked a whole chicken before. He rinses it. The water is cold against the skin, which is smoother than he expected, stippled with goosebumps that look disturbingly human.

He puts the chicken in the largest pot he owns, a stainless steel thing he bought at a stoop sale three years ago and has used exactly twice. It fits, barely. The legs stick up above the rim, and he pushes them down, feeling the joints resist then give. The sound makes him wince.

Carrots. He peels them over the trash can, the peeler catching and stuttering on the knobby parts. The peels come off in long curls, bright orange against the garbage below. He cuts them into rounds, then looks at the card. It doesn't say how to cut the carrots. His grandmother's rounds were always thin, nearly translucent, but that detail lives in his memory, not on the card. He cuts them thicker than she did. Wrong, probably. He leaves them.

The onion makes him cry, which he expected, but the force of it surprises him, tears spilling fast enough to blur his hands. He wipes his eyes with his wrist and keeps cutting. Skin on, the card says. The papery outer layer floats off the cutting board onto the floor.

Celery, parsnip, dill. The dill goes in last, the whole bunch, because he never decided how much was "a big bunch" and it's easier to use all of it than to decide. The pot is crowded now, vegetables jammed around the chicken like passengers on the L train.

Water to cover. He fills the pot from the tap, watching the water rise over the breast, the drumsticks, the vegetables. It overflows slightly when he lifts the pot to the stove, sloshing onto the burner, hissing when the gas catches.

Salt -- a pinch. He pinches salt from the box. It feels like nothing, a few grains between his fingers. He adds it. Pinches again. Again. How much salt did his grandmother's pinch hold? Her fingers were smaller, but her pinches were confident -- born of repetition, of decades of Fridays. He adds one more pinch and stops.

Patience.

He stands at the stove while the pot heats. The water takes a long time to move. First nothing, then a few bubbles clinging to the bottom of the pot, then more, rising slowly, popping at the surface with a sound too small to hear but somehow audible anyway. Steam begins to curl from the surface. He turns the heat down. The card doesn't say how far down. He guesses medium-low and watches.

The apartment darkens around him. He hasn't turned on the lights, and October's early sunset pulls the color from the room by five. The windows go gray, then blue. The stove's gas flame makes a small circle of orange light on the ceiling. The soup begins to smell.

First it's just hot water and raw chicken, a smell like nothing. Then, slowly, the dill comes through. Then the onion, deepening, turning sweet. Then something underneath he can't identify -- the combination, maybe, the alchemy of ordinary things in hot water over time. The steam rises and fogs the window above the stove. He wipes it clear with his sleeve, sees Bedford Avenue below -- headlights in the early dark, a man walking a dog, the laundromat's neon sign flickering on.

He stands there a long time. Forty-five minutes, maybe an hour. Occasionally he lifts the lid and looks. The broth yellows. The fat rises in small circles. The chicken softens, the skin going translucent, pulling away from the bone.

On the counter beside the stove, his grandmother's *yahrzeit* candle sits in its glass. Unlit. Just there.

He tastes the broth with a spoon at seven-fifteen. Too much salt. The carrots are thick, blocky, nothing like his grandmother's paper-thin coins. The dill is overwhelming -- he used too much, the whole bunch when probably half would have been right. The parsnip has disintegrated entirely, leaving fibrous threads floating in the broth. The chicken is fine. Just chicken.

He ladles a bowl and stands at the counter to eat. He doesn't sit down. Doesn't turn on the television or open his phone. Just stands in the kitchen in the near-dark, the blue flame of the stove the only light, eating soup that isn't his grandmother's.

But the apartment smells like her kitchen on a Friday afternoon. Dill and onion and chicken fat. The warmth of the broth in the bowl, the steam against his face. The same species of thing -- the distant echo of a sound he is trying to learn.

He eats the whole bowl. Ladles another. This one he takes to the window and drinks standing up, looking out at Bedford Avenue and the night coming down hard over Brooklyn, the streetlights catching the last of the leaves as they let go.

The soup is not good. He eats it anyway.

He washes the bowl and the spoon and leaves the pot on the stove to cool. The recipe card stays on the windowsill, propped against the glass, his grandmother's handwriting catching the streetlight. The last word visible in the dark.

*Patience.*
