# Chapter 33: The Light of Israel

The muezzin's call threaded through the dark, and then the birds answered, and then the first gray light touched the limestone sill where Ethan Israel Stone's hand rested, palm down, feeling the cold seep out of the stone as Jerusalem warmed. He had been watching for this, the moment the city traded black for blue. Three years of watching, and it still caught him. He wondered if this was what living felt like, or if he was just better at pretending. The habits had not broken. Maybe they were not supposed to.

The sertraline sat on the nightstand next to his phone. Seventy-five milligrams now, adjusted twice since the move. His Israeli psychiatrist, Dr. Yael, had warned him about this: the assumption that arrival cured everything. "Aliyah is not an antidepressant," she'd said at their last session, pulling no punches in the Israeli way. "You moved your geography, not your neurochemistry." She was right. The gray mornings still came. Not as often, not as deep, but they came. Some weeks he woke feeling the old nothing, the ceiling-staring blankness that no amount of Jerusalem stone could fill. Those weeks he called Dr. Yael, took his pills, went to the Wall anyway. The medication didn't make him spiritual. The spirituality didn't replace the medication. Both held him up, different hands on different parts of his weight.

His phone buzzed with work notifications. The consulting clients he had picked up after construction work wrecked his back did not care that he davened before their morning calls.

Sera stirred beside him, her hand finding his chest, the steady heartbeat that still surprised him each morning. The twins slept in the next room, three years old today. Born during Passover 2024, when the sirens had been quiet for two weeks—the longest stretch of peace they'd known. The timing had felt symbolic: freedom and birth, exodus and arrival.

His parents slept in the guest room, visiting from New York, still adjusting. "It's so different than we imagined," his mother had said yesterday, though Ethan couldn't tell if that was disappointment or wonder in her voice.

The Old City at dawn: empty except for soldiers and insomniacs. Same route for three years. Muscle memory now, like his seventeen-minute showers had been in Brooklyn. The thought came unbidden that he might just be replacing one routine with another. Same person, different backdrop.

The coffee shop at the corner. The owner nodded as Ethan passed, a gesture of recognition across the divide. Some mornings they exchanged a few words. Today, just the nod.

The Wall. Some mornings it felt like comfort. Today it felt like a gravestone. The security checkpoint was routine now. Three years of muscle memory.

His usual stone was unmarked today, warm from the morning sun.

The stone was warm. Always warm. Body heat from thousands of hands, or the accumulated weight of three thousand years of prayer pressed into limestone. He didn't question it anymore.

He pulled out the paper. The note was written in Hebrew that now came naturally, not fluent but functional. The language had been sleeping in his DNA and was awakening word by word. The alphabet that once looked like mathematics had become the mathematics of prayer, of the daily negotiation with existence that required Hebrew's directness, its compression of eternity into syllables.

The note was simple:

"Ani Ethan Israel Stone.
Ben David v'Sarah shechazru.
Neked shel Rivka shenitzla.
Aba shel Ari v'Lev shelo yad'u galut.
Ba'al shel Sera sheher'eta li or.
Todah al hashlemut shetzmacha min hashevira shel October Shiva.
Todah al hachoshech shegila et ha'or.
Hineni. Ani kan.
Or Yisrael od dolek."

I am Ethan Israel Stone.
Son of David and Sarah who returned.
Grandson of Rivka who was saved.
Father of Ari and Lev who will never know exile.
Husband of Sera who showed me light.
Thank you for the wholeness that grew from the breaking of October 7th.
Thank you for the darkness that revealed the light.
Here I am. I am here.
The light of Israel still burns.

He folded the paper, found a crack between stones, pushed it deep where it would join the millions of other prayers. The Wall was not just stone but repository, not just barrier but the eternal ear listening to every language, hearing every prayer. The sunrise turned Jerusalem gold.

"Still coming."

Rabbi Levi's voice behind him. Not a question. The rabbi looked thinner than last month, the chemo showing.

"Where else would I—" Ethan stopped. "How are you feeling?"

"Like I'm running out of time to say important things." His eyes held Ethan's. "My son called last night. Wants me in New York for treatment. Better doctors."

"Are you going?"

"Maybe. Probably." He paused. "Who'll teach here?"

The question hung between them. *Is he asking me to stay? Or giving me permission to leave?*

"There are other rabbis—"

"Not for you." Levi coughed, small and dry. "You think you chose this place. This place chose you. That's the difference between tourists and residents."

They stood together. Teacher and student, though the roles were blurring faster now.

"Three years," the rabbi said.

"Three lifetimes."

"Not long enough. But maybe all we get."

The minyan formed. Ten men, just enough. Some faces were new: tourists, visitors. Some constants, like Ethan.

Ethan wrapped tefillin. The leather straps left marks on his arm that lasted until noon. Sera said they looked like bondage marks. "They are," he'd told her. She hadn't laughed.
His phone buzzed. Aaron: "Remember that remote CTO role I mentioned? They're offering 400K. Full remote. Decision needed by Friday."

Ethan stared at the message. Four hundred thousand. Sera could quit the hospital shifts that left her hollow-eyed. The boys could grow up in a yard instead of a safe room. His parents would see them more than twice a year.

A second text: "Westchester. Great Jewish day school. Sarah from your old shul teaches there. Her kids love it."

The prayers began. Shacharit, morning service. Ethan found himself lingering on the Modim, the gratitude blessing, his lips moving slower over the words. He skipped the petition for prosperity—what was the point?—but stayed longer with the Shehecheyanu, the blessing for reaching this moment alive.

"Shema Yisrael..."

Hear O Israel. His name called in the essential prayer, the declaration that despite multiplicity, there was unity. The divine was not elsewhere but everywhere, not listening but being the listening itself.

His Hebrew was functional now. Accented. Mistakes in every sentence. But functional. The prayers came automatically, the way any repeated act becomes reflex. Different life now. Same muscle memory.

"Abba! Abba!"

The twins' voices cut through morning prayer like the future running toward him on small legs that had learned to run in Jerusalem.

Ari reached him first, limping—he'd fallen yesterday, running during the siren. Already learning the Jerusalem run: not from play but from fear. Lev behind him, quieter since the bus bombing, since the weeks Ethan spent in the hospital. Three years old and already carrying the weight of almost-orphaned.

"Abba, your face is wet."

Not crying. Not exactly. Aaron's text burned in his pocket. Four hundred thousand. Safety. *What if he's right? What if stubbornness isn't the same as conviction?*

"Happy tears," he said in Hebrew, though his voice wavered slightly. Their first language now, though sometimes he still searched for words.

Sera arrived, moving carefully. Seven months pregnant now, their third child coming in spring. She'd adapted to Jerusalem like water finding its level.

"Quiet morning," she said, checking the horizon automatically. A habit now.

"Every morning," he replied, but the words felt hollow after the siren. After Aaron's text. *Four hundred thousand would buy a lot of quiet mornings.* The doubt settled in his chest like lead.

His parents appeared, having visited the women's section. His mother looked uncomfortable, not with the place but with herself, as if she had felt a pull toward prayer and did not know what to make of it.

"The children shouldn't—they shouldn't run here." His mother caught herself, hand moving to the Star of David pendant at her throat—a gift from Ethan she'd started wearing sometime in the last year without explanation.

"Let them run," his father said, adjusting the kippah he still forgot he was wearing. "I mean... this is—" He paused, looking around. "This is their place, isn't it?"

"Saba!" Lev called to his grandfather. The word meant grandfather—and, elsewhere, return. Ethan let the double meaning sit.

They walked home together through the Jerusalem morning. Thursday routine: shops opening, traffic building. It looked like home. Ethan was not sure it felt like home, or whether he was just committed to the performance. The question followed him like a shadow. Arabs sold bread to Jews. Jews sold coffee to Christians. Christians sold souvenirs to tourists who didn't understand they were participating, not observing.

Their apartment overlooked the Old City. Purchased with his Brooklyn money when the shekel crashed. The view that had seemed worth everything until the missile alert system was installed on their balcony. Now they saw the city through metal slats.

"Coffee?" Sera asked. The same morning ritual that had grounded them since the early days.

"After," Ethan said, pulling out his notebook. Three years of trying to make sense of this choice, these changes, this life that felt both inevitable and impossible.

He wrote:

"October 7, 2026. Three years. The twins are three. Sera carries our daughter. I think I'm learning what it means to stay. Not certainty—I still wake up some mornings wondering if I'm fooling myself. Whether the prayers are faith or performance. Whether the sertraline is doing the real work and the Judaism is just the story I tell myself about why I take it. Dr. Yael says these questions are healthy. She also says my cortisol levels don't care about Kabbalistic frameworks—the body keeps its own accounts, and three years of sirens and checkpoints and the low hum of living in a place that wants to kill you have left chemical signatures no amount of Torah study will erase. Some mornings the depression returns not as emptiness but as a specific, physical weight behind my sternum that I recognize from Brooklyn, from the years before any of this. The same weight. Different city. The spiritual life didn't cure it. I don't think anything cures it. I manage it. I light candles and take pills and see my therapist and some weeks I do all three and still lie in bed staring at the ceiling while Sera sleeps, wondering if the whole thing—the aliyah, the teaching, the faith—is just an elaborate structure I built to house a depression that would have followed me anywhere.

"What I've lost: Saturday mornings reading alone. Friendships that couldn't survive the distance. The career I was good at. The freedom of not believing in anything, which I didn't appreciate until I started believing in something and discovered that belief is a kind of weight. The universal beauty of Athens—I still dream about the Parthenon sometimes, its perfect indifference. The cosmopolitan freedom of being nobody, belonging nowhere, answerable to nothing. I traded all of that for this: particular, demanding, dangerous, mine.

"The light that flickers but doesn't go out. I am Ethan Israel Stone, still wrestling, still learning what that means. Still here. Still not sure if 'here' is home or habit."

Through the window, Jerusalem caught the morning light—not the warm glow of spiritual postcards but the flat, honest light of a Middle Eastern morning that illuminated everything equally: the ancient walls and the modern scars, the beauty and the barbed wire, the prayer shawls and the Iron Dome batteries. *Still here,* Ethan thought, and realized he was talking about the city and himself both.

Ari had removed his diaper and was trying to put it on the cat. The cat—a street tabby they'd adopted against Sera's better judgment—tolerated this with the resigned patience of all Jerusalem creatures. Lev sat in the corner methodically peeling the label off a jar of tahini, his face and arms already smeared with the stuff, a sticky Jackson Pollock of toddlerhood.

"Ari, no. The cat doesn't wear—Ari. *Ari.*"

Ari looked at his father with the serene defiance of a three-year-old who has made his decision. "Chatula needs," he said in Hebrew, pressing the diaper against the cat's haunches.

"Lev, stop eating the—that's not food, that's the *label*—"

Lev had moved on from peeling to tasting, his tongue working at the adhesive with scientific curiosity. When Ethan pulled the jar away, Lev's face crumpled—not into tears but into the operatic outrage of a toddler whose important work has been interrupted. He threw himself flat on the tile floor, kicked twice, then lay still, staring at the ceiling with his father's own thousand-yard stare.

*God, he looks like me when I'm depressed,* Ethan thought, and laughed despite himself.

"What's funny?" Sera called from the kitchen, where Ladino melodies her grandmother had taught her competed with the hiss of the stovetop espresso maker.

"Our son is lying on the floor having an existential crisis about tahini."

"Which son?"

"The one who isn't dressing the cat."

"Ah." She appeared in the doorway, seven months pregnant, a wooden spoon in one hand. She assessed the scene—naked Ari, tahini-covered Lev, the patient cat—and turned back to the kitchen without comment. Some battles weren't worth fighting.

Lev recovered as quickly as he'd collapsed, sat up, and began building a tower with blocks. Seven high, leaning left. Ari abandoned the cat project, came over, and raised his hand to topple it.

"Let it stand," Ethan said.

"It's going to fall anyway."

"Let it stand while it can."

The tower fell. Lev rebuilt without complaint, already reaching for blocks. Ari lost interest and went to find the cat again. The game continued, as it always had—three-year-olds destroying and creating with equal indifference to meaning, their father watching and trying not to turn everything into metaphor.

Outside, the city stirred to life. Jerusalem beginning another day.

Ethan sat at the kitchen table with his coffee—Ethiopian beans, rich and dark. The table was solid wood, the first piece of furniture they'd chosen together for this life.

"Abba, teach me letters," Ari said, climbing onto his lap.

"Which letter?"

"The first one."

"Aleph," Ethan said, and began to draw it on the paper. His son's hand over his, learning the shape. "It makes no sound by itself."

"Why?"

"Because it's the... the breath before the word. The silence that—" He paused, searching. "That holds all sounds. I think."

Ari traced the letter, tongue out in concentration.

Through the window, Jerusalem. Stone and light and shadows.

His grandmother's candlesticks sat on the shelf, polished now, used every Friday. Beside them, new ones. Smaller. For the twins when they were older. If there was an older.
Tonight they'd light together—his parents, Sera, the boys—no speeches, just small flames and a table that held all their versions of home.

There would be. Had to be.

Ari traced another aleph, then another, filling the page with silent letters.

The siren cut through their lesson like a blade through prayer.

Ari's crayon stopped mid-stroke. Lev dropped his block. Three years old and they knew that sound meant running.

"Mamek!" Sera called from the kitchen, her voice sharp but controlled. Seven months pregnant and still faster than anyone to the safe room.

Ethan's parents froze. His mother's face went white.

"Move," Ethan said, lifting both boys. "Safe room. Now."

They ran. Again. As they had dozens of times. As they might dozens more.

The safe room: reinforced concrete, water bottles, children's books. Their life reduced to essentials.

"How long?" his father whispered.

"Until it stops," Ethan said. "Could be minutes. Could be—"

The explosion came from the north. Close enough to rattle windows. Far enough to mean someone else's tragedy.

Lev buried his face in Ethan's shoulder. Ari counted on his fingers—a game he'd invented. How many sirens this month.

"Is this living?" Sera asked quietly, her hand on her belly.

The question hit different now. Not philosophical. Immediate. Real.

When the all-clear sounded, they emerged blinking. The lesson forgotten. The morning shattered.

His phone buzzed again. Aaron, with details now: the company was run by his old Columbia classmate, the house Aaron had found was on a street lined with maples. "Backyard big enough for the boys. Twenty minutes from your parents."

Sera read over his shoulder. Her hand still on her belly.

"We should talk about this," she said quietly. Not pushing. Not pulling.

Ethan looked at his parents, pale and shaken. At the boys, who had stopped counting sirens and started humming—a defense he recognized from his own childhood fears.

He picked up his phone and typed: "Let me think about it. Talk soon."

Send.

He set the phone on the table, screen still glowing, and turned to Ari, who was staring at his abandoned aleph, half-finished on the paper.

"Let's finish the letter," Ethan said.

Ari's hand found his father's. Together, they completed the stroke.

"What comes after aleph?"

"Bet," Ethan said. "It means house."

Through the window, light caught the limestone—the flat, honest light of a Jerusalem morning, though from here he couldn't tell if the glow on the northern horizon was dawn still unfolding or the trail of something intercepted. His grandmother's candlesticks on the shelf held the light like a menorah reduced to its essence. On the table, his phone. Aaron's offer. The maple-lined street.

Ari held his crayon, waiting.

"Our house?" he asked.
