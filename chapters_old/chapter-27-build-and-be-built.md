# Chapter 27: Build and Be Built

July in Jerusalem, the heat different from Brooklyn summer. Dry, bone-deep, pressing down with the weight of blessing or burden depending on the day. By nine in the morning the air shimmered above the pavement and the stone buildings radiated heat like bread ovens, holding the sun's punishment long after shadow fell. He slept differently now—deep and sudden, the construction work emptying him so completely that he'd fall unconscious at 9 PM and wake at 4:30 without an alarm, his body synced to the site's rhythm. In Brooklyn, he'd been an insomniac, lying awake parsing his own thoughts until 2 AM. Here, sleep came like a trapdoor opening. Sera said he didn't move all night, lay flat on his back like a man in a coffin, she joked, which wasn't funny but was accurate.

Seven months since his return from the journey through exile. Seven months of Sera's apartment becoming theirs, of navigating the byzantine process of transforming tourist status into temporary residency, then into the long aliyah process that would make him Israeli. Transforming return into remaining.

The Ulpan classroom at eight in the morning already stifling, windows open to catch breeze that never came. The plastic chairs stuck to the backs of everyone's thighs, and condensation ran down water bottles left on desks for five minutes. Twenty-three new immigrants bent over Hebrew worksheets, conjugating verbs that refused to follow patterns. They wrestled with a language that read backward and thought in circles and somehow made more sense than English when it came to describing the soul. Natasha fanned herself with a workbook. Philippe had dark crescents under his arms. Orna taught in a sleeveless blouse, unfazed, Jerusalem-born and heat-calibrated in ways none of them would ever be.

"Ani boneh," Ethan repeated, the verb for building. "I build. Atah boneh. You build. Hu boneh. He builds."

Beside him, Natasha from Moscow corrected his pronunciation. She'd been a cybersecurity specialist back home. Now she stocked shelves at Rami Levy while her credentials underwent "evaluation." "Boneh. From the throat. Like you're coughing up dust from old buildings."

Behind them, Philippe from Lyon muttered the same words, his French accent turning Hebrew into something musical and frustrated. He'd been a cardiologist back home, fled rising antisemitism after his hospital parking spot was repeatedly vandalized. Everyone in the room sculpting new lives from the demolition of everything they'd been before.

The teacher, Orna, wrote on the board: "אני בונה את עצמי." I build myself. Born in Haifa, sabra-tough, she had patience for genuine effort and none for whining.

"This is reflective verb," she said in Hebrew slow enough for them to catch. "Not just constructing. Shaping the self. Forging identity. You understand?"

They understood. Everyone in the room had dismantled themselves to be here. Former doctors and lawyers and professors and tech workers, all apprentices again, learning to speak, to read, to think in a language their ancestors had spoken. America or Russia or France had erased it, generation by generation, until only fragments remained. Holiday words. Grandmother prayers. The sounds but not the meaning.

"Ethan," Orna called. "Tell us in Hebrew what you're building."

He rose, the words forming slowly, each one heavy as lifted stone. "Ani boneh... bayit. Ani boneh... mishpacha. Ani boneh..." He stopped, searched for the word. "Life. How do you say life?"

"Chayim," the class responded in unison. They all knew that one. L'chayim. To life. The toast that survived even the most thorough assimilation.

"Ani boneh chayim chadashim." I build new life.

"Tov me'od," Orna said. Very good. But her eyes carried a longer knowledge. She'd seen thousands of Americans come with romantic ideas about return, most leaving when romance met reality, when building meant actual construction, actual struggle, actual transformation of self that hurt more than anyone expected.

At break, the immigrants clustered by shared confusion rather than nationality. Everyone traded bureaucratic horror stories. Natasha leaned against the wall, fanning herself with her workbook. "Freedom from antisemitism, they told me in Moscow. Come to Israel. Now I have freedom from my career, my salary, and hot water after 7 PM." She said it lightly, but her eyes were flat. Philippe nodded. "At least you have sun. In Lyon I had dignity and rain. Here I have neither." They stood in the strip of shade along the building's east wall, the courtyard too bright to cross without squinting. But Ethan settled beside Sera, who'd come to pick him up, her presence grounding him when Hebrew made his brain feel liquefied. She'd parked in the shade but the car would still be volcanic inside — they both knew they'd have to stand with the doors open for five minutes before sitting down, the steering wheel untouchable until it cooled.

"How was class?" she asked in English, merciful.

"My brain hurts. Everything backward. Verbs don't make sense."

"You're thinking in English still. Stop translating. Let Hebrew think for you."

"How do I let a language think for me?"

She smiled, that particular smile that had been different lately, carrying secret knowledge. "The same way you let Israel think for you. Surrender. Stop fighting. Become."

Her hand drifted to her stomach, an unconscious gesture he'd noticed more often lately. Something shifted in Ethan's chest. Recognition before knowledge. Body understanding before mind.

"Sera?"

"I was waiting. To be sure. To find the right words." She switched to Hebrew, slow, careful, making sure he understood. "Ani beherayon." I am pregnant.

The courtyard sounds faded. Russian arguments, French complaints, Arabic from the construction site next door where they were raising luxury apartments on land with ten thousand years of competing claims. All of it became distant, irrelevant against the sound of future arriving in Hebrew words his brain translated slow as archaeology. Pregnant. Carrying. Creating life.

"When?" Joy and terror flooded him in equal measure. A baby. Their baby. He wasn't ready. They couldn't afford this. None of that mattered.

"Hanukkah. That first week you were back." Her smile was tired but radiant. "The holiday of light. We received more than we knew."

His phone buzzed. His parents, their daily check-in. He caught himself composing a reply in his head the way he used to draft release notes — clear, stakeholder-appropriate, managing expectations. *Mom, Dad — excited to share we're entering a new phase of the lifecycle.* He put the phone away and just said it out loud to the courtyard air: "I'm going to be a father."

"I need a job," he heard himself say. "A real job. My A-1 temp permit needs renewal next month, and they want employment verification for the aliyah application. We need to — " He stopped. "Wait. We?"

"We," she confirmed. "If you want. If you're staying. Really staying, not just — "

"I'm staying."

The words came out certain though nothing was certain, though he had six thousand dollars left from his savings, though his Hebrew was broken, though the Ministry of Interior treated his aliyah application as an offense against bureaucracy itself.

"Then we need to make it official," she said. "Not the romantic part. The practical part. You need a work visa at least, better to start the aliyah process properly. My cousin works at Misrad HaPnim." Ministry of Interior. "She says bring everything. Every document. Birth certificate, parents' birth certificates, grandparents' death certificates, everything proving you're Jewish enough to return."

Jewish enough. The phrase caught in his throat. His grandmother would have laughed. Or cried. Or both. Jewish enough for the Nazis to kill, now needing to prove Jewish enough for Israel to accept.

He thought about the word he had been avoiding since Columbus Circle. *Zionist.* He was filling out aliyah forms. He was choosing to raise children in the Jewish state. The word still tasted wrong, too loaded with other people's definitions. But he was a Zionist by action if not by slogan. He believed this state had a right to exist and he knew its existence came at a cost paid disproportionately by people who had not chosen to pay it. The aliyah forms did not have a box for that.

And underneath: the privilege he kept forgetting to name. He could *choose* this. Natasha could not go back to Moscow. Philippe's hospital in Lyon had become hostile territory. They were refugees dressed as immigrants. Ethan had a Brooklyn apartment he could have returned to. *You can afford to seek meaning because you have already had safety.*

That afternoon, Ethan came home early to find Sera on the living room floor, hundreds of photographs spread around her like a paper timeline, her laptop open beside her showing grant application forms. She didn't look up when he entered, just kept arranging and rearranging photos in perfect chronological order.

"What are you doing?" he asked softly.

"Applying for the Herzl Institute fellowship," she said, not looking up. She held up a photo of teenage Michal laughing at some long-ago beach. "Michal always said photos steal souls. I told her that was superstitious bullshit. Now I'm trying to prove her wrong by giving souls back, piece by piece."

All the photos were of the same woman. Dark-haired, laughing in most of them. Young.

"She was at Nova," Sera said quietly. "We were supposed to go together but I had a wedding to shoot. She went alone. Danced alone. Died alone while I was photographing someone else's happiness."

The photos kept spreading, widening circles of memory. Ethan sat carefully at the edge, not disturbing the pattern.

"This isn't your fault."

"I know. But the fellowship deadline is next week. If I get it, I can quit the survivor counseling. Focus on this full-time." She finally looked up at him, eyes red but determined. "Sometimes I think that's why I fell for you. Another interrupted story. But maybe that's what I photograph — how people rebuild after their narratives break."

Later, while Sera met with her gallery contact — who'd asked to see her Michal project but then canceled last minute, citing "sensitive content concerns" — Ethan went to the construction site he passed each morning. Sera had been quiet about the rejection, but he'd seen her methodically reorganizing the photos afterward, searching for some other way forward. The foreman, Yossi, looked at his soft hands, his American clothes, his immigrant desperation. The heat at the site was different from street heat — it rose from the concrete and the raw stone walls and hit you from all directions, and by midmorning even the shadows were warm.

"You ever build anything?"

"No."

"You strong?"

"I can become strong."

"You speak Hebrew?"

"Learning."

Yossi laughed, not unkindly. "Americans. You all come to find yourselves. You know what you find?" He gestured at the site. "Stones are heavy. Sun is hot. Building hurts." He pulled out his phone, showed Ethan a photo — little girl missing her front teeth, grinning at a Purim carnival. "My granddaughter. Eight years old next month. Lives in Haifa with my daughter who thinks I should retire, learn watercolors. 'Saba,' she tells me, 'your back is shot.' True. But concrete doesn't complain like watercolors do."

"I need work."

"Tomorrow, five AM. Bring water. Bring work clothes. Bring humility. I pay cash first week while you sort documents. Work permit's tricky — Ministry's been cracking down. We'll see. If you last that long."

He lasted — barely. The first day, he carried a bag of cement up two flights and tore something in his shoulder that wouldn't heal for months. The second day, he mixed mortar wrong — too wet, useless — and Yossi made him dump it and start over while the crew watched. The third day, he dropped a box of tiles worth eight hundred shekels. Yossi docked his pay without discussion.

By the second week, his body had stopped betraying him constantly and settled into steady, low-grade failure. He couldn't drive the site van — Israeli driving required an aggression his reflexes rejected, and after nearly backing into a parked bulldozer, Yossi banned him permanently. "You drive like an old woman from Haifa," he said. "No offense to old women from Haifa." He couldn't cook for the crew either — his one attempt at shakshuka produced something Mahmoud quietly scraped into the dumpster while making eye contact. "Stick to American food," Mahmoud suggested. "Sandwiches, maybe."

But every morning, predawn darkness to the site, hands blistering then callousing, back screaming then strengthening, Hebrew improving through construction vocabulary — concrete, rebar, level, plumb, all the words for making things straight and true and permanent. The Palestinians on the crew taught him Arabic curses. The Russians taught him to drink Turkish coffee so strong it could dissolve mortar, and how to calculate angles without a calculator. By ten the heat pressed down like a hand on the back of his neck, and by noon the stone they carried burned through their work gloves. They moved slower in the midday hours, not from laziness but from animal wisdom — Jerusalem July could put a man on the ground if he fought it. Yossi enforced a water break every forty minutes and nobody argued.

On his third week, a man in expensive shoes appeared during lunch break, arguing with Yossi in rapid Hebrew. The crew sat in the building's shadow, passing Mahmoud's mint tea — scalding hot even in the heat, which he insisted was the point. Mahmoud unwrapped vine leaves his wife had stuffed that morning and broke off a piece for Ethan without being asked.

"Neighbor," Mahmoud, the Palestinian tile-setter, explained to Ethan in careful English, nodding toward the man in expensive shoes. "His apartment — shadow from our building will block his garden three hours each day. He threatens lawsuit." The man's voice rose, gesturing at Mahmoud and two other Palestinian workers. "And he doesn't like us working here. Says we're 'security risk.'" Mahmoud smiled grimly. "Forty years I work construction in Jerusalem. Built half the Jewish Quarter after '67. Now I'm security risk for laying tile."

During the break, Mahmoud showed Ethan a photo on his phone — a young woman in a black graduation gown, mortar board tilted, grinning against a backdrop of stone arches. "My daughter. Al-Quds University. Engineering." He said it the way fathers say such things everywhere, the pride too large for any language. She'd graduated in June. She wanted to design bridges. Mahmoud had spent forty years building Jewish homes in a city where his daughter needed a permit to visit him at work.

Yossi's response to the neighbor was sharp, final. The expensive-shoes man left, still shouting.

But the next afternoon, something shifted back. They were pouring the fourth-floor slab, the whole crew in rhythm, and somewhere in the middle of it someone started singing. Viktor began with something low and Ukrainian. Mahmoud countered with an Arabic work song. Yossi belted an old army tune completely off-key. Ethan tried Hava Nagila, which made the entire crew groan and then laugh harder.

They were covered in concrete splatter. The sun was cooking them. The sound was terrible, genuinely awful, and nobody cared. Just six men building something together in July heat, singing badly, alive and ridiculous in a way that asked nothing of them except to keep pouring.

The following Tuesday, Mahmoud and two other Palestinian workers weren't at the site. Ethan found Yossi on the phone, arguing in rapid Hebrew, his free hand making the chopping gesture that meant someone above was being unreasonable.

"New security restrictions," Yossi told the crew. "Permits revoked. All Palestinian workers, effective this morning." He kicked a bag of grout. "I've got a deadline in six weeks and they take my best tile man."

Ethan stood in the half-finished apartment, the floor where Mahmoud's tiles should have been going down still bare concrete. The work permits. The security review. The system that decided who could cross which line on which day. He thought about his own paperwork sitting in the Ministry of Interior — the aliyah application, the Law of Return, the documents proving his grandmother's blood entitled him to walk through doors that Mahmoud, who'd built these doors, could not. Different paperwork. Same system. One set of papers opened a country; the other set closed it.

He didn't say anything. There was nothing to say that wouldn't sound like exactly what it was — an American discovering that his arrival and someone else's exclusion ran on the same machinery.

They worked shorthanded that day. The concrete was the same. The sun was the same. Ethan noticed a hairline crack running through the foundation slab they'd poured the week before, a thin dark line like a sentence that hadn't been finished. He marked it with chalk for Yossi. Something had shifted, and he couldn't un-shift it.

Three days later, Mahmoud's permit was reinstated. He came back to work without comment, laid tile with the same precision, brewed the same mint tea. Neither of them mentioned it. The crack in the foundation was filled with epoxy. You could still see the line if you knew where to look.

"You see?" Yossi pointed at the skyline one morning, Jerusalem spread golden in sunrise, the brief hour before heat made the city shimmer and blur. "Every building here is politics. Every stone is history. We build on top of what was, always. Layers and layers. Jewish, Arab, Roman, Byzantine, Crusader, Ottoman, British, now us again." He kicked at a piece of rubble, uncovering a pottery shard. "Someone's breakfast bowl, maybe. Thousand years old."

His parents arrived two weeks later. Instead of the son returning to Brooklyn, they found him dark from sun, rough from labor, speaking broken Hebrew to his pregnant girlfriend — not wife, they noted — in a two-room apartment where the mezuzah was actually touched, actually kissed. The heat hit them at the airport door and his mother flinched. "How do people live here?"

"This is what you chose?" His mother's voice cracked. She stood at the window, watching the construction site.

*God, she looks old. When did that happen? I'm breaking her heart.* "Mom, this is what — it chose me."

"Don't be — you're a college graduate. Six-figure salary. You had — "

"I have a future. Just not — " *Not the one you sacrificed for. Not the safe American dream you built for me.* "Not the one you planned."

His father scanned the apartment. "Healthcare?" *Always practical. Always worried.*

"It's free. Universal."

"Security? That stabbing in the Old City — "

"Dad, there was a shooting — in Brooklyn. Yesterday. Three dead." *Why am I being defensive? He's scared. They're both scared.*

Sera made dinner — a Thursday-night Shabbat to show them something beyond politics. The kitchen filled with roasting chicken and za'atar, challah she'd braided that afternoon, marak thick with chickpeas and turmeric. His mother helped in the kitchen, then quietly hummed a Hebrew lullaby she'd learned from his grandmother. "I haven't thought of that song in thirty years," she murmured, surprised by her own voice. His father noticed the mezuzah screws were loose and quietly tightened them, his lips moving in what looked like prayer, though he'd claim later he was just checking if the parchment was straight.

"The pregnancy," his mother said carefully. "When?"

"January. Maybe February."

"You'll come home for — for the birth? American citizenship — "

"No." *Too quick. Too harsh.* "The baby will be Israeli."

The silence that followed had weight, had history, had his grandparents' fleeing and his parents' assimilation and his own return all compressed into the space between words.

His mother reached into her purse, pulled out something wrapped in tissue paper. "I brought these." His grandmother's Shabbat candlesticks, the ones from Poland she'd mentioned in her voicemail months ago. "Your father found them in the attic. Along with her ketubah. And..." She hesitated. "Her diary. From 1948. When Israel..." She couldn't finish.

"When Israel became real," his father said quietly, running his fingers over the brass. "She wrote about dancing in the streets of Brooklyn. Crying and dancing. She wrote..." His voice caught. "She wrote that she wished her parents could see it. That the promise had been kept." He looked at his son, conflict raw in his eyes. "I never knew she dreamed of going there herself. Never knew she saved money for a trip that never happened."

"You can't be serious," his father said.

"I've never been more serious."

His mother crying now, quiet tears she tried to hide. "We gave you everything. Good schools. Safety. And you choose this?"

"You gave me everything except a reason to be Jewish. Beyond avoiding antisemitism. Here, my child will know who they are."

"Your child will grow up in a war zone," his father said.

"Home. My child will grow up home."

The next evening, his mother surprised him. "I got tickets," she said, holding up her phone. "Jerusalem Theater. Beethoven — the late quartets. Opus 132. Don't argue."

He almost refused. But something in her face — not the grief, which had been constant, but a kind of offering beneath it — made him follow her to the taxi.

The Jerusalem Theater sat at the edge of Talbiyeh, limestone like everything else, but inside it could have been Lincoln Center. The seats were velvet. The program was in three languages. His mother sat beside him in the dark and he realized this was the first time in days they hadn't been fighting.

The Heiliger Dankgesang began. Beethoven's hymn of thanksgiving after illness, the music moving between ancient modal writing and something that sounded almost modern, almost free. The first violin climbed into a register so high and pure it seemed to exist outside of argument. Outside of covenant. Outside of any system that required you to be chosen or choosing.

Ethan felt something open in his chest that prayer hadn't reached. Not better than prayer — different. The music needed nothing from him. No response. No belief. No identity. It moved through the hall like light through glass, and he was moved by it, and that was enough.

His mother was crying again, but differently this time.

"This is what you gave up," she whispered during the pause between movements. "Not Brooklyn. Not the salary. This — the freedom to be moved by something without needing it to mean you're chosen."

He had no answer. The second movement began and he sat in the dark, shaken, because she wasn't wrong. The beauty of the quartets had nothing to do with Judaism, with Israel, with return. It was universal in the way Athens had been universal, and he had traded that universality for something particular and demanding and sometimes suffocating. The music asked nothing except listening. His life here asked everything.

They walked back through the warm night without speaking. She held his arm. He did not try to explain why he was staying. She did not ask again. Something had been said that couldn't be answered, and they both let it stand.

They left the next morning, earlier than planned. At the airport, his mother hugged him long, whispered, "When you come to your senses — and you will — we're here."

The Ministry of Interior, a week later. Five hours of waiting. The clerk, a Russian immigrant herself twenty years ago, looked at his papers with the exhaustion of someone who'd processed ten thousand dreams and watched nine thousand fade.

"Parents Jewish?" "Yes." "Grandparents?" "Holocaust survivor." "Documents?"

He had them. Death certificate, birth certificate, maiden name: Rosen. The names that had been changed, hidden, Americanized, now excavated for the purpose of return.

She pushed forms across the desk. "Sign. Hebrew date, not English."

He signed Ethan Israel Stone in Hebrew letters, the script still awkward but becoming familiar. She stamped his forms. "Temporary permit extended. Come back in six weeks."

Some nights, lying awake while Sera slept, he catalogued what he had lost. Not the salary — the subtler things. Saturday mornings reading the Times until noon. Spontaneous dinners at whatever restaurant caught his eye. The intellectual lightness of believing nothing. His LinkedIn still said Senior Product Manager and sometimes he opened it to remember that version of himself. That Ethan had been hollow. But he had been competent. This Ethan was full of meaning and could not tile a bathroom floor.

Most mornings, he woke at 4:30 for the construction site and tried to pray first. Some mornings it worked—the Modeh Ani coming from a real place, gratitude for another day in Jerusalem, the words connecting him to something larger. But most mornings, if he was honest, it was garbage. He'd stand by the window with his siddur open and his mind would be calculating how much rebar they needed for the third floor, or rehearsing the Hebrew phrase he'd botched at the grocery store yesterday, or wondering if Sera had remembered to buy toilet paper. The words came out of his mouth—"Baruch atah Adonai"—and they meant nothing. He was saying them because he was supposed to say them, because Rabbi Levi had told him that consistency mattered more than feeling, that prayer was a practice not a performance, that even the most devout Jews had mornings where the words tasted like sawdust.

He'd believed that in theory. Now he was living it. One Thursday morning in August, standing at the window at 4:37 AM, he caught himself reading the Amidah and simultaneously thinking about whether he should switch to a cheaper phone plan. The shame was immediate—here he was, addressing the Creator of the Universe, and his brain was comparison-shopping for cellular data. He closed the siddur, opened it again, tried to start over. The same emptiness. The same mechanical recitation.

He went to the site that morning feeling like a fraud.

But the following Shabbat—the one where Sera lit the candles and the light caught her face in a way that made him forget to breathe—the Kiddush prayer broke him open without warning, and he understood that the empty mornings were the price of admission for the moments that weren't.

That evening, Shabbat arriving as the heat finally loosened its grip. Ethan's hands, rough from the week's labor, lit candles with Sera. The Hebrew blessings came easier now, from repetition if not understanding.

"Baruch atah Adonai..." Blessed are you, Lord...

Through the window, construction cranes paused against the skyline.

"Your hands," Sera said, taking them after the blessing. "They're different."

They were. Calloused, scarred, strong. Builder's hands. Israeli hands. Split knuckles from carrying rebar. Concrete burns on the palms.

"Everything's different," he said in Hebrew, the words coming without translation now, without thought, the language thinking for him as Sera had promised.

"Nachon," she agreed. Correct. "Everything different. Everything beginning."

She guided his rough hands to her belly where something built itself without blueprint, without struggle, with the same certainty that had brought him here, kept him here, transformed him here.

"Names," she said. "If it's a boy?"

*A boy. Will he look like me? Will he sound Israeli? Will my parents ever meet him?* "Something with building. Boneh? Binyamin?"

"From everything," she said. "You came from grandmothers. From October 7th. From Cairo and Berlin and — " She yawned. "All of it led here."

*All of it. Including the fear. Including the doubt. Including the possibility this is all a mistake.*

The muezzin's call echoed from East Jerusalem, church bells from the Christian Quarter, and in their apartment, Jewish life continued its insistence on continuing.

"I need to call Rabbi Levi," Ethan said. "About the wedding. About making this official."

"Tomorrow," Sera said. "After Shabbat."

His phone, set to silent, showed a text from his mother he wouldn't read until tomorrow: "Your father thinks I should let you go. I can't. You're my son. Even this version I don't recognize."

"Shabbat shalom," Sera said.

"Shabbat shalom," he replied, his builder's hands holding her carefully, concrete dust still in the grain of his prayer book.

Outside, the cranes stood motionless against the sky, waiting for Sunday. The worry remained, quiet and persistent as Jerusalem stone.
