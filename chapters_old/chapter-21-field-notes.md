# Chapter 21: Field Notes

---

**November 28**

Ethan,

I'm writing this in a notebook I took from the supply tent. Blue cover, lined pages, IDF stamp on the back. The kind they give you for field reports. I'm using it wrong.

No email access. No phone. They took everything at intake except the Glock and what fits in my vest. So you get a letter you won't read until I hand it to you, which means I'm talking to myself, which is fine. I've done worse.

We're somewhere on the border. I can't say where. The heat is the kind that sits on your chest, not the dry desert heat from the Negev trip. Wet. Salty. Everything smells like turned earth and diesel.

Twelve of us in the unit. I'm the only photographer. They think that's funny.

---

**November 29**

Routine: wake at 0430. Gear check. Briefing that says nothing. Patrol. Wait. Hydrate. Wait more. Briefing that says nothing again. The waiting is the thing nobody warns you about. Not the operations. The waiting. Your body stays tensed for hours and then nothing happens and you can't release it, so it just accumulates, day after day, until your shoulders are concrete and your jaw aches from clenching.

Tomer, our sergeant, is twenty-three and already has the face of a man who stopped sleeping years ago. He calls me Kodak. The others call me worse.

I keep reaching for a camera that isn't there.

---

**November 30**

Your grandmother's birthday. You told me once. I remembered because you said she planted trees on her birthday instead of receiving gifts. Seventeen cedars in the Carmel.

I wonder where you are right now. Athens, if you kept the itinerary. Eating something with olive oil, probably. Looking at columns. Thinking deep thoughts about beauty and meaning while I inventory ammunition.

That's not fair. I know it's not fair.

**December 1**

Night watch. The border fence hums. You can hear it if everything else goes quiet, a low electrical drone like a fluorescent bulb in a dark room. Beyond it, nothing visible. Just black. Occasionally a flare goes up from another position and the landscape freezes for three seconds -- every bush and rock and coil of wire sharp-edged, overexposed, like a photograph taken at f/1.4 with a hard flash. Then it drops and your eyes have to start over.

I keep composing shots I can't take. The way Tomer's hands look when he field-strips a rifle -- muscle memory in the fingers, same economy of motion as a guitarist tuning. The silhouette of the observation post against pre-dawn sky, a geometry problem, all angles and no curves. The way dust hangs in a beam of light from the generator shed like particles suspended in developer fluid.

My eyes won't stop framing. It's the only part of me that still works the way it used to.

---

**December 2**

Something happened today. Can't write about it specifically. Security.

What I can say: sound carries differently underground. It doesn't echo, it compresses. You feel it in your teeth before your ears register it. A low-frequency vibration that reminds me of standing too close to a bass amp, except the bass amp is beneath you and it doesn't stop.

We stood in formation afterward and nobody said anything. Tomer smoked three cigarettes back to back. The medic checked us one by one. When she got to me she said, "You're shaking," and I said, "I'm cold," and she looked at me like I was an idiot because it was thirty-two degrees. She's right. I'm an idiot.

The fear isn't what you think. It's not the sharp kind, not like in movies. It's low-grade and constant, like a headache you stop noticing until someone asks if you're okay, and then suddenly it's unbearable. The boring kind. The kind that lives in your stomach and makes food taste like nothing and sleep feel like falling.

---

**December 3**

Found a photograph in my combat vest today. Didn't know it was there. Must have been in the pocket since I packed the bag at the apartment.

Michal. Her face, mid-laugh, hair across her mouth. I took it at a rooftop party in Florentin two years ago. She was dancing to something awful -- Mizrachi pop, too loud, the neighbors shouting. She didn't care. She never cared about the right amount of anything. Too much laughter, too much volume, too much love for the world.

I sat with the photo behind the supply tent for a long time. Didn't cry. Past crying about her. But my body did something -- a full-body clench, ribs to knees, like trying to hold water in your hands. The grief isn't in my head anymore. It moved into my muscles months ago. Some mornings I wake up and my fists are closed so tight the nails leave marks in my palms.

She would have hated all of this. The uniforms, the fence, the waiting. She would have sat down in the dust and played guitar until someone told her to stop, and then she would have kept playing.

Mine is still in the apartment. I haven't touched it since October.

---

**December 4**

I drew something today. Pencil on the back of a logistics form. Tomer said I could keep it since it's not operational.

A woman on the other side of the fence. I saw her through binoculars during morning watch. Maybe two hundred meters out. She was hanging laundry on a line between two cinder-block walls. White sheets, a child's shirt, something red. She worked methodically, the way my mother hangs laundry at the house in Teaneck -- pin, smooth, pin, step. Her back was to me. She didn't know I was watching.

Behind her, a kid sat on an overturned bucket. Just sitting. Kicking dust.

I watched her for eleven minutes. I know because I checked the log afterward. Eleven minutes of a woman doing domestic work while I held a weapon and observed her through military optics, and I thought: this is someone's Tuesday. This is someone hanging laundry. This is someone whose life I can see but never enter, and she can't enter mine, and between us is a fence that hums and a war that neither of us started.

I had to put the binoculars down. My hands were steady. My hands are always steady. But something behind my sternum came unlatched for a moment, and I needed to breathe in a way that had nothing to do with breathing.

I drew her from memory. The angle of her arms reaching up to the line. I got the proportions wrong -- her arms too long, the sheets too white. But the shape is right. The gesture of it. Ordinary life, framed by concrete.

---

**December 6**

Leaving tomorrow. Rotation complete. Replacement unit arrives at 0600.

Two weeks here. It feels like something measured in a different unit. Not days. Exposures, maybe. Each one burning a little deeper into the film.

I'm heading back to Jerusalem. The apartment will smell closed-up and stale. I'll open the windows and drink coffee on the balcony and watch the city do its thing and try to remember what it feels like to not have a weapon within arm's reach.

You're somewhere in Europe by now. Rome? Berlin? Collecting history in cities that tried to erase us. I hope you're finding what you went looking for. I hope the ruins are teaching you something the living couldn't.

But I worry, Ethan. I worry you're building a version of this -- of us, of Israel, of Judaism -- that's all library and no mud. That you'll come back with beautiful ideas and I'll come back with dust in my lungs and we won't be able to hear each other.

~~Maybe that's already~~

I don't know who I'll be when you see me again. I know who I was before this rotation, and she's not quite the person writing these words. Not dramatically different. Not broken. Just -- recalibrated. Like a lens that's been knocked slightly out of alignment, and everything looks almost right but the focal point has shifted two millimeters to the left, and only a photographer would notice.

You might not notice.

I might not tell you.

*Ani ohevet otcha.* Still. Even from here. Even in this dust and noise and boredom and fear. The Hebrew holds it better than English. English makes it a feeling. Hebrew makes it a fact.

Come back. I'll be here.

--- S.
