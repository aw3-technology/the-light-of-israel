# Chapter 32: What the Light Demands

Rabbi Levi's study smelled of binding glue and yellowed paper. Thursday evening light cut through dust motes.

"You're ready to teach," Rabbi Levi said. Not a question.

"I can barely explain what happened on the Temple Mount."

"Exactly. The teacher who thinks he knows teaches nothing. The student who knows he knows nothing—" Rabbi Levi rose, joints creaking. "They're waiting."

Six people on worn cushions, the study's formal chairs pushed against walls stacked with commentaries. Ethan recognized the Russian programmer from his first *ulpan* class, still tapping his phone obsessively. Beside him, an Ethiopian nurse in wrinkled scrubs, surgical mask tucked in her pocket, antiseptic under her nails from a double shift. The American lawyer's expensive suit carried the faint smell of cigarettes he'd quit but still craved. A *sabra* artist kept a sketchpad on her knee, cerulean paint under her nails. Moshe, older, Moroccan, kept checking his watch. His grandson's soccer game started at eight. And at the edge of the circle, a German student who'd introduced herself with her grandfather's history and then gone quiet.

Ethan's hands shook slightly. Chalk dust already coated his fingers from writing on the board.

"Three years ago," Ethan began, voice catching, "I thought *Yom Kippur* was 'Yom Kipper.'" He paused. "And I should tell you: this morning I stood at my window to pray and spent the entire *Amidah* thinking about whether we need a new water heater. The whole thing. Eighteen blessings, and all I could think was: the hot water cuts out after four minutes. That's what three years of devotion looks like some days. The water heater and the divine, fighting for space in the same skull."

The nurse snorted. The artist smiled. The room loosened. Permission to be imperfect.

The American lawyer laughed. Recognition, not mockery. "I thought *Shabbat* was a type of bread."

"I came here because October 7th cracked me open, made me realize I was Jewish in my bones but nowhere else." Ethan pulled out his grandmother's Hebrew Bible, its margins filled with her annotations. "Tonight we'll study *Lech Lecha*. God telling Abraham to go forth. But the Hebrew holds a secret: *lech lecha* doesn't mean go forth. It means go to yourself, into yourself."

"The Hebrew is specific," Ethan said, writing the letters on the whiteboard, his handwriting still childlike. "*Lech lecha*. Go to you. Not just from your land but to your essence."

"What does that mean exactly?" the nurse asked, exhaustion making her blunt.

"Who you are beneath... beneath who you learned to be, I guess." He paused, caught between certainty and doubt. "Though honestly, I'm still figuring—"

The German student interrupted: "But Abraham was commanded. We choose. How is it—"

"October 7th was our command," Moshe said quietly. "The world broke. We heard a voice calling through the—sorry, through the cracks."

The group shifted. Chairs creaked. The lawyer stopped scrolling.

"When I touched the Western Wall for the first time, I expected stone. I found a pulse."

The Russian programmer leaned forward. "Is this poetry or psychology?"

"I don't know." Ethan shrugged, surprising himself with the honesty. "Maybe both? Look, I'm not a scholar. I'm just trying to share what happened to me."

The American lawyer was taking notes on his phone. "So synthesis? Dialectics?"

"Um, honestly? I don't really know about the—what did you call it? Dialectics?" Heat rose in his cheeks. "But I know about tension. You need tension, or there's no—there's no music."

He drew two circles on the board, overlapping. "October 7th. Before, I was here"—pointing to one circle—"Jewish but not. After, everyone expected me to jump here"—the other circle—"fully religious, fully Israeli, fully certain. But the truth happened here"—pointing to the overlap—"in the middle kingdom where nothing is pure and everything is possible."

The artist drew as she talked. "You're describing negative space. The shape between shapes."

"Yes!" Ethan said. "In Poland, at Treblinka, I understood this. The absence was so present it became presence. The silence was so loud it became song."

The German student was crying silently, tears tracking through makeup she'd carefully applied. "My grandfather. He was there. Not as victim. As—" She couldn't say the word.

"As human," Ethan finished gently. "Capable of everything. That's the terrible teaching. We're all capable of everything. The choosing makes us who we are."

"What's your name in Hebrew?" the nurse asked.

"Ethan Israel." He wrote it on the board. "Added Israel when I moved here. The wrestling part."

"And before that?"

"Just Ethan. My parents picked it because it sounded American but not... you know. Too ethnic." He laughed, embarrassed. "I used to hate my name. Now I'm trying to grow into it."

The Russian programmer raised his hand again, old Soviet habits dying hard. "You speak of vision as fact."

Ethan hesitated. "I saw... I can't fully explain it. Ahmad, the guard, he—" He stopped. "Look, I'm not asking you to believe my experience. I'm asking what yours tells you."


"Enough theory," Ethan said. "Close your eyes. Breathe in for four counts. Hold for four. Release for four. The four-letter name of God that can't be pronounced. But it can be breathed."

Five minutes of silence. Jerusalem's evening sounds through the windows.

"This is beautiful," the American lawyer said when they opened their eyes, "but isn't it just spiritual bypassing? October 7th happened. Real people died. Real hatred exists. How does breathing and mysticism address actual evil?"

Ethan nodded. "Fair question. Next week, we start a practical piece. Monthly visits to Hadassah trauma unit. We'll read to survivors, sit with families. The teaching isn't separate from action. It's preparation for it."

The nurse shifted uncomfortably. "Hospital visits? I work there. Twelve-hour shifts. Fifteen years in this country." She paused. "You talk about the middle kingdom. My middle kingdom is the bus at 5 AM and the bus at midnight. My son, he's twenty-two, engineering student. Police stopped him three times this month. Three times. Walking home from the library." She looked at Ethan. "You want to know about the overlap between worlds? My son lives in it every day. He doesn't need a diagram on a whiteboard."

"The middle kingdom sounds nice in theory," the lawyer pressed. "But what about the mother whose child was murdered? Is she supposed to find the 'overlap' with her child's killer?"

The room tensed. This was the question beneath every question since that October morning.

Ethan walked to the window, the Old City walls golden in dying light. "I keep thinking about what I experienced on the Mount. How even the darkest things are still... part of reality. Part of what is." He turned back. "Not justified, not excused, but there."

"That's obscene," the lawyer said, standing. "They butchered children."

"Yes. And if we become them in response, they win twice. Once in the killing, once in the becoming."

"Easy to say when it's not your child." The lawyer's voice was tight now. "My nephew was at the music festival. Nineteen years old. They found him—" He stopped, breath ragged. "So forgive me if your mystical light feels like bullshit right now."

Silence stretched. The German student stared at her hands. The artist stopped drawing.

"I'm not asking you to forgive. I'm not sure I could either if—"

"Then what are you asking?" The lawyer's anger filled the room. "What's the point of this 'middle kingdom' if it doesn't help with the actual horror?"

Moshe glanced at his watch, then spoke quietly: "In Morocco, my grandfather was killed. 1948. The Jewish quarter." He stopped, searching for words, then checked his watch again. "Sorry, my grandson has a game. But my grandmother, she could have... instead she taught us to bake. *Challah*. Every Friday." He paused, torn between the teaching and the clock. "She said: we bake instead of burn. I was young, I thought it was just a saying. But maybe she knew."

The artist looked up from her sketch pad. "This middle thing you're talking about. It's not neutral, right? It's where—" She gestured with her pencil. "Where the categories break down? I'm trying to draw it but it keeps changing."

"I don't forgive," the German student said suddenly, fiercely. "Not them. Not my grandfather. Not myself for carrying his blood."

"I don't know about forgiveness," Ethan said carefully. "Maybe transformation? Taking the... the grief, the rage, the inheritance, and building with it instead. I'm still figuring out what that means."

The Russian programmer, who'd been silent, spoke up. "Can I say something uncomfortable?" He didn't wait. "This whole approach. The Kabbalah, the hidden light, the sparks. It's beautiful. It's also escapism. My grandmother survived the gulags. She didn't need mystical frameworks. She needed food and warm clothing. Sometimes suffering is just suffering. It has no hidden meaning. No sparks. No light waiting to be gathered. It's just pain that ruins people." He looked at Ethan. "I worry that what you're offering here is a way to make horror feel manageable. Comfortable, even. And maybe that comfort is what we need. But let's not pretend it's truth."

The room went quiet. *Is that what I'm doing? Making horror manageable? Dressing up my own need for meaning as a teaching?*

"You might be right," Ethan said. The admission cost him. "I don't know if what I'm teaching is wisdom or a coping mechanism with better vocabulary. Maybe all I can offer is a room where we don't pretend to have answers."


Students lingered, reluctant to break the circle's spell. The German student approached Ethan privately.

"My grandfather," she said. "He was at Treblinka. Not as guard. As commander. I came to Israel to... I don't know. Apologize? Atone? Disappear?"

"To transform," Ethan suggested. "To take that history and build from it. Not erasure but alchemy."

She nodded, tears again but different. Cleaner. "Will you teach every week?"

"Every Thursday. As long as people come."

The American lawyer was helping stack cushions, his earlier challenge dissolved into participation. "I didn't expect... this. I expected rules. Laws. Judaism 101."

"This is Judaism 101. The wrestling. The questioning. The refusing to let go until blessed, even if the blessing comes as permanent limp."

A figure lingered in the doorway, tote bag of workbooks against her hip. "I caught the end," Samira said quietly when the room thinned. "That part about the middle kingdom."

"You should come. Thursday nights."

"Your middle kingdom requires both sides to enter it," she said. "My students can't enter yours. One of them — Nour, eight years old — her family received an eviction notice last week. She asks me every morning if they'll still have a house when school ends." Samira touched the doorframe. "You want to build bridges. I'm trying to keep children from drowning. Those are different projects."

"They don't have to be."

She looked at him for a long moment. "If buses run on time," she said, and disappeared into the corridor.

Rabbi Levi appeared in the doorway, scattering breadcrumbs for the one-legged pigeon that had been coming for three years.

"*Nu*?" he asked.

"They learned. I learned. We all learned."

"Good. The teacher who stops learning stops teaching. Same time next week?"

Ethan nodded, then stopped. "Rabbi, the key—"

"Keep it." Rabbi Levi was already turning away, pocketing the remaining breadcrumbs. "The study is yours Thursday nights. I'll find somewhere else."


Jerusalem's streets were dark except for pools of lamplight, the evening's warmth giving way to desert cold.

His phone buzzed. A text from the Russian programmer: "Won't make it next week. Work deadline." Then another, from Sera: "Ari and Lev won't sleep until you sing the *Shema*. They're asking for their teacher *abba*."

Ethan climbed the stairs to his apartment, hearing the twins' voices through the door. He touched the *mezuzah*, kissing his fingers.

Inside, Sera held Ari while Lev hugged her leg. On the wall, two photographs: a sunrise over the Dead Sea from the morning after Sera's therapy breakthrough, and beside it, smaller — two teenage girls laughing on a beach. Sera and Michal, seventeen years before October stole one of them.

"How was the teaching?" Sera asked.

"Intense. The lawyer lost it. His nephew was at the festival."

"Are you ready for that kind of anger every week?"

"I don't know."

"Sometimes the teaching is just being there when someone breaks." She touched his hand.

He went inside to sing his children to sleep.
