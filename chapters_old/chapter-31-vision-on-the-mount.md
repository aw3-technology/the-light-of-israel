# Chapter 31: Vision on the Mount

The Temple Mount at dawn, empty except for birds, and I saw what was, what is, and what might be. All three tenses collapsing into a single moment that contained every prayer ever whispered here, every stone laid and destroyed and laid again.

He hadn't planned to be there. Jews rarely received permission to ascend, and when they did, it came with restrictions. But around 6 AM on the last day of *Sukkot*, walking the Old City walls in the half-light, a pull he couldn't name drew him toward the Mughrabi Gate. The morning air tasted of sage and dust, temple spices carried on wind that shouldn't exist between stone walls. His footsteps echoed wrong. Too loud, then too soft, as if the sound were being filtered through time.

The guard looked up from his phone. His name tag read Ahmad al-Rashid. A wedding ring worn thin from years of nervous twisting. The booth smelled of Turkish coffee gone cold, cigarette smoke clinging to fabric. He'd been here all night. Ethan could smell the exhaustion on him, sour and human.

"You don't have permission," Ahmad said in accented English, but without conviction. His shoulders sagged under a weight that went deeper than fatigue.

"No."

They stood there. Ahmad glanced at the security camera, back at Ethan, rubbed his face with both hands. "My daughter. She's sick. Leukemia. The doctors at Hadassah..." He stopped. "Yesterday she said she dreamed of a man with light. American. Said he would come to the morning gate."

A cold wire tightened in Ethan's chest. Fear or recognition.

"Seven years old. She shouldn't—" Ahmad shook his head. "Kids that age, they say crazy things. But she described your face." He glanced around. They were alone, but never really alone here. "Morning shift arrives in twelve minutes."

Ahmad stared at the security pad, numbers worn smooth by years of entry. His hands shook. "If they check the logs... I have six years until retirement. Pension for my daughter's treatments." His jaw worked. "Why should I risk that for you?"

Ethan had no answer.

Ahmad laughed bitterly. "My grandfather called prayer here forty years. Now my son won't visit from Berlin. Says he's ashamed of us all. The occupation, the collaboration, the whole mess." He looked up at the security camera. "Maybe he's right. Maybe we're all just keeping other people's peace."

His finger hovered over the keypad. "You have children?"

"Twin boys."

"Then you know." Ahmad's voice turned hard. "We do stupid things for them. And stupid things to keep them safe." He punched in the code. "Coffee spill on the keypad. Happens all the time."

The lock clicked.

"Quickly."

Ethan climbed the wooden ramp, each plank creaking a different note. His legs felt unsteady, as if the ground were tilting.

At the top, the plaza opened. Limestone worn smooth by millions of feet, each stone humming at a frequency just below hearing. Jerusalem sage sharp in the October air, but underneath it: frankincense, myrrh, the ghost of temple incense that shouldn't still linger after two thousand years.

A cat watched from beneath an olive tree, its eyes reflecting light that wasn't there yet.

The Dome of the Rock caught first light, gold burning against blue sky. But the gold moved. Rippled. Breathed. The blue tiles sang, each one a different note in a harmony that made Ethan's teeth ache. Al-Aqsa stretched elegant to the south, its arches whispering Arabic that he suddenly understood without knowing how. Between them, the vast plaza where everything had happened and was still happening. He could see it all at once, layered: Solomon's temple, Herod's courts, Crusader churches, Ottoman fountains, all existing simultaneously.

Birds erupted. Dozens of doves, their wings making sounds that resolved into Hebrew letters. Each feather left silver-white script in the air that hung for a moment before dissolving.

Ethan's vision sharpened until everything became too clear. The stones beneath his feet pulsed with their own heartbeat, and he could feel it through his shoes, up his legs, synchronizing with his own pulse until he couldn't tell where he ended and the Mount began.

Then fire.

Not metaphor. Actual heat on his face, cedar smoke thick in his throat.

The sound of stones falling carried the salt taste of tears.

Grief as physical weight in his chest.

He was watching it burn, but also burning with it. Rome taking what couldn't be taken, only transformed.

*What is this? Am I dying? Having a stroke?*

Unlike Warsaw's gentle revelations or Egypt's patient teaching, this place demanded everything at once. It wasn't offering healing. It was breaking him open, spilling him out.

His grandmother's voice: "Sometimes God speaks in ways that hurt."

But this wasn't hurt. This was dissolution.

The Dome blazed golden, and the gold rang. Blood from his nose mixed copper with cedar ash. Men arriving for *Fajr* moved in ripples he could see, concentric circles of faith spreading outward, passing through him.

Children played where the Temple courtyards had been. Arab and Jewish children together, their voices harmonizing.

Then walls rose between them, each brick a small death in his chest.

Then the walls crumbled, the reunion sharp with joy and salt.

He fell through layers of time, each century a different weight pressing down. Not metaphor but physics. Gravity pulling him through Solomon's gold, Herod's marble, crusader stone, all the way down to bedrock that hummed with accumulated prayer.

When his sight cleared, he was sitting on stones that felt too solid after what he'd experienced. Ahmad pressing water to his lips, the liquid shocking cold, tasting of ordinary minerals. Blessedly normal. "You fell. Hit your head." Ahmad's voice sounded flat after the symphonies he'd been hearing.

"Did you see—"

"I saw you fall." Ahmad's face was careful, neutral. Then, quieter: "My grandfather had visions here. Before the medications. Said this place..." He gestured helplessly. "Shows you things."

"What did you see?"

Ahmad was quiet for a moment, eyes on the golden Dome. "A man who needed water. A father thinking of his sick daughter." He paused, almost smiled. "Birds flying in circles. Could mean anything. Or nothing."

Ahmad pulled out a worn photo from his wallet: his grandfather and another man in 1940s Haifa, arms around each other's shoulders, both smiling. "They owned shops next to each other. Made some kind of promise about..." He trailed off, studying Ethan's face. "Probably nothing."

He put the photo away. "My grandfather had strange ideas about time. Said some debts take generations to pay."

They traded numbers without talking about why.

Ethan walked back as Jerusalem woke. Shops opening, children heading to school. His head throbbed, a deep pulse behind his right eye that sent sparks across his vision with every heartbeat. The nosebleed had stopped but his upper lip was crusted with it, metallic taste still coating his tongue. His legs worked but badly, each step deliberate and uncertain, as if the neural pathways between intention and movement had been scrambled and were still reassembling themselves.

Halfway down the Via Dolorosa, he had to stop and lean against a wall. His hands were trembling. Not the subtle shake of adrenaline but a coarse, visible tremor, his fingers jerking independently. A shopkeeper opening his stall eyed him with concern. "*Sababa*?" he asked. You okay?

Ethan nodded, but when he pushed off the wall, the street tilted and he grabbed for the doorframe. His body felt emptied out. Not tired, not weak, but hollowed, as if the vision had burned through his reserves and left nothing but the shell. Every sound was too loud. The scrape of a metal gate being lifted rang in his skull. The smell of coffee from a nearby café hit his stomach and he had to breathe through his mouth to keep from gagging.

A thought he'd been holding at bay since Ahmad's booth broke through: he could have died up there. Not from the fall, though that was real enough. The bump on his head testified to that. But from whatever had happened. Seizure, stroke, cardiac event. He didn't know what had caused the vision or the collapse, and that not-knowing lodged in his chest. He was a father of twins. He had no business climbing to contested holy sites at dawn, no business risking his body and his mind on the chance that God might speak to him through the architecture. The fear arrived late but total: Sera getting a phone call from an IDF officer telling her that her husband had been found unconscious on the Haram al-Sharif. The paperwork. The questions. The boys growing up with a story about their father that was either miraculous or pathetic depending on who was telling it.

At home, Sera was feeding the twins. Ari fussing, Lev patient. She looked up, saw his face.

"Where were you?" 

"Walking. The Old City." 

"You look awful." Her voice sharpened. "What happened? Ethan, you're scaring me."

"I think... I don't know what happened." He dropped into a chair. "I was on the Temple Mount somehow and I had some kind of... vision? Seizure? I don't know."

"The Temple Mount?" She set down Avi's bottle hard. "How did you—" She stopped, stared at him. "You're bleeding."

He touched his nose. Dried blood.

"Jesus, Ethan. What if something had happened? What if you'd had a real seizure up there? What if they'd arrested you?" Her voice rose. "I'm here with two babies and you're off having mystical experiences in the most contested piece of real estate on earth?"

"Sera—"

"No." She rose, Avi still in her arms. "You get to search for meaning. You get to fall apart and rebuild yourself. But I don't get to fall apart, do I? I just get to hold everything together while you figure out who you are." Her eyes were bright with tears she wouldn't let fall. "I need you present. Not transcendent. Present."

She handed him Ezra, who immediately spit up on his shirt. "You're burning up. Eat something. Be a father."

He tried. He ate toast and it tasted of nothing. He drank water and his hands shook so badly it sloshed over the rim. The headache had settled into a steady migraine, light sensitivity turning the apartment's windows into instruments of torture. He closed the blinds, held Lev against his chest, and the baby's warm weight was the only thing that felt fully real. Ten pounds of undeniable physical fact against the strange unreality of everything else.

By noon he was shivering under a blanket on the couch despite the October heat. Sera took his temperature: 38.4. "You're going to a doctor."

"It's just exhaustion."

"You had a seizure on the Temple Mount and you're running a fever. You're going to a doctor or I'm calling an ambulance. Pick."

The doctor at the clinic, a Russian immigrant who'd been an ER surgeon in Moscow, shone a light in his eyes, checked his reflexes, asked about the fall. "Mild concussion, probably. The fever could be stress response, could be infection from the head wound." She prescribed rest and ibuprofen. "No spiritual revelations for at least a week," she added, straight-faced.

He slept fourteen hours that night and woke at 3 AM drenched in sweat, the sheets soaked through. The vision came back in fragments. Not the full symphony but isolated images: the singing tiles, the children playing, the walls rising. Each one brought a wave of nausea. He barely made it to the bathroom before vomiting.

The next three days blurred together. Fever, sleep, the twins' cries pulling him to the surface, Sera's hand on his forehead checking temperature, the ibuprofen wearing off and the migraine returning in waves. His body was paying for whatever his soul had experienced. Or his misfiring neurons. Or the concussion. Or all three at once.

On the fourth day, the fever broke. He woke clear-headed for the first time, weak but present, and found Sera asleep in the chair beside the bed, both boys in their crib, the apartment quiet.

That evening, Ahmad texted: "Daughter's fever broke. Doctors say good sign."

Then: "What you saw this morning. My grandfather used to say this place shows people what they need to see. Good and bad together."

Then: "Better delete these."

Ethan deleted them, but the words remained.

He thought of Samira's students and the girl at the checkpoint; Ahmad's daughter belonged beside his own boys now, not as a counterpoint but as part of what he held when he said this city out loud.

He put the boys to bed, started the *Shema*, then stopped. The words felt suddenly foreign in his mouth, too big, too certain.

"*Shema*..." he began again, then just hummed the melody.

Avi watched him with dark, serious eyes. Ezra had already fallen asleep.

Ethan touched where his head had hit stone. The bump was real.

His phone buzzed. Ahmad: "Deleted everything. But kept the photo."
